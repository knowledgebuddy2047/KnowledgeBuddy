Inflammation and Repair

Key Concepts

  • Definition: Inflammation is a protective response of vascularized tissues to infection or injury, delivering leukocytes and plasma proteins to eliminate harmful agents.
  • Cardinal Signs: Heat (calor), Redness (rubor), Swelling (tumor), Pain (dolor), Loss of function (functio laesa).
  • Types:
    • Acute inflammation: Rapid onset (minutes/hours), short duration, neutrophil predominance, edema.
    • Chronic inflammation: Slow onset, long duration, lymphocytes and macrophages, tissue destruction, fibrosis.
  • Causes: Infections, tissue necrosis, foreign bodies, immune reactions (autoimmune, allergies).
  • Recognition:
    • Toll-like receptors (TLRs) detect PAMPs.
    • Inflammasome detects DAMPs (e.g., uric acid, ATP, cholesterol crystals).
    • Complement system and mannose-binding lectin recognize microbes.
  • Acute Inflammation Components:
    1. Vasodilation → increased blood flow (erythema, heat).
    2. Increased vascular permeability → exudate formation, edema.
    3. Leukocyte emigration → neutrophils migrate, phagocytose, kill microbes.
  • Exudate vs Transudate:
    • Exudate: High protein, cellular debris, due to increased vascular permeability.
    • Transudate: Low protein, few cells, due to hydrostatic/oncotic imbalance.
  • Lymphatic Response: Lymphangitis (inflamed lymphatics), lymphadenitis (inflamed nodes).
  • Clinical Relevance: Excessive inflammation → autoimmune disease, allergies, chronic disorders (RA, atherosclerosis, Alzheimer’s). Defective inflammation → immunodeficiency, infections.

Subjective Questions

  1. Define inflammation and describe its cardinal signs.
  2. Differentiate between acute and chronic inflammation with examples.
  3. Explain the role of Toll-like receptors and inflammasomes in recognizing microbes and damaged cells.
  4. Discuss the vascular changes in acute inflammation and their clinical manifestations.
  5. Compare exudate and transudate with examples.
  6. Describe the role of lymphatic vessels and lymph nodes in inflammation.
  7. Explain how inflammation can be both protective and harmful, with clinical examples.

Objective Questions (MCQs)

1. Which of the following is NOT a cardinal sign of inflammation?

  • A. Heat
  • B. Redness
  • C. Swelling
  • D. Fever

Answer: D. Fever (it is a systemic manifestation, not a cardinal local sign).

2. Acute inflammation is characterized by predominance of:

  • A. Lymphocytes
  • B. Macrophages
  • C. Neutrophils
  • D. Plasma cells

Answer: C. Neutrophils

3. Which receptor family recognizes pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs)?

  • A. G-protein coupled receptors
  • B. Toll-like receptors
  • C. Cytokine receptors
  • D. Integrins

Answer: B. Toll-like receptors

4. Which cytokine is produced by inflammasome activation?

  • A. IL-2
  • B. IL-1
  • C. TNF-alpha
  • D. IFN-gamma

Answer: B. IL-1

5. Exudate differs from transudate in having:

  • A. Low protein content
  • B. High protein content and cellular debris
  • C. Low specific gravity
  • D. No leukocytes

Answer: B. High protein content and cellular debris

6. Vasodilation in acute inflammation is primarily mediated by:

  • A. Histamine
  • B. IL-1
  • C. TNF-alpha
  • D. Complement C3a

Answer: A. Histamine

7. Which of the following is a systemic disease where inflammation plays a major role?

  • A. Atherosclerosis
  • B. Osteoarthritis
  • C. Migraine
  • D. Cataract

Answer: A. Atherosclerosis

Exam Tips

  • Remember the “5 Rs” of inflammation: Recognition, Recruitment, Removal, Regulation, Resolution.
  • Differentiate clearly between acute and chronic inflammation — this is a frequent exam question.
  • Know the mediators: histamine (vasodilation), IL-1/TNF (fever, leukocyte recruitment), complement (opsonization, chemotaxis).
  • Exudate vs transudate is a classic MCQ topic.
  • Clinical correlations (RA, asthma, atherosclerosis, Alzheimer’s) are high-yield for PLAB/USMLE.
