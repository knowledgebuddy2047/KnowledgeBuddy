Cell as a Unit of Health and Disease

Key Concepts

  • Pathology: Study of disease; modern pathology focuses on cellular and molecular abnormalities.
  • Genome: Human genome has ~3.2 billion base pairs, ~20,000 protein-coding genes (1.5%). Noncoding DNA (~98.5%) regulates gene expression.
  • Noncoding DNA Functions: Promoters, enhancers, regulatory RNAs (miRNA, lncRNA), transposons, telomeres, centromeres.
  • DNA Variations: SNPs (single nucleotide polymorphisms) and CNVs (copy number variations) contribute to disease susceptibility and diversity.
  • Histone Organization: DNA wrapped around histones (nucleosomes). Epigenetic regulation via methylation, acetylation, phosphorylation.
  • Noncoding RNAs:
    • miRNA: Short RNAs (~22 nt) regulate translation via RISC complex.
    • lncRNA: >200 nt, modulate gene expression, chromatin modification, scaffolding.
  • Gene Editing: CRISPR-Cas9 allows precise DNA cleavage and editing (NHEJ vs HDR repair).
  • Cellular Housekeeping: Organelles maintain homeostasis:
    • Proteasomes: Degrade tagged proteins.
    • Lysosomes: Digest macromolecules, microbes, organelles.
    • Peroxisomes: Oxidative metabolism, very long-chain fatty acids.

Subjective Questions

  1. Explain the role of noncoding DNA in regulating gene expression and disease causation.
  2. Describe the difference between euchromatin and heterochromatin with examples of their functional significance.
  3. Discuss the mechanisms of epigenetic regulation and their therapeutic implications.
  4. Outline the process of microRNA biogenesis and its role in gene silencing.
  5. What is CRISPR-Cas9? Explain its mechanism and potential applications in medicine.
  6. Compare and contrast the functions of proteasomes, lysosomes, and peroxisomes.

Objective Questions (MCQs)

1. What percentage of the human genome codes for proteins?

  • A. 10%
  • B. 5%
  • C. 1.5%
  • D. 20%

Answer: C. 1.5%

2. Which of the following is NOT a function of noncoding DNA?

  • A. Promoter and enhancer activity
  • B. Coding for enzymes
  • C. Chromatin organization
  • D. Regulatory RNA synthesis

Answer: B. Coding for enzymes

3. Histone acetylation generally leads to:

  • A. DNA condensation and gene silencing
  • B. DNA unwinding and increased transcription
  • C. DNA methylation
  • D. RNA degradation

Answer: B. DNA unwinding and increased transcription

4. CRISPR-Cas9 introduces DNA breaks at specific sites using:

  • A. Histone acetyltransferase
  • B. Guide RNA and Cas9 nuclease
  • C. DNA methyltransferase
  • D. RNA polymerase

Answer: B. Guide RNA and Cas9 nuclease

5. Which organelle is primarily responsible for degradation of phagocytosed microbes?

  • A. Proteasome
  • B. Lysosome
  • C. Peroxisome
  • D. Golgi apparatus

Answer: B. Lysosome

Exam Tips

  • Focus on the regulatory role of noncoding DNA and RNAs—frequently tested in USMLE and PLAB.
  • Understand epigenetic modifications (acetylation, methylation) and their reversibility—important for therapeutic questions.
  • CRISPR-Cas9 is a high-yield topic for modern medicine and genetics.
  • Know organelle functions and differences—classic MBBS and board exam questions.
