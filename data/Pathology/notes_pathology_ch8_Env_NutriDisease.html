Chapter 8: Environmental and Nutritional Diseases

1. Explanation Notes of the Full Chapter

This chapter explores the pathological impact of the environment and nutrition on human health. Environmental diseases encompass disorders caused by exposure to chemical or physical agents in the ambient, workplace, and personal environments [1]. Key factors contributing to environmental degradation include population explosion, industrial effluents, and automobile exhausts [1, 2]. However, personal choices—specifically tobacco use, alcohol consumption, and drug abuse—represent major voluntary pollutants that account for significant global morbidity [2].

The chapter transitions from environmental pollutants (air, chemicals) to iatrogenic drug injuries and non-therapeutic toxins like lead and carbon monoxide [3, 4]. It also covers injuries by physical agents such as thermal burns and ionizing radiation, which causes damage through the radiolysis of water and generation of free radicals [5, 6].

The final segment addresses nutritional diseases, ranging from obesity (overnutrition) to starvation and Protein-Energy Malnutrition (PEM) [7, 8]. It concludes with an exhaustive review of vitamin deficiencies (A, D, E, K, C, and B complex) and the relationship between diet and cancer [9, 10].

2. Core Concepts & High-Quality Explanations

  • Air Pollution: Adverse effects on the lungs depend on exposure duration, total dose, host clearance ability, and particle size (1–5 µm are most dangerous as they reach distal airways) [11, 12].
  • Alcohol Metabolism: Ethanol is primarily metabolized in the liver via three pathways: Alcohol Dehydrogenase (ADH) (major), Microsomal Ethanol Oxidizing System (MEOS) (active at high levels), and catalase [13]. The toxic metabolite acetaldehyde is responsible for most tissue damage [14, 15].
  • Lead Toxicity: Lead inhibits enzymes required for heme synthesis (delta-aminolevulinic acid dehydrogenase and ferroketolase), leading to microcytic hypochromic anemia with characteristic basophilic stippling [16, 17].
  • Protein-Energy Malnutrition (PEM):
    • Kwashiorkor: Protein deficiency with adequate calories; characterized by edema and fatty liver [8, 18].
    • Marasmus: Overall calorie starvation; characterized by muscle wasting and loss of subcutaneous fat [8, 18].
  • Vitamin D Metabolism: Must be activated to 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D (Calcitriol) through hydroxylation in the liver (25-OH) and then the kidney (1,25-OH) to be functionally active [19].

3. Critical Thinking Focus

Assumptions and Common Pitfalls

  • The "Social Drinker" Assumption: Clinicians often underestimate the damage of "social" drinking. The sources note that daily consumption of 40g of ethanol is harmful, and 100g is dangerous [13, 20].
  • Poverty vs. Lifestyle: While undernutrition is often associated with poverty in developing nations, secondary (conditioned) deficiency can occur in wealthy individuals due to malabsorption, alcoholism, or chronic illness [21-23].
  • Vitamin Over-supplementation: Students must recognize that Hypervitaminosis (A and D) can be as toxic as deficiency, leading to increased intracranial pressure (Vit A) or metastatic calcification (Vit D) [24, 25].

Reflective Questions

  1. Why does carbon monoxide cause tissue hypoxia even when the atmospheric oxygen concentration is normal? [26]
  2. How does the "Pickwickian syndrome" illustrate the systemic impact of mechanical obesity on respiratory physiology? [27]

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Fussy Eater

Scenario: A 3-year-old child presents with irritability, abdominal pain ("lead colic"), and developmental delay. Blood film shows microcytic anemia with basophilic stippling.

  • Differential Diagnosis: Iron deficiency anemia, Lead poisoning, Thalassemia.
  • Clinical Reasoning: The combination of abdominal pain and basophilic stippling strongly points to Lead Poisoning, likely from pica (eating lead-based paint flakes) [16, 28].
  • Intervention: Immediate removal from the source and chelation therapy.

Case Study 2: The Chronic Inebriate

Scenario: A 50-year-old male known for chronic alcoholism is brought to the ER confused, with staggering gait (ataxia) and abnormal eye movements (ophthalmoplegia).

  • Problem-Solving: This triad suggests Wernicke’s Encephalopathy (Cerebral Beriberi) due to Thiamine (B1) deficiency [29-31].
  • Decision Under Pressure: Administering intravenous glucose before Thiamine can exhaust remaining B1 stores and worsen the neurological damage. Always give Thiamine first or concurrently with glucose.

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Briefly describe the metabolic pathways of ethanol in the liver.
    • Answer: Ethanol is metabolized via: 1) Cytosolic ADH (major pathway), 2) Microsomal MEOS (P-450 system, active at high levels), and 3) Peroxisomal catalase. All produce toxic acetaldehyde, which is then converted to acetate by ALDH [13, 15].
  2. Compare and contrast Kwashiorkor and Marasmus.
    • Answer: Kwashiorkor is protein-selective deficiency showing edema and enlarged fatty liver. Marasmus is total calorie deprivation with "monkey-like" facies and no edema [18, 32].
  3. List the skeletal changes seen in Rickets.
    • Answer: Craniotabes, Harrison’s sulcus, Rachitic rosary, Pigeon-chest deformity, Bow legs, and enlarged epiphyses [33].
  4. What is the primary mechanism of injury by ionizing radiation?
    • Answer: It acts through the radiolysis of water, generating toxic hydroxyl free radicals that cause somatic cell killing, genetic mutations, or malignant transformation [6].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 70-year-old man living alone presents with bleeding gums, petechiae on his legs, and poor wound healing. He subsists mainly on tea and toast. What is the most likely deficiency?
    • A. Vitamin K
    • B. Vitamin B12
    • C. Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)
    • D. Vitamin A
    • Reasoning: Scurvy (Vitamin C deficiency) presents with hemorrhagic diathesis and impaired collagen synthesis, common in the elderly with poor diets [34-36].
  2. Which of the following findings is most characteristic of chronic Lead poisoning in an adult?
    • A. Osteomalacia
    • B. Wristdrop due to demyelinating neuropathy
    • C. Night blindness
    • D. Hyperkeratosis
    • Reasoning: Adults with lead toxicity often develop peripheral motor neuropathy, typically affecting the radial nerve (wristdrop) [17, 37].
  3. A patient presents with a 3D syndrome: Dermatitis, Diarrhea, and Dementia. Which vitamin is deficient?
    • A. Thiamine (B1)
    • B. Riboflavin (B2)
    • C. Niacin (B3)
    • D. Pyridoxine (B6)
    • Reasoning: This is the classic triad of Pellagra caused by Niacin deficiency [38, 39].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Availability Bias: Doctors in urban areas may forget to screen for agriculture chemical poisoning (e.g., organophosphates) because they don't see it often, leading to missed diagnoses of "sudden death" in visiting farmers [40, 41].
    • Structured Approach: Use occupational history checklists for every patient with unexplained acute symptoms.
  • Diagnostic Overshadowing: Attributing all of an alcoholic patient's symptoms to "just being drunk" may lead to missing a subdural hematoma or hypoglycemia [42, 43].
    • Structured Approach: Always perform a full neurological exam and check blood glucose in altered mental state patients, regardless of the smell of alcohol.

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Environmental pollutants (air/chemicals) cause disease based on dose, duration, and particle size [3, 11].
  • Tobacco is the most preventable cause of death, linked to numerous cancers and COPD [44, 45].
  • Alcohol's toxicity is mediated by acetaldehyde [14].
  • Lead causes anemia and neurological deficits [17].
  • PEM exists as Marasmus (starvation) or Kwashiorkor (protein loss) [46].
  • Vitamins are essential; A (Vision), D (Bones), K (Clotting), C (Collagen), B-complex (Metabolism/Nerves) [47-49].
  • Obesity is a systemic health risk predisposing to Diabetes, HTN, and Atherosclerosis [50].
