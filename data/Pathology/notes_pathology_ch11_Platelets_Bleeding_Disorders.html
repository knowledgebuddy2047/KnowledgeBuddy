Disorders of Platelets, Bleeding Disorders, and Basic Transfusion Medicine

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Thrombopoiesis: The Birth of Platelets

Platelets are formed in the bone marrow via fragmentation of megakaryocyte cytoplasm under the control of thrombopoietin [1]. The maturation stages are: Megakaryoblast → Promegakaryocyte (undergoes endo-reduplication, creating a polyploid cell with up to 32x DNA) → Megakaryocyte (large cell, 30-90 µm) → Discoid Platelets [1-3]. A single megakaryocyte can produce up to 4,000 platelets over a 10-day maturation period [3].

Haemostasis: Primary vs. Secondary

  • Primary Haemostasis: Immediate formation of a platelet plug at the site of injury [4].
    1. Adhesion: Platelets bind to subendothelial collagen via GpIa-IIa and GpIb-IX (stabilized by von Willebrand factor) [4, 5].
    2. Release Reaction: Activated platelets release alpha-granules (vWF, Fibrinogen, PDGF) and dense granules (ADP, Calcium, Serotonin) [5].
    3. Aggregation: Mediated by Fibrinogen bridging adjacent platelets via GpIIb-IIIa receptors [5].
  • Secondary Haemostasis: Involves the plasma coagulation cascade, culminating in a stable fibrin plug [6, 7].

Investigations of Haemostatic Function

  • Bleeding Time (BT): Measures capillary function and platelet plug formation (Normal: 3-8 mins) [8]. Prolonged in thrombocytopenia, vWD, and aspirin use [8, 9].
  • Prothrombin Time (PT): Measures Extrinsic and Common pathways (Factors VII, X, V, II, I). Used for oral anticoagulant monitoring [9, 10].
  • Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT): Measures Intrinsic and Common pathways (Factors XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, V, II, I). Used for heparin monitoring [10, 11].
  • Hess Capillary Resistance Test: Assesses capillary fragility; positive if >20 petechiae appear after cuff inflation [12].

Platelet Disorders

  • Thrombocytopenia (<150,000/µl): Caused by impaired production (marrow failure), accelerated destruction (ITP, TTP), or sequestration (splenomegaly) [13, 14]. Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP) involves IgG autoantibodies against GpIIb-IIIa or GpIb-IX [15].
  • Thrombotic Microangiopathies (TTP/HUS): Characterized by a triad of thrombocytopenia, microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (MAHA), and microthrombi [16]. TTP is triggered by endothelial injury, not primary clotting activation [17].
  • Qualitative Defects: Bernard-Soulier (GpIb deficiency - defective adhesion) and Glanzmann’s (GpIIb-IIIa deficiency - defective aggregation) [18, 19].

Coagulation Disorders

  • Haemophilia A: X-linked recessive deficiency of Factor VIII [20].
  • Haemophilia B (Christmas Disease): X-linked recessive deficiency of Factor IX [21].
  • von Willebrand Disease (vWD): Most common hereditary disorder; autosomal dominant. Features both defective platelet adhesion and reduced Factor VIII activity [22].
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): A complex thrombo-haemorrhagic disorder where widespread activation of coagulation leads to consumption of factors and platelets, followed by secondary fibrinolysis [23-25].

Transfusion Medicine & HDN

  • ABO/Rh Systems: Type O is a universal donor; Rh-D is the most immunogenic Rh antigen [26, 27].
  • Complications: Can be immunologic (ABO incompatibility leading to intravascular hemolysis) or non-immunologic (circulatory overload, infections like HIV/HBV) [28-31].
  • Haemolytic Disease of Newborn (HDN): Occurs when maternal IgG antibodies (usually anti-D) cross the placenta and destroy fetal RBCs [32].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Must-Know Receptors: GpIb = Adhesion; GpIIb-IIIa = Aggregation (Target of Fibrinogen) [4, 5].
  • Platelet Lifespan: 7–10 days; 30% are sequestered in the spleen [3].
  • The 4 Ts of HIT: Thrombocytopenia, Thrombosis, Timing (5-10 days after heparin), and exclusion of oTher causes [33].
  • Lab Distinction: vWD has prolonged BT and APTT [34]. Haemophilia has normal BT but prolonged APTT [34]. ITP has prolonged BT but normal PT/APTT [34].
  • DIC Lab Triad: Low Platelets, Prolonged PT/APTT/TT, and Elevated FDPs/D-dimer [35].
  • Universal Rule: Anti-D antibodies are never naturally occurring; they only arise after exposure (pregnancy/transfusion) [27].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Mistaking TTP for DIC.
Mechanism: In DIC, the primary event is widespread activation of the clotting cascade, leading to consumption of factors (prolonged PT/APTT) [17, 24]. In TTP, the primary event is endothelial injury and platelet microthrombi; the coagulation cascade is not primarily activated, so PT and APTT are typically normal [17, 34].

Reflective Question: Why is ITP characterized by increased megakaryocytes in the bone marrow?
Answer: Because the peripheral destruction of platelets triggers a compensatory response from the marrow to produce more [36, 37].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Post-Viral Petechiae

Scenario: A 6-year-old boy presents with a sudden onset of "pinpoint red spots" (petechiae) on his legs and epistaxis. He recovered from a viral upper respiratory infection two weeks ago. Physical exam is normal except for the petechiae; no splenomegaly. Labs: Hb 13g/dL, WBC 7,000/µl, Platelets 12,000/µl. BT is 12 mins, PT/APTT are normal.

  • Differential: Acute ITP, Drug-induced thrombocytopenia, Leukemia.
  • Step-by-Step Reasoning: The sudden onset following a viral illness in a child, isolated thrombocytopenia, and absence of organomegaly strongly point to Acute ITP [36, 38].
  • Intervention: Observation (90% recover spontaneously) or corticosteroids/IVIG if bleeding is severe [39].

Case Study 2: The "Bleeder"

Scenario: A 12-year-old boy presents with a swollen, painful right knee after a minor fall. He has a history of easy bruising and prolonged bleeding after a tooth extraction. His maternal uncle has similar "joint issues." Labs: Platelets 250,000/µl, PT 12s (Normal), APTT 65s (High).

  • Differential: Haemophilia A or B, vWD (Type 2/3).
  • Reasoning: Hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints) is characteristic of coagulation factor defects, not platelet defects [40]. The X-linked family history and isolated prolonged APTT suggest Haemophilia A or B [20, 21].
  • Priority: Factor VIII or IX assay to confirm the specific deficiency [41].

Case Study 3: Decision Under Pressure (Transfusion)

Scenario: A patient undergoing a massive transfusion for trauma suddenly develops restlessness, flushing, chest pain, and dark urine. The nurse notes the BP is dropping and heart rate is 130 bpm.

  • Action: Immediately stop the transfusion. This is an Immediate Haemolytic Transfusion Reaction, likely due to ABO incompatibility [29].
  • Reasoning: Rapid intravascular hemolysis fixes complement, leading to shock and potential renal failure [29]. Survival depends on immediate cessation and aggressive fluid resuscitation.

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define endo-reduplication and its role in thrombopoiesis.
    Answer: It is the process where nuclear chromatin replicates in multiples of two without cell division, occurring in the promegakaryocyte stage to create polyploidy (up to 32n), allowing the cell to become large enough to produce thousands of platelets [2].
  2. Describe the steps of primary haemostasis at the molecular level.
    Answer: 1. Adhesion: GpIb-IX binds vWF on collagen. 2. Activation: Release of ADP/TXA2. 3. Aggregation: GpIIb-IIIa binds Fibrinogen to cross-link platelets [4, 5].
  3. Contrast the bone marrow findings in ITP versus Aplastic Anaemia.
    Answer: ITP shows increased or normal megakaryocytes (compensatory) [36, 37]. Aplastic Anaemia shows a hypocellular marrow with a marked reduction in all lineages, including megakaryocytes [14].
  4. What are the components of the "triad" seen in TTP and HUS?
    Answer: Thrombocytopenia, Microangiopathic Haemolytic Anaemia (MAHA), and hyaline fibrin microthrombi in the microvasculature [16].
  5. Explain why vWD causes both a platelet defect and a coagulation defect.
    Answer: vWF is essential for platelet adhesion (platelet defect) and also acts as a carrier protein that stabilizes Factor VIII (coagulation defect) [22, 42].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 70-year-old male with chronic back pain has been taking high-dose aspirin for 2 weeks. He now has easy bruising. What is the most likely laboratory finding?
    A) Low platelet count
    B) Prolonged PT
    C) Prolonged BT
    D) Prolonged APTT
    Answer: C. Aspirin inhibits cyclooxygenase, suppressing TXA2 and impairing platelet function (aggregation/release), which prolongs Bleeding Time but doesn't lower the count [43].
  2. A pregnant woman (Rh-negative) is in her first pregnancy with an Rh-positive fetus. Why is the risk of HDN low for this first child?
    A) Anti-D antibodies are IgM and cannot cross the placenta.
    B) Significant feto-maternal hemorrhage usually occurs only at delivery.
    C) The mother's immune system is suppressed during pregnancy.
    D) The fetus does not express D-antigen until birth.
    Answer: B. Sensitization usually occurs during parturition (delivery); thus, the first child is unaffected, but subsequent Rh-positive children are at risk [44].
  3. A patient with Gram-negative sepsis develops oozing from IV sites. Labs show low platelets, low fibrinogen, and prolonged PT/APTT. What is the diagnosis?
    A) ITP
    B) TTP
    C) DIC
    D) Haemophilia A
    Answer: C. Sepsis is a major trigger for DIC, which consumes both platelets and factors, resulting in the "all-prolonged" lab profile [35, 45].
  4. Which receptor deficiency is characteristic of Bernard-Soulier Syndrome?
    A) GpIIb-IIIa
    B) GpIa-IIa
    C) GpIb-IX
    D) P2Y12
    Answer: C. GpIb-IX deficiency prevents proper adhesion to vWF [18].
  5. A "dry tap" on bone marrow aspiration in a patient with massive splenomegaly suggests which cause of thrombocytopenia?
    A) Splenic sequestration
    B) Myelofibrosis
    C) ITP
    D) Vitamin K deficiency
    Answer: B. Myelofibrosis causes marrow infiltration, leading to a "dry tap" and impaired production [14].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias: Search Satisfaction.
Example: A doctor finds a low platelet count and stops looking for other clues, diagnosing ITP. They miss the Schistocytes on the peripheral blood film, which would have pointed to the much more dangerous TTP [34, 35].
Structured Approach: Always follow a systematic "Bleeding Screen": Platelet count → PBF (check for schistocytes) → BT → PT/APTT [7, 46, 47].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Platelets: Formed by megakaryocyte fragmentation; lifespan 7–10 days [3].
  • Primary Haemostasis: GpIb (Adhesion), GpIIb-IIIa (Aggregation via Fibrinogen) [4, 5].
  • ITP: Isolated low platelets; increased marrow megakaryocytes; IgG mediated [15, 36, 37].
  • TTP/HUS: MAHA + Thrombocytopenia + Normal PT/APTT [16, 34].
  • Haemophilia A/B: Prolonged APTT; Factor VIII/IX deficiency; Hemarthrosis [20, 21, 41].
  • vWD: Defective adhesion AND Factor VIII stability; prolonged BT and APTT [22, 34, 48].
  • DIC: Consumption of everything; all screening tests prolonged; high FDPs [25, 35].
  • Transfusion: ABO mismatch causes immediate intravascular hemolysis; Rh mismatch causes extravascular hemolysis [29, 49].
