Disorders of Leucocytes and Lymphoreticular Tissues: Comprehensive Study Guide

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Normal Structure and Reactive Changes

  • Haematopoiesis: Stem cells in the bone marrow differentiate into lymphoid (T, B, NK cells) and myeloid progenitors (granulocytes, monocytes, erythroid cells, and megakaryocytes) [1].
  • Lymph Node Anatomy: Bean-shaped structures with a peripheral cortex (B-cell follicles) and a paracortex (T-cell zone) [2]. Follicular centers are active zones for lymphocyte maturation [3].
  • Reactive Lymphadenitis: Nonspecific response to stimuli. Follicular hyperplasia (B-cell proliferation) is seen in rheumatoid arthritis and early AIDS [4, 5]. Paracortical hyperplasia (T-cell proliferation) occurs in viral infections like infectious mononucleosis [6].
  • Leucocyte Variations:
    • Neutrophilia: Response to acute bacterial infections [7].
    • Neutropenia: Caused by typhoid, viral hepatitis, or marrow aplasia [8].
    • Lymphocytosis: Seen in viral infections (pertussis, IM) and CLL [9].

Infectious Mononucleosis (IM)

A benign, self-limiting disease caused by Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) [10]. It is characterized by the Paul-Bunnell test for heterophile antibodies and the presence of atypical T lymphocytes (Downey cells) in the blood [11, 12].

Lymphohaematopoietic Malignancies

  • Leukaemoid Reaction: Excessive reactive leucocytosis resembling leukaemia but with high NAP/LAP scores and negative Philadelphia chromosome [13-15].
  • Myeloid Neoplasms:
    • Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML): Defined by the Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22) and BCR-ABL fusion gene [16]. Characterized by massive splenomegaly and reduced NAP score [17, 18].
    • Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (AML): Characterized by >20% blasts in the bone marrow (WHO criteria) [19, 20].
    • Polycythaemia Vera (PV): Panmyelosis linked to the JAK2 mutation [21, 22].
  • Lymphoid Neoplasms:
    • Hodgkin’s Disease (HD): Identified by pathognomonic Reed-Sternberg (RS) cells [23]. Subtypes include Nodular Sclerosis (most common) and Lymphocyte Predominance (best prognosis) [24, 25].
    • Non-Hodgkin’s Lymphomas (NHL): A heterogeneous group including B-cell (e.g., Follicular, Burkitt's) and T-cell (e.g., Mycosis Fungoides) malignancies [26, 27].

Plasma Cell Disorders

Characterized by monoclonal immunoglobulin production (M-band). Multiple Myeloma presents with a triad: marrow plasmacytosis (>10%), lytic bone lesions, and M component in serum/urine [28, 29].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • The NAP/LAP Score: Vital for exams. It is high in leukaemoid reactions and low in CML [15, 18, 30].
  • Blast Count Threshold: For AML/ALL, the WHO requires >20% blasts in marrow; FAB previously required >30% [19, 20, 31].
  • Reed-Sternberg Cells: Classic "mirror-image" or "owl-eye" nuclei. CD15+ and CD30+ in classic HD [32, 33].
  • Burkitt’s Lymphoma: "Starry sky" appearance due to macrophages phagocytosing debris; linked to t(8;14) and MYC gene [34].
  • Bence-Jones Proteins: Free light chains excreted in the urine of many myeloma patients [29, 35].
  • Hairy Cell Leukaemia: TRAP-positive cells with "hairy" projections; presents with massive splenomegaly and dry tap [36, 37].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

  • Common Pitfall: Confusing Leukaemoid Reaction with CML.
    Reasoning: Both show massive leucocytosis. Always check the NAP score and Philadelphia chromosome status to distinguish them [30].
  • Assumption: Assuming all splenomegaly in a child is malignant.
    Reasoning: In endemic areas, chronic malaria or leishmaniasis can cause massive splenomegaly [38].
  • Reflective Question: Why is the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase the primary target for CML therapy (Imatinib)? [39].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Pale Child [40]

Scenario: A 4-year-old male child presents with low-grade fever, headache, and epistaxis. Exam shows hepatosplenomegaly. Labs: Hb 6 g/dl, WBC 80,000/µl, Platelets 40,000/µl.

  • Differential Diagnosis: ALL, AML, or severe viral infection.
  • Prioritizing Interventions: Immediate Peripheral Blood Film (PBF) and Bone Marrow Aspiration [31].
  • Reasoning: The age group and high blast count strongly suggest Acute Lymphoblastic Leukaemia (ALL) [41, 42].

Case Study 2: Back Pain in the Elderly [43]

Scenario: A 70-year-old man presents with lower back pain and fatigue. X-ray reveals multiple punched-out lytic lesions in the pelvis and sacrum.

  • Differential Diagnosis: Multiple Myeloma or Metastatic Carcinoma (e.g., Prostate).
  • Reasoning: Lytic lesions combined with age suggest Multiple Myeloma. Confirm with Serum Electrophoresis (M-band) and marrow plasmacytosis (>10%) [29, 44].

Case Study 3: Decision Under Pressure

Scenario: A young patient with infectious mononucleosis presents to the ER with sudden, severe left upper quadrant pain and signs of shock (tachycardia, hypotension).

  • Action: Immediate surgical consultation for Splenic Rupture [45].
  • Reasoning: IM causes splenomegaly, making the spleen vulnerable to even minor trauma [45, 46].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Leukaemoid Reaction and list three causes.
    Answer: A reactive excessive leucocytosis (>25,000/µl) resembling leukaemia. Causes include severe infections (sepsis), intoxications (mercury), or severe haemorrhage [13, 47].
  2. Describe the classic morphology of a Reed-Sternberg cell.
    Answer: A large cell with a bilobed nucleus ("mirror image") and prominent, eosinophilic nucleoli with a clear halo ("owl-eye") [32].
  3. What is the "Starry Sky" pattern and where is it seen?
    Answer: It is a histological pattern seen in Burkitt's lymphoma where clear macrophages containing debris are scattered among dark, rapidly dividing B-cells [34].
  4. Short Essay: Explain the lab diagnosis of Multiple Myeloma.
    Answer: Triad of: 1) Marrow plasmacytosis >10%, 2) Lytic bone lesions on X-ray, 3) Monoclonal M-band on serum electrophoresis or Bence-Jones proteins in urine [29].
  5. List the functional zones of a lymph node.
    Answer: Cortex (B-cell follicles), Paracortex (T-cell zone), and Medulla (plasma cell cords) [2, 3].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 45-year-old male presents with massive splenomegaly. Labs show a WBC of 150,000/µl with all stages of myeloid maturation. The NAP score is 5 (Normal 20-100). What is the likely genetic finding?
    A) JAK2 mutation
    B) t(8;14)
    C) t(9;22)
    D) Trisomy 21
    Correct Answer: C. Low NAP score and mature myeloid leucocytosis identify CML (Philadelphia chromosome) [16, 18].
  2. A child presents with a large mass on his jaw. Histology shows intermediate-sized non-cleaved cells with high mitotic activity. Which virus is most likely associated?
    A) HIV
    B) EBV
    C) HTLV-I
    D) HPV
    Correct Answer: B. Endemic Burkitt’s lymphoma of the jaw is strongly linked to EBV [48].
  3. Which marker is pathognomonic for "hairy cells" in a patient with splenomegaly and pancytopenia?
    A) CD15
    B) Myeloperoxidase
    C) TRAP
    D) PAS
    Correct Answer: C. Tartrate-resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP) is the classic stain for Hairy Cell Leukaemia [37].
  4. A 20-year-old student presents with fever, sore throat, and cervical lymphadenopathy. A peripheral smear shows 15% atypical lymphocytes. Which test confirms the diagnosis?
    A) Bone marrow biopsy
    B) Paul-Bunnell test
    C) NAP score
    D) BCR-ABL PCR
    Correct Answer: B. Atypical lymphocytes and the clinical triad suggest Infectious Mononucleosis, confirmed by the heterophile antibody test [11, 12].
  5. In the Ann Arbor staging for Hodgkin’s Disease, what does the suffix "B" indicate?
    A) Bone marrow involvement
    B) Bilateral nodal involvement
    C) Constitutional symptoms (fever, weight loss)
    D) Benign course
    Correct Answer: C. Suffix B indicates the presence of systemic symptoms [49].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Availability Bias: Diagnosing every patient with "back pain and anaemia" as Multiple Myeloma.
    Correction: In younger patients, consider disc herniation; in older patients, always rule out metastatic prostate or breast cancer [43, 50].
  • Search Satisfaction: Finding a high WBC and stopping.
    Correction: You must perform a DLC and check for "blasts" to differentiate between a reactive infection and leukaemia [31, 51].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • CML: t(9;22), low NAP, massive spleen [16-18].
  • AML: Blasts >20%, Auer rods (in M1, M2, M3) [19, 52, 53].
  • Hodgkin's: RS cells, CD15+/CD30+, Ann Arbor staging [23, 33, 49].
  • Myeloma: M-band, lytic lesions, >10% plasma cells [29].
  • IM: EBV, atypical T-cells, heterophile antibodies [10-12].
  • Spleen: Splenectomy leads to Howell-Jolly bodies and target cells [54].
