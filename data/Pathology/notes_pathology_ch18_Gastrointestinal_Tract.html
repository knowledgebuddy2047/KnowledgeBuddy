Pathology of the Gastrointestinal Tract: Comprehensive Exam Notes

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Oesophagus

  • Normal Structure: A 25 cm muscular tube. The mucosa is non-keratinising stratified squamous epithelium, except for the distal 0.5–1.5 cm "junctional mucosa" which is columnar [1-3].
  • Achalasia: A neuromuscular failure of the cardiac sphincter to relax, leading to "mega-oesophagus" [4]. Primary cause is the loss of intramural neurons [4].
  • Barrett’s Oesophagus: A premalignant condition where squamous epithelium is replaced by metaplastic columnar epithelium (intestinal metaplasia with goblet cells) due to chronic reflux [5, 6]. It increases the risk of adenocarcinoma [7, 8].
  • Carcinoma: 90% are Squamous Cell Carcinomas, most common in the middle third [9, 10]. Adenocarcinoma is linked to Barrett’s and usually affects the lower third [11, 12].

Stomach

  • Gastritis:
    • Acute: Transient mucosal inflammation caused by NSAIDs, alcohol, smoking, or stress [13, 14].
    • Chronic Type A (Autoimmune): Affects the body-fundus; associated with pernicious anaemia and antibodies against parietal cells [15, 16].
    • Chronic Type B (H. pylori): Affects the antrum; hypersecretory and associated with peptic ulcers [17, 18].
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD): Chronic, solitary lesions in areas exposed to acid-pepsin [19]. Duodenal ulcers (4x more common) are linked to hyperacidity and 95-100% H. pylori infection [20-22]. Gastric ulcers are often due to impaired mucosal defense [23].
  • Gastric Carcinoma: Most common in the pyloric antrum [24]. Subtypes include Early Gastric Carcinoma (EGC) (limited to mucosa/submucosa) and Advanced patterns like Linitis Plastica (leather-bottle stomach) [25-27].

Intestines and Appendix

  • Hirschsprung’s Disease: Congenital megacolon caused by the absence of ganglion cells in the distal segment, leading to functional obstruction [28, 29].
  • Malabsorption Syndrome (MAS): Characterised by steatorrhoea [30]. Coeliac sprue is a gluten-sensitive enteropathy showing villous atrophy [31-33].
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD):
    • Crohn’s Disease: Transmural, granulomatous, "skip lesions," and "cobblestone" mucosa [34-36].
    • Ulcerative Colitis: Mucosal/submucosal, continuous involvement starting from the rectum, with "pseudopolyps" [37-39].
  • Acute Appendicitis: Most common acute abdomen [40]. Diagnosis requires neutrophilic infiltration of the muscularis propria [41].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • H. pylori: A spiral bacterium that does not invade mucosa but breaks the protective barrier via urease, protease, and toxins (CagA, VacA) [42, 43]. It is a Class 1 Carcinogen [44, 45].
  • The "Punched Out" Ulcer: Characteristic of benign chronic peptic ulcers, unlike malignant ulcers which have rolled-out, indurated margins [46-48].
  • Virchow’s Sign: Involvement of the left supraclavicular lymph node, often the first sign of gastric carcinoma [49].
  • Dukes’ / Astler-Coller Staging: The most important prognostic factor for colorectal cancer [50, 51]. Stage A (mucosa only) has >90% 5-year survival [52, 53].
  • Carcinoid Syndrome: Occurs when carcinoid tumours (usually midgut) metastasize to the liver, releasing serotonin (5-HT) into systemic circulation, causing flushing and diarrhoea [54, 55].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

  • Differential Pitfall: Confusing Leukaemoid Reaction with Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML). In the GI context, remember that H. pylori can mimic different pathologies; always correlate biopsy with clinical history [42, 56].
  • Reflective Question: Why does Crohn’s Disease lead to fistula formation while Ulcerative Colitis rarely does? Reasoning: Crohn’s is transmural (affects all layers), allowing inflammation to penetrate into adjacent loops or organs [36, 57]. Ulcerative Colitis is confined to the mucosa and submucosa [58, 59].
  • Assumptions in MAS: Do not assume all villous atrophy is Coeliac disease. Tropical Sprue presents similarly but responds to antibiotics rather than a gluten-free diet [60, 61].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Executive’s Dull Pain

Scenario: A 35-year-old male executive presents with dull midepigastric pain for one month. Pain is worse at night and relieved by meals. He takes analgesics for headaches [53, 62].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Duodenal Ulcer, Gastric Ulcer, Gastritis.
  • Prioritizing Interventions: 1. Upper GI Endoscopy. 2. H. pylori testing (Urea breath test or biopsy). 3. Stool for occult blood [63-65].
  • Reasoning: The "pain-food-relief" and night pain patterns are classic for Duodenal Ulcer [66, 67].

Case Study 2: Migrating Abdominal Pain (Decision Under Pressure)

Scenario: A 25-year-old male develops sudden mid-abdominal pain that localises to the Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ) over 24 hours. He has fever (102°F), nausea, and RLQ tenderness [62, 68].

  • Diagnosis: Acute Appendicitis [69].
  • Decision Under Pressure: If the pain suddenly subsides but the patient becomes more tachycardic and the abdomen becomes "board-like," suspect perforation and peritonitis. Immediate surgical intervention is required [70].

Case Study 3: The Newborn’s First Feed

Scenario: A newborn infant experiences regurgitation of every feed, cough, and cyanosis within the first 48 hours of life [71].

  • Diagnosis: Oesophageal Atresia with Tracheo-oesophageal Fistula (TEF) [72].
  • Intervention: Recognize and surgically correct within 48 hours to prevent aspiration pneumonia and electrolyte imbalance [71].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Barrett’s Oesophagus and explain its clinical significance.
    Answer: Metaplasia where squamous epithelium is replaced by columnar epithelium with goblet cells due to chronic GERD [5]. Significance: It is a precursor to adenocarcinoma [5, 6].
  2. Distinguish between Crohn’s Disease and Ulcerative Colitis (Macroscopic).
    Answer: Crohn’s: Segmental (skip areas), "hose-pipe" wall, cobblestone mucosa, thick fat. UC: Continuous from rectum, superficial ulcers, pseudopolyps, "garden-hose" appearance [58, 73].
  3. What is the morphology of an early gastric carcinoma (EGC)?
    Answer: A glandular adenocarcinoma limited to the mucosa and submucosa, regardless of lymph node status [26, 74].
  4. List the causes of Upper GI Bleeding.
    Answer: Oesophageal varices, Mallory-Weiss tears, Peptic ulcers, Gastritis, Gastric cancer [75, 76].
  5. Describe the "Leather Bottle Stomach" (Linitis Plastica).
    Answer: A form of advanced scirrhous gastric carcinoma where the stomach wall is thickened and rigid due to extensive desmoplasia [27, 77].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 60-year-old male with a history of chronic smoking and alcohol use presents with progressive dysphagia, first to solids and now to liquids. Weight loss is significant. What is the most likely histological finding?
    A. Signet-ring cells
    B. Keratin pearls and prickle cells
    C. Intestinal metaplasia
    D. Non-caseating granulomas
    Answer: B. Squamous cell carcinoma (90% of oesophageal cancers) is associated with smoking/alcohol and shows keratinisation [9, 78].
  2. A patient presents with macrocytic anaemia and a biopsy showing subtotal villous atrophy. Symptoms do not improve on a gluten-free diet but improve with tetracycline. Diagnosis?
    A. Coeliac Sprue
    B. Whipple’s Disease
    C. Tropical Sprue
    D. Crohn’s Disease
    Answer: C. Tropical sprue presents with MAS and responds to antibiotics, unlike Coeliac [61, 79].
  3. Which of the following genes is mutated in the earliest stage of the "adenoma-carcinoma sequence" in colon cancer?
    A. p53
    B. K-RAS
    C. APC
    D. DCC
    Answer: C. Loss of the APC gene on chromosome 5q is the initial event [80, 81].
  4. A 45-year-old woman has chronic epigastric pain. Biopsy of the stomach body shows lymphocytic infiltration and loss of parietal cells. Her serum gastrin is high. Diagnosis?
    A. Type B Gastritis
    B. Type A Gastritis
    C. Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome
    D. Peptic Ulcer Disease
    Answer: B. Type A (Autoimmune) affects the body, loses parietal cells (causing achlorhydria), and results in compensatory hypergastrinaemia [15, 16].
  5. The diagnostic histological criterion for Acute Appendicitis is:
    A. Mucosal ulceration
    B. Faecolith in the lumen
    C. Neutrophilic infiltration of the muscularis propria
    D. Lymphoid hyperplasia
    Answer: C. This distinguishes true appendicitis from simple reactive changes [41, 82].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Availability Bias: A clinician may diagnose "GERD" in any patient with heartburn, missing an early Oesophageal Cancer or Achalasia [4, 83].
    Approach: Use "red flags" (weight loss, dysphagia, anaemia) to trigger endoscopic investigation [83, 84].
  • Search Satisfaction: Finding a Peptic Ulcer on endoscopy and stopping.
    Approach: Always biopsy gastric ulcers to rule out malignancy, as 1% of chronic gastric ulcers may transform or be malignant from the start [47, 85].
  • Premature Closure: Diagnosing every RLQ pain in a young male as appendicitis.
    Approach: Consider Meckel’s Diverticulitis or Crohn's Disease which can perfectly mimic appendicitis [34, 35, 86].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Oesophagus: Reflux → Barrett’s (Metaplasia) → Adenocarcinoma [5, 87].
  • H. pylori: Central to Chronic Type B Gastritis, Duodenal Ulcers, and Gastric Cancer [45, 88, 89].
  • PUD: Duodenal ulcers are hypersecretory; Gastric ulcers are defense-deficient [23, 90].
  • IBD: Crohn’s is transmural/skip; UC is mucosal/continuous [73, 91].
  • Colorectal Cancer: Follows the APC → K-RAS → p53 mutation sequence [80, 92].
  • MAS: Look for steatorrhoea and use Schilling test for B12 malabsorption [30, 93].
