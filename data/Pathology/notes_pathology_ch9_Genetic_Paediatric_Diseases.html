Genetic and Paediatric Diseases: Comprehensive Study Guide

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

This chapter explores the pathology of genetic and developmental disorders affecting the fetus and pediatric population. In western countries, these defects account for nearly 50% of infant mortality [1].

Developmental Defects (Teratology)

  • Definitions: Teratology is the study of developmental anomalies during fetal life caused by errors in morphogenesis [2]. Teratogens (chemicals, drugs, or infections) induce malformations [2].
  • Pathogenesis: Outcome depends on individual susceptibility, dose, and the specific stage of pregnancy, with the first trimester being the most critical period [2].
  • Classification:
    • Agenesis: Complete absence of an organ (e.g., renal agenesis) [3].
    • Aplasia: Failure of development despite the presence of a rudiment [3].
    • Hypoplasia: Incomplete development (e.g., microglossia) [3].
    • Atresia: Failure of lumen formation in a hollow viscus (e.g., esophageal atresia) [3].
    • Developmental Dysplasia: Abnormal histogenetic structures, distinct from precancerous cellular dysplasia [4].
    • Ectopia (Heterotopia): Normal tissue in an abnormal location [4].
  • High-Yield Examples: Anencephaly/Spina bifida (failure of neural tube fusion), Thalidomide (phocomelia), Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (mental retardation), and the TORCH complex [5, 6].

Cytogenetic (Karyotypic) Abnormalities

Normal human somatic cells are diploid (2N, 46 chromosomes), while germ cells are haploid (1N, 23 chromosomes) [7].

  • Lyon Hypothesis: One X chromosome in females is inactivated during embryogenesis, forming the Barr body (seen as a "drumstick" appendage in neutrophils) [7, 8].
  • Numerical Abnormalities:
    • Aneuploidy: Not an exact multiple of n (e.g., Trisomy 2n+1). Usually caused by nondisjunction during meiosis or mitosis [9].
    • Down Syndrome: Trisomy 21; most common cause of mental retardation; risk increases with maternal age >35 [10].
    • Klinefelter Syndrome: 47, XXY; characterized by testicular dysgenesis and gynecomastia [11].
    • Turner Syndrome: 45, X0; characterized by webbed neck, short stature, and primary amenorrhea [11].
  • Structural Abnormalities:
    • Philadelphia Chromosome: Balanced reciprocal translocation t(9;22), critical in chronic myeloid leukemia [12].
    • Robertsonian Translocation: Fusion of two acrocentric chromosomes at the centromere [13].
    • Deletion: Loss of material (e.g., Cri du chat syndrome, 5p deletion) [13].

Single-Gene (Mendelian) Disorders

  • Mutations: Permanent DNA changes. Types include point mutations (e.g., Sickle cell), frameshift (e.g., Cystic fibrosis), and trinucleotide repeats [14, 15].
  • Inheritance Patterns:
    • Autosomal Dominant (AD): 50% transmission risk; one mutant allele causes disease (e.g., Marfan syndrome, Neurofibromatosis) [16, 17].
    • Autosomal Recessive (AR): 25% transmission risk; requires two mutant alleles (e.g., Thalassemia, most storage diseases) [16, 18].
    • X-Linked: Mostly recessive; expressed in males (e.g., Hemophilia A, G6PD deficiency) [19, 20].

Storage Diseases (Inborn Errors of Metabolism)

These result from genetic defects in enzymes, leading to intracellular accumulation of metabolites, primarily in lysosomes [21].

  • Glycogen Storage Diseases (GSD):
    • Type I (Von Gierke): Glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency; causes hepatomegaly and hypoglycemia [22, 23].
    • Type II (Pompe): Acid maltase deficiency; the only lysosomal GSD; causes cardiomegaly [22, 24].
    • Type V (McArdle): Muscle phosphorylase deficiency; causes exercise-induced cramps [22, 25].
  • Sphingolipidoses:
    • Gaucher Disease: Glucocerebrosidase deficiency. Features Gaucher cells (crumpled tissue paper cytoplasm) [26, 27].
    • Niemann-Pick Disease: Sphingomyelinase deficiency. Features foamy macrophages and cherry-red spots on the macula [26, 28, 29].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Must-Know Cytogenetics: Trisomy 21 is the most common chromosomal disorder [10]. Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22) is the hallmark of CML [12].
  • Barr Body Rule: The number of Barr bodies is always one less than the number of X chromosomes (e.g., 47, XXY has one Barr body) [7].
  • Gaucher vs. Niemann-Pick: Gaucher cells look like "crumpled paper"; Niemann-Pick cells are "foamy" [27, 29].
  • Paediatric Tumors by Age:
    • < 4 years: Blastomas (Neuroblastoma, Wilms' tumor) [30].
    • 5–9 years: Leukaemias [30].
    • 10–14 years: Soft tissue and bone sarcomas (Osteosarcoma) [30, 31].
  • Hamartoma vs. Choristoma: Hamartoma is a disorganized mass of indigenous tissue; Choristoma is normal tissue in a foreign site (Heterotopia) [32, 33].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

  • Pitfall: Confusing Developmental Dysplasia with Precancerous Dysplasia. Developmental dysplasia refers to structural defects in tissues (e.g., renal dysplasia), whereas precancerous dysplasia is a cellular alteration in response to chronic irritation [4].
  • Assumption: Assuming all storage diseases are lysosomal. Correction: While most are, only Type II GSD (Pompe) is a lysosomal storage disease among the glycogenoses [26, 34].
  • Reflective Question: Why does the Lyon hypothesis mean that females are effectively "mosaics" for their X-linked genes? [7]

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Enlarged Abdomen

Scenario: A 6-month-old infant presents with failure to thrive and a massively enlarged liver. Labs show severe hypoglycemia and ketosis. Biopsy reveals hepatocytes filled with glycogen and a lack of glucose-6-phosphatase [23].

  • Step 1 (Differential): Von Gierke disease (Type I GSD), Pompe disease (Type II GSD), or Gaucher disease.
  • Step 2 (Prioritizing): Hypoglycemia management is the immediate priority to prevent brain injury.
  • Step 3 (Reasoning): The absence of glucose-6-phosphatase specifically points to Von Gierke disease [23].

Case Study 2: Primary Amenorrhea

Scenario: A 16-year-old girl is brought to the clinic because she has not started her periods. On exam, she has a short stature, a webbed neck, and a low posterior hairline [8, 11].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Turner Syndrome, Constitutional growth delay, or Mullerian agenesis.
  • Diagnosis: Turner Syndrome (45, X0). The clinical triad of short stature, webbed neck, and primary amenorrhea is classic [11].

Case Study 3: Decision Under Pressure (Neonatal Distress)

Scenario: A newborn baby presents with excessive salivation and immediate coughing/choking upon the first feeding attempt. A gastric tube cannot be passed into the stomach.

  • Immediate Action: Stop all oral intake to prevent aspiration pneumonia.
  • Reasoning: This is a surgical emergency likely representing Esophageal Atresia [3]. Under pressure, the clinician must prioritize airway protection over diagnostic imaging.

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Teratology and name three categories of teratogens.
    Answer: Teratology is the study of developmental anomalies [2]. Categories: Drugs (Thalidomide), Chemicals (Alcohol), and Infections (TORCH) [5, 6].
  2. Explain the difference between Agenesis and Aplasia.
    Answer: Agenesis is the complete absence of an organ; Aplasia is the absence of development despite a rudiment (anlage) being present [3].
  3. What is the Philadelphia chromosome and what is its clinical significance?
    Answer: It is a balanced reciprocal translocation t(9;22) involving the long arms of chromosomes 9 and 22. It is found in most cases of Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia [12].
  4. Short Essay: Describe the clinical and microscopic features of Gaucher disease.
    Answer: Clinical: Splenomegaly, bone pain, and pathologic fractures [35]. Microscopic: Gaucher cells in the spleen and bone marrow, which have abundant, fibrillar cytoplasm resembling "crumpled tissue paper" [27].
  5. Which of the following is an example of an acrocentric chromosome?
    A) 1 B) 16 C) 21 D) 20
    Answer: C. Chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, 22, and Y are acrocentric [36].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 38-year-old pregnant woman is concerned about her risk of having a child with Down Syndrome. Which mechanism is most likely responsible for this condition?
    A) Balanced translocation
    B) Meiotic nondisjunction
    C) Ring chromosome formation
    D) Anaphase lag
    Correct Answer: B. Trisomy 21 in 95% of cases is due to meiotic nondisjunction [10].
  2. A 2-year-old child presents with hepatosplenomegaly and progressive neurological deterioration. Fundoscopic exam reveals a cherry-red spot on the macula. What is the most likely enzyme deficiency?
    A) Glucose-6-phosphatase
    B) Acid maltase
    C) Sphingomyelinase
    D) Glucocerebrosidase
    Correct Answer: C. This describes Niemann-Pick disease Type A [28].
  3. An infant is born with limb-reduction anomalies (phocomelia). Which maternal exposure is most likely linked to this?
    A) Phenytoin
    B) Ethanol
    C) Thalidomide
    D) Cytomegalovirus
    Correct Answer: C. Thalidomide is the classic teratogen for phocomelia [5].
  4. A genetic study of a phenotypically normal male with infertility reveals a 47, XXY karyotype. What is the diagnosis?
    A) Turner Syndrome
    B) Down Syndrome
    C) Klinefelter Syndrome
    D) Edwards Syndrome
    Correct Answer: C. 47, XXY is the classic karyotype for Klinefelter Syndrome [11].
  5. A biopsy of a mass in a child’s stomach reveals normal pancreatic tissue. This is an example of:
    A) Hamartoma
    B) Choristoma
    C) Aplasia
    D) Dysplasia
    Correct Answer: B. Choristoma (Heterotopia) is normal tissue in an abnormal location [33].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Bias Example (Search Satisfaction): A clinician sees a child with hepatomegaly and immediately suspects Gaucher disease because they recently read about it (Availability Bias). They fail to check for hypoglycemia, missing a diagnosis of Von Gierke disease.
  • Approach to avoid bias: Use a structured differential diagnosis based on biochemical pathways. Always correlate clinical findings (like hypoglycemia) with morphologic ones (hepatomegaly) [23, 34].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Developmental defects occur primarily in the first trimester due to teratogens [2].
  • Aneuploidy (e.g., Down, Turner, Klinefelter) usually results from nondisjunction [9, 10].
  • Mendelian disorders follow AD, AR, or X-linked patterns [16, 19].
  • Storage diseases result from enzyme defects leading to metabolic accumulation [21].
  • Gaucher disease is the most common storage disease; look for "crumpled paper" macrophages [27, 35].
  • Paediatric malignancies are often "blastomas" in early childhood and change to leukaemias and sarcomas as the child ages [30, 31].
