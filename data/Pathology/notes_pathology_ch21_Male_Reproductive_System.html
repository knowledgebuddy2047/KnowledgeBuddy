1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

The Testis and Epididymis

The testis consists of seminiferous tubules lined by germ cells (producing spermatozoa) and Sertoli cells, which provide support and produce androgens [1, 2]. Between these tubules lies the stroma containing Leydig cells, characterized by Reinke’s crystals and acting as the primary source of testosterone [2].

Developmental Disorders: Cryptorchidism

  • Definition: Failure of the testicle to descend into the scrotum; most commonly arrested in the inguinal ring (70%) [3].
  • Pathology: Atrophy begins by age 2, showing loss of germ cells, thickened basement membranes, and prominent Leydig cells [4].
  • Complications: Sterility (if bilateral) and a 30–50 times increased risk of malignancy, primarily seminoma and embryonal carcinoma [5, 6].

Inflammations

Orchitis and epididymitis are often caused by retrograde infection from the urinary tract [7]. In men under 35, N. gonorrhoeae and C. trachomatis are the primary pathogens, while older men typically present with E. coli infections [7]. Tuberculous epididymo-orchitis begins in the epididymis and is characterized by caseating granulomas and acid-fast bacilli [8, 9].

Testicular Tumours

Testicular tumours are mostly Germ Cell Tumours (GCTs) (95%), divided into Seminomatous and Non-Seminomatous (NSGCT) groups [10].

  • Classic Seminoma: Most common (45%), occurs in the 4th decade, is highly radiosensitive, and shows uniform cells with clear cytoplasm and lymphocytic infiltration [11-13].
  • Yolk Sac Tumour: Most common in infants/children under 4; characterized by Schiller-Duval bodies and elevated AFP [14, 15].
  • Choriocarcinoma: Highly malignant, produces hCG, and is composed of syncytiotrophoblasts and cytotrophoblasts [16, 17].
  • Teratoma: Tissues from all three germ layers; in adults, all are considered malignant [18, 19].

The Prostate

The prostate has two critical functional zones: the inner periurethral part (sensitive to estrogen/androgen, site of BNH) and the outer subcapsular part (sensitive to androgen, site of carcinoma) [20].

  • Benign Nodular Hyperplasia (BNH): A common aging process (75–80% over age 80) causing urinary obstruction [21]. Histology shows hyperplasia of glandular and fibromuscular elements [22].
  • Prostatic Carcinoma: The second most common cancer in males [23]. Most are adenocarcinomas arising in the peripheral zone (posterior lobe) [24, 25]. Diagnosis relies on digital rectal examination (DRE), PSA levels, and core needle biopsy [26, 27].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Leydig Cells: Contain Reinke’s crystals; produce testosterone [2].
  • Cryptorchidism Rule: Surgical correction (orchiopexy) must be done by age 2 to minimize infertility and malignancy risk [5].
  • i(12p): An isochromosome of the short arm of chromosome 12 is found in >90% of GCTs [28].
  • Tumour Marker "Double-Check": AFP is elevated in Yolk Sac components; hCG is elevated in Choriocarcinoma and some seminomas (10%) [11, 29, 30].
  • Schiller-Duval Bodies: Pathognomonic for Yolk Sac Tumours [15].
  • PSA Interpretation: Normal is 0–4 ng/ml; values above 10 ng/ml are highly diagnostic of carcinoma [31].
  • Gleason Grading: Based on glandular differentiation and growth patterns; scores are the sum of the primary (predominant) and secondary patterns (range 2–10) [32].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Assuming all testicular masses in children are malignant. While GCTs are the most common, always consider Yolk Sac Tumour as the most frequent infant malignancy and Teratomas as often benign in children but malignant in adults [15, 18, 19].

Diagnostic Bias: Mistaking Granulomatous Prostatitis for carcinoma. Both can feel "firm to hard" on DRE; however, biopsy will reveal macrophages and multinucleate giant cells in prostatitis rather than malignant acini [33].

Reflective Question: Why does BNH cause urinary symptoms earlier than prostatic carcinoma?
Reasoning: BNH occurs in the inner periurethral zone, directly compressing the urethral lumen, whereas carcinoma typically begins in the outer peripheral zone and may remain asymptomatic until it spreads or becomes quite large [20, 34].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Firm Testicular Mass

Scenario: A 25-year-old male presents with a painless, 5 cm firm enlargement of the right testis for one month. There is no history of trauma. The mass does not transilluminate [27].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Seminoma, Embryonal Carcinoma, Teratocarcinoma, Granulomatous Orchitis [10, 11, 35, 36].
  • Prioritizing Interventions: 1. Ultrasound of the scrotum. 2. Serum tumour markers (AFP, hCG, LDH). 3. Radical inguinal orchiectomy for histopathologic confirmation [29, 37].
  • Reasoning: In a young adult, a firm, non-tender testicular mass that doesn't transilluminate is a Germ Cell Tumour until proven otherwise [27].

Case Study 2: Sudden Scrotal Pain (Decision Under Pressure)

Scenario: A 15-year-old boy presents to the ER with sudden, agonizing pain in the left scrotum that began after heavy lifting. The testis is swollen, extremely tender, and elevated.

  • Action: Immediate surgical exploration.
  • Reasoning: This is Torsion of the Testis, a vascular emergency caused by the cessation of venous drainage and arterial supply [38, 39]. Delay beyond 6 hours leads to hemorrhagic infarction and coagulative necrosis, resulting in loss of the testis [38].

Case Study 3: The Obstructed Stream

Scenario: A 75-year-old male complains of nocturia, increased frequency, and a "weak stream." DRE reveals a smooth, firm, nodular, and enlarged prostate [40].

  • Differential: BNH, Prostatic Carcinoma [21, 23].
  • Reasoning: The smooth, nodular enlargement in the central area suggests BNH, but PSA determination is essential to rule out concurrent latent or incidental carcinoma [26, 41].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Cryptorchidism and list its major complications.
    Answer: Arrest of testicular descent along its normal path [3]. Complications include sterility/infertility and a significantly high risk of testicular malignancy [5, 6].
  2. Short Answer: Describe the histological features of a Classic Seminoma.
    Answer: Lobules of uniform cells with clear cytoplasm (containing glycogen), large hyperchromatic nuclei with prominent nucleoli, separated by delicate fibrous stroma with lymphocytic infiltration [12, 13].
  3. Distinguish between BNH and Prostatic Carcinoma based on anatomical location.
    Answer: BNH arises in the inner periurethral part and compresses the urethra; Carcinoma usually arises in the outer subcapsular/peripheral part (posterior lobe) and often does not compress the urethra initially [20, 24].
  4. What is the "Gleason Score" and how is it calculated?
    Answer: A grading system for prostatic adenocarcinoma based on glandular differentiation. It is the sum of the primary (most common) and secondary (second most common) histological patterns, each graded 1–5 [32].
  5. True or False: Seminomas are generally radioresistant.
    Answer: False. Seminomas are highly radiosensitive, whereas non-seminomatous GCTs are relatively radioresistant [37, 42].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 3-year-old boy has a testicular mass. Histology shows flattened cuboidal cells forming structures resembling the endodermal sinuses of a rat placenta. Which marker is most likely elevated?
    A. hCG
    B. AFP
    C. PSA
    D. Testosterone
    Correct Answer: B. The description fits Schiller-Duval bodies in a Yolk Sac Tumour, which produces AFP [15].
  2. A 70-year-old male with back pain has a hard, nodular prostate on DRE and osteoblastic lesions in the lumbar spine. What is the most specific investigation to confirm the prostate as the primary site?
    A. Serum Acid Phosphatase
    B. Bone Scan
    C. PSA Immunohistochemistry of a biopsy
    D. Transrectal Ultrasound
    Correct Answer: C. PSA immunohistochemistry is useful in deciding if a metastasis originated from the prostate [26, 31].
  3. Which genetic abnormality is nearly constant in all testicular germ cell tumours?
    A. p53 mutation
    B. BRCA2 mutation
    C. i(12p) isochromosome
    D. Trisomy 13
    Correct Answer: C. Found in more than 90% of testicular GCTs [28].
  4. A 55-year-old male is diagnosed with BNH. He is at increased risk for which of the following?
    A. Prostatic Adenocarcinoma
    B. Hydronephrosis
    C. Choriocarcinoma
    D. Torsion of the Testis
    Correct Answer: B. Chronic urinary obstruction from BNH leads to secondary effects like hydroureter and hydronephrosis [40]. Carcinoma is not directly caused by BNH [43].
  5. A biopsy of a small, yellowish parotid-like nodule in the testis of a 30-year-old reveals sheets of cells with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm and Reinke’s crystals. What is the likely diagnosis?
    A. Sertoli Cell Tumour
    B. Seminoma
    C. Leydig Cell Tumour
    D. Gonadoblastoma
    Correct Answer: C. Reinke’s crystals are pathognomonic for Leydig cells [2, 44].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias (Search Satisfaction): Finding BNH on a prostatectomy specimen and failing to look for Incidental Carcinoma.
Approach: Pathologists must thoroughly sample tissue, as 15–20% of prostatectomies for BNH reveal incidental malignant foci [41].

Diagnostic Bias (Availability Bias): Attributing a testicular mass to Trauma just because the patient mentions a recent injury.
Approach: Trauma often only brings a pre-existing tumour to the patient's attention; always perform imaging and markers for any firm testicular mass regardless of trauma history [45].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Cryptorchidism: Major risk factor for Seminoma; higher risk if the testis is abdominal [6, 46].
  • Testicular Tumours: 95% Germ Cell; Seminoma (Radiosensitive, PAS+) vs NSGCT (Radioresistant, aggressive) [10, 12, 37].
  • Markers: AFP = Yolk Sac; hCG = Choriocarcinoma; PSA = Prostate Cancer [30, 31].
  • Prostate Zones: Inner (BNH) vs. Outer (Cancer). Most cancers are in the posterior lobe [20, 24].
  • Metastasis: Prostate cancer famously spreads to bones (osteoblastic) via the pampiniform/venous plexus [47].
  • Emergency: Testicular torsion requires surgery within 6 hours to prevent infarction [38].
