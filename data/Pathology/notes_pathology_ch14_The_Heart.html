1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Anatomy and Normal Structure

The heart is a muscular pump weighing 300-350g in males and 250-300g in females [1]. Wall thickness is critical for assessing hypertrophy: RV is 0.3-0.5 cm and LV is 1.3-1.5 cm [1, 2]. The myocardium consists of branching fibers connected by intercalated discs, which act as tight junctions for rapid action potential transport [3].

Heart Failure (HF)

  • Definition: The heart is unable to maintain adequate circulation for metabolic needs [4].
  • Left-Sided Failure: Caused by systemic hypertension, IHD, or valvular disease [5]. Leads to pulmonary congestion (dyspnoea, orthopnoea) and decreased renal perfusion [5, 6].
  • Right-Sided Failure: Most commonly follows left-sided failure or intrinsic lung disease (Cor Pulmonale) [6, 7]. Manifests as systemic venous congestion (edema, passive congestion of liver/spleen, ascites) [6].

Congenital Heart Disease (CHD)

Categorized into shunts and obstructions [8].

  • Left-to-Right Shunts (Acyanotic/Late Cyanotic): Includes VSD (most common, 30%), ASD, and PDA [9-12]. These cause volume overload on the right heart, potentially leading to pulmonary hypertension [13].
  • Right-to-Left Shunts (Cyanotic): Includes Tetralogy of Fallot (most common cyanotic defect: VSD, overriding aorta, pulmonary stenosis, RVH) and Transposition of Great Arteries [14-16].
  • Obstructions: Coarctation of Aorta (Adult/postductal type shows hypertension in upper limbs and weak pulses in lower limbs) [17, 18].

Ischaemic Heart Disease (IHD)

IHD results from an imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand, with atherosclerosis accounting for >90% of cases [19, 20]. Acute Coronary Syndromes include MI, unstable angina, and sudden cardiac death [21, 22].

  • Angina Pectoris: Transient ischaemia. Stable (exertional), Prinzmetal (vasospasm at rest), and Unstable (crescendo pain, high risk of MI) [23-26].
  • Myocardial Infarction (MI): Usually caused by acute plaque rupture and thrombosis [27, 28].

Rheumatic Fever (RF) and Valvular Disease

RF is a post-streptococcal (Group A) autoimmune response [29, 30]. Pancarditis is characterized by Aschoff bodies (pathognomonic), which contain Anitschkow cells (caterpillar/owl-eye nuclei) [31-34]. The Mitral valve is the most frequent site of chronic deformity (stenosis/insufficiency) [34, 35].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • MI Morphology Timeline:
    • 0–6 hours: No change (EM shows glycogen depletion) [36, 37].
    • 12–24 hours: Coagulative necrosis, early neutrophilic infiltrate [38, 39].
    • 3–7 days: Yellow softening (maximal risk of cardiac rupture) [38-40].
    • 8 weeks+: Grey-white fibrous scar [41, 42].
  • Serum Markers for MI:
    • Myoglobin: First to rise, lacks specificity [43].
    • CK-MB: Rises in 4-6 hours, disappears by 48 hours (useful for detecting re-infarction) [44].
    • Cardiac Troponins (cTnT/cTnI): Highly specific; rise in 4-6 hours, stay elevated for 7-14 days [45].
  • Endocarditis Vegetations:
    • RHD: Small, firm, along line of closure [46, 47].
    • Infective (IE): Large, friable, destructive [48, 49].
    • Libman-Sacks (SLE): Small, on both surfaces of valves [47, 50].
    • Marantic (NBTE): Small, sterile, associated with cancer/wasting [47, 51, 52].
  • Cardiomyopathy Types: Dilated (most common, globular heart), Hypertrophic (asymmetrical septal hypertrophy, risk of sudden death in athletes), Restrictive (amyloidosis) [53-56].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

  • Mechanism Check: Why does Mitral Stenosis lead to Right Heart Failure?
    Reasoning: The narrowed valve increases left atrial pressure, which is transmitted backward to the pulmonary veins and capillaries, causing pulmonary hypertension, which eventually forces the RV to work against higher pressure (pressure overload) [57].
  • Pitfall: Assuming ST-segment elevation always means MI.
    Fact: Prinzmetal angina also shows ST elevation due to transmural ischemia, but it is transient and lacks enzyme elevation [25].
  • Reflective Question: Why is the Left Ventricle more susceptible to infarction than the Right Ventricle?
    Consider: Relative wall thickness, metabolic requirements, and the presence of thebesian vessels in the RV [58].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Crushing Chest Pain

Scenario: A 55-year-old male with hypertension presents with 4 hours of severe, crushing substernal pain radiating to the left arm. He is sweating and apprehensive. BP is 90/60 mmHg.

  • Differential Diagnosis: Acute MI, Unstable Angina, Aortic Dissection, Pulmonary Embolism [26, 59, 60].
  • Prioritizing Interventions: 1. Immediate ECG (look for ST elevation). 2. Draw Cardiac Troponins. 3. Thrombolytics or PTCA [60-62].
  • Reasoning: The duration and radiation suggest STEMI. BP of 90/60 indicates potential cardiogenic shock [63, 64].

Case Study 2: The Fleeting Joint Pains

Scenario: A 12-year-old girl presents with fever and painful, swollen knees that "moved" from her ankles to her knees over 3 days. She had a severe sore throat 3 weeks ago. A new murmur is heard.

  • Differential: Rheumatic Fever (RF), Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis, Septic Arthritis [65, 66].
  • Applying Jones Criteria: Polyarthritis (Major), Fever (Minor), Recent throat infection (Supportive) = High probability of Acute RF [66, 67].

Case Study 3: Decision Under Pressure (The "Silent" Killer)

Scenario: A patient 4 days post-MI suddenly collapses with no pulse. Neck veins are distended. Heart sounds are muffled.

  • Diagnosis: Cardiac Rupture leading to Cardiac Tamponade [40, 68].
  • Action: This is a surgical emergency. The infarcted wall has softened (yellow softening phase), allowing blood to flood the pericardial sac, preventing diastolic filling [40, 69].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Short Answer: Define and describe the "Bread and Butter" appearance of the heart.
    Answer: This is the gross appearance of fibrinous pericarditis. The pericardial surfaces are shaggy and covered with thick fibrin, resembling two buttered slices of bread pulled apart [70, 71].
  2. Essay: Describe the morphological stages of Myocardial Infarction from 12 hours to 8 weeks.
    Answer: (See High-Yield section above for sequential changes in light microscopy and gross appearance) [36, 38, 39, 41, 42, 72-74].
  3. MCQ: Which cell is pathognomonic for Rheumatic Heart Disease?
    A. Reed-Sternberg Cell
    B. Anitschkow Cell
    C. Aschoff Cell
    D. Foam Cell
    Answer: C (Aschoff Cell). While Anitschkow cells are characteristic, the multinucleated Aschoff cell is pathognomonic [33, 75].
  4. Definition: What is Cor Pulmonale?
    Answer: Right ventricular hypertrophy or dilatation resulting from disorders of the lungs (e.g., emphysema, TB) causing pulmonary hypertension [7, 76].
  5. Short Answer: List the four components of Tetralogy of Fallot.
    Answer: 1. VSD, 2. Overriding Aorta, 3. Pulmonary Stenosis, 4. Right Ventricular Hypertrophy [14].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 65-year-old male with a history of colon cancer presents with new-onset heart murmurs. Autopsy reveals small, sterile, brownish vegetations along the line of closure of the mitral valve. The valve itself shows no inflammation. What is the diagnosis?
    A. Rheumatic Endocarditis
    B. Acute Bacterial Endocarditis
    C. Non-bacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE)
    D. Libman-Sacks Endocarditis
    Correct: C. NBTE (Marantic endocarditis) is sterile and associated with advanced cancer or wasting diseases [51, 52].
  2. A patient presents with mid-systolic click and a systolic murmur. He is 6'4" with long fingers and joint hypermobility. What is the likely underlying valvular pathology?
    A. Mitral Stenosis
    B. Mitral Valve Prolapse (MVP)
    C. Aortic Regurgitation
    D. Tricuspid Atresia
    Correct: B. MVP (Floppy valve syndrome) is strongly associated with Marfan's syndrome [77, 78].
  3. A 45-year-old male presents with massive cardiac enlargement (1000g), high pulse pressure, and a "water hammer" pulse. Which valve is likely affected?
    A. Mitral Valve
    B. Tricuspid Valve
    C. Pulmonary Valve
    D. Aortic Valve
    Correct: D. These are classic signs of Aortic Insufficiency (Regurgitation) [79, 80].
  4. What is the most common primary tumor of the heart, usually found in the left atrium?
    A. Rhabdomyoma
    B. Myxoma
    C. Angiosarcoma
    D. Lipoma
    Correct: B. Myxoma is the most common primary cardiac tumor [81, 82].
  5. A patient with Gram-negative sepsis develops small, painful nodules on his fingertips. What are these called?
    A. Janeway Spots
    B. Osler's Nodes
    C. Aschoff Bodies
    D. Milk Spots
    Correct: B. Osler's nodes are painful/tender and seen in SABE. Janeway spots are painless and seen in ABE [83].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Availability Bias: Assuming every young patient with chest pain has pericarditis or anxiety.
    Structured Approach: Remember that 5% of heart attacks occur in people under 40, especially those with risk factors like familial hypercholesterolaemia or smoking [84].
  • Search Satisfaction: Stopping after finding one Jones criterion (e.g., fever).
    Structured Approach: Always complete the Jones criteria checklist (2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor) to ensure a correct diagnosis of RF [67, 85].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Weight/Thickness: LV (1.3-1.5cm) is ~3x thicker than RV (0.3-0.5cm) [1, 2].
  • IHD: Mostly due to atherosclerosis; Troponins are the gold standard diagnostic marker [20, 45].
  • RF: Autoimmune post-strep pharyngitis; Aschoff bodies are the histological hallmark [30, 32].
  • CHD: VSD is the most common overall; Tetralogy of Fallot is the most common cyanotic type [9, 14].
  • Valves: The Mitral valve is the "workhorse" and most commonly diseased [34, 35].
  • Tamponade: Sudden accumulation of even 250ml of fluid can be fatal [68, 69].
