Pathology of the Nervous System: Harsh Mohan Chapter 28

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Normal Histology and Cellular Response

The nervous system consists of neuroectodermal (neurons and neuroglia) and mesodermal (microglia, meninges, vessels) tissues [1, 2]. Neurons are post-mitotic specialized cells; brain damage involving neurons is irreversible [2]. Neuroglia include astrocytes (support/gliosis), oligodendrocytes (myelin formation), and ependymal cells (lining ventricles/CSF formation) [3-5]. Microglia act as the CNS macrophage system, assuming the form of gitter cells when phagocytosing necrotic tissue, a process called neuronophagia [6].

Developmental Anomalies and Hydrocephalus

Spina bifida involves incomplete closure of vertebral arches, ranging from occulta (bone only) to cystica (meningocele or meningomyelocele) [7, 8]. Syringomyelia is a tubular cavity (syrinx) in the spinal cord, often causing loss of pain and temperature sensation [9]. Hydrocephalus is increased CSF volume with ventricular dilatation, categorized as obstructive (non-communicating, e.g., aqueductal stenosis) or non-obstructive (communicating, e.g., overproduction or deficient reabsorption) [10-13].

Infections of the CNS

Acute pyogenic meningitis presents with purulent CSF, high pressure, neutrophilic leucocytosis, and decreased sugar [14-16]. Common pathogens vary by age: E. coli (neonates), H. influenzae (children), N. meningitidis (young adults), and S. pneumoniae (trauma/elderly) [14, 17]. Chronic meningitis (Tuberculous or Cryptococcal) features mononuclear leucocytosis and significantly raised protein [18-20]. Viral encephalitis includes HIV encephalopathy (AIDS-dementia complex) and slow virus diseases like Progressive Multifocal Leucoencephalopathy (PML) which infects oligodendrocytes [21, 22]. Spongiform Encephalopathy (CJD) is caused by prion proteins, showing characteristic neuronal vacuoles [23, 24].

Cerebrovascular and Traumatic Diseases

Ischemic brain damage results from global hypoxia (hypoperfusion) or focal infarction (vascular occlusion) [25]. Infarcts may be anaemic (thrombotic) or haemorrhagic (embolic/venous), eventually forming cystic infarcts through reactive astrocytosis [26-28]. Intracranial hemorrhage includes intracerebral (hypertensive origin, often basal ganglia) and subarachnoid (ruptured berry aneurysm near the Circle of Willis) [29-31]. Trauma consequences include epidural haematoma (arterial, often middle meningeal artery), subdural haematoma (venous, rupture of bridging veins), and concussions [32-35].

Miscellaneous and Neoplastic Diseases

Multiple Sclerosis (MS) is a demyelinating disease marked by scattered plaques in the white matter [36, 37]. Degenerative diseases like Alzheimer’s feature senile plaques and neurofibrillary tangles, while Parkinson’s features Lewy bodies in the substantia nigra [38-41]. CNS Tumours are predominantly gliomas (astrocytomas/GBM) [42, 43]. Meningiomas are usually benign, arise from arachnoid cap cells, and show whorled patterns and psammoma bodies [44-46]. Schwannomas are encapsulated tumors showing Antoni A/B patterns and Verocay bodies [47].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Differential Diagnosis Pitfall: Confusing Pemphigus (Not in these neuro sources) with Bullous Pemphigoid. In neuro, a common pitfall is confusing Epidural and Subdural haematomas. Epidural is arterial, fast-expanding, and compresses gyri; Subdural is venous and may present slowly or chronically [33, 55].

Reasoning under Uncertainty: In cases of Hydrocephalus, it is critical to determine if it is communicating or non-communicating [12, 13]. If CSF is absent from the subarachnoid space but ventricles are dilated, the obstruction is internal (non-communicating) [12].

Reflective Question: Why are neurons described as post-mitotic and their damage irreversible, whereas astrocytes can undergo hyperplasia and hypertrophy (gliosis)? [2, 56].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Sudden Hemiplegia

Scenario: A 62-year-old hypertensive and diabetic male smoker experiences sudden weakness, numbness on the right side, and a severe headache [57]. He is unable to stand upright.

  • Differential Diagnosis: Intracerebral haemorrhage, Cerebral infarction (Ischemic stroke) [57].
  • Interventions: 1. Immediate CT/MRI to distinguish between bleed and infarct. 2. Control BP. 3. Assess for thrombolysis if ischemic [57].
  • Reasoning: His long history of hypertension and diabetes increases the risk of intracerebral haemorrhage from microaneurysm rupture or infarction from atherosclerosis [26, 30, 57].

Case Study 2: The Stiff Neck

Scenario: An 18-year-old college student presents with high fever, severe headache, vomiting, and neck stiffness on forward bending [15].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Acute pyogenic meningitis, Viral meningitis [14, 16].
  • Step-by-Step Guidance: 1. Urgent Lumbar Puncture for CSF analysis [15]. 2. Start empiric antibiotics immediately [15].
  • Reasoning: Neisseria meningitidis is the most likely culprit in this age group [17]. CSF showing low sugar and high neutrophils confirms a pyogenic etiology [16, 20].

Case Study 3: Decision Under Pressure (The Pupil Dialation)

Scenario: A construction worker falls from a height and hit his head. He had a brief loss of consciousness, felt fine for an hour (lucid interval), but then became rapidly drowsy with a dilated pupil on one side.

  • Critical Diagnosis: Epidural Haematoma [33].
  • Action: Immediate surgical drainage [33].
  • Reasoning: The "lucid interval" followed by rapid deterioration is classic for arterial bleeding (middle meningeal artery) [33, 34]. Delay leads to fatal brain herniation [27, 33].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Describe the types and etiopathogenesis of Hydrocephalus.
    Answer: Hydrocephalus involves increased CSF volume and ventricular dilatation [10]. Primary can be Obstructive (non-communicating, e.g., tumor, aqueduct stenosis) or Non-obstructive (communicating, e.g., overproduction/papilloma or deficient reabsorption/meningitis) [12, 13]. Secondary is compensatory due to tissue loss (normal pressure hydrocephalus) [58].
  2. Differentiate between Epidural and Subdural Haematomas based on origin and morphology.
    Answer: Epidural is arterial (middle meningeal artery), follows skull fractures, and compresses gyri [33, 34]. Subdural is venous (bridging veins), develops between the dura and arachnoid, and may not significantly compress gyri initially [33, 55, 59].
  3. What are the morphological hallmarks of Alzheimer's Disease?
    Answer: Macroscopically: Brain atrophy [39]. Microscopically: Senile neuritic plaques (amyloid core) and neurofibrillary tangles (filamentous collections in neurons) [40].
  4. Define 'Rosenthal fibres' and 'Corpora amylacea'.
    Answer: Rosenthal fibres are eosinophilic elongated bodies on astrocytic processes in gliosis [4]. Corpora amylacea are basophilic starch-like bodies in degenerating astrocytes of the elderly [4].
  5. Explain the '10% rule' for Pheochromocytoma? (Note: Not in these neuro sources). Instead: List the types of demyelinating processes in the PNS.
    Answer: 1. Wallerian degeneration (distal to transection). 2. Axonal degeneration (proceeds backward from terminal). 3. Segmental demyelination (loss of myelin between nodes) [60-62].

  1. A 25-year-old male presents with a bilateral CP-angle mass. Biopsy shows whorled fibrocellular bundles and Verocay bodies. What genetic condition is most likely?
    A. NF-1
    B. NF-2
    C. VHL
    D. APC
    Answer: B (NF-2). Bilateral schwannomas (Antoni A/B, Verocay bodies) are diagnostic of Neurofibromatosis type 2 [63-65].
  2. Which of the following CSF findings is characteristic of acute tuberculous meningitis?
    A. 10,000 neutrophils/µl
    B. Normal sugar and clear appearance
    C. Fibrin web formation and mononuclear leucocytosis
    D. Markedly reduced protein
    Answer: C. Chronic tuberculous meningitis shows slightly turbid CSF that forms a fibrin web, with mononuclear cells and reduced sugar [19, 20].
  3. A pathology report describes 'small round vacuoles in neuronal cells' in a patient with rapidly progressive dementia. What is the agent?
    A. JC Virus
    B. HIV
    C. Prion Protein
    D. Herpes Simplex
    Answer: C (Prion Protein). This describes spongiform encephalopathy (CJD) [24, 66].
  4. A patient presents with a 'strawberry' gallbladder? (Note: Not in neuro). Which tumor shows 'pseudopalisading' of anaplastic cells around areas of necrosis?
    A. Meningioma
    B. Ependymoma
    C. Glioblastoma Multiforme
    D. Medulloblastoma
    Answer: C (Glioblastoma Multiforme). This is the hallmark of WHO Grade IV astrocytoma [54, 67].
  5. What is the primary function of oligodendrocytes in the white matter?
    A. Blood brain barrier
    B. Phagocytosis
    C. Myelin maintenance
    D. Secretion of CSF
    Answer: C. They are the CNS counterparts of Schwann cells [4].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Anchoring Bias: Assuming a drowsy patient after a fall has a simple concussion [35].
    Approach: Always suspect haematoma if there is a lucid interval or focal neurologic deficits [33, 34].
  • Confirmation Bias: Attributing dementia in an elderly person solely to Alzheimer's [39].
    Approach: Rule out reversible causes or vascular dementia (lacunar infarcts) and CJD if myoclonus is present [24, 28, 40].
  • Search Satisfaction: Finding one focus of demyelination and assuming Multiple Sclerosis [68].
    Approach: MS requires dissemination in time and space (recurrent attacks at different sites) [37, 68].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Neurons: Post-mitotic and damage is irreversible [2].
  • Astrocytes: Form gliosis (the CNS scar) [56].
  • Microglia: Mobile phagocytes; form gitter cells [6].
  • Meningitis: Pyogenic (Neutrophils/Low Sugar) vs. Viral (Lymphocytes/Normal Sugar) [20].
  • Stroke: Ischemic (Anoxic) vs. Haemorrhagic (Hypertensive/Aneurysmal) [25, 29, 30].
  • Haematomas: Epidural (Arterial/Traumatic) vs. Subdural (Venous/Traumatic) [33, 34, 59].
  • Gliomas: Commonest primary tumours; graded by malignancy [43, 51].
  • Nerve Sheath: Schwannoma (Verocay bodies) vs. Neurofibroma (Unencapsulated/NF-1) [47, 65, 69].
