Chapter 24: The Skin – Comprehensive Exam Notes

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Normal Skin Structure & Histology

  • Layers: The skin consists of the epidermis and dermis, separated by a dermo-epidermal junction where dermal papillae meet epidermal rete ridges [1, 2].
  • Epidermal Layers (Base to Surface):
    • Stratum Germinatum (Basal Layer): Single layer of keratinocytes; site of mitoses and melanocytes [3].
    • Stratum Spinosum (Prickle Cell Layer): Polygonal cells with desmosomes and PAS-positive tonofilaments [4].
    • Stratum Granulosum: Contains basophilic keratohyaline granules [5].
    • Stratum Lucidum: Present only in palms and soles [5].
    • Stratum Corneum (Horny Layer): Non-nucleated eosinophilic keratin layers [5].
  • Dermal Components: Papillary and reticular dermis containing collagen, blood vessels, and specialized nerve endings (e.g., Meissner corpuscles for touch, Pacinian corpuscles for pressure) [6, 7].
  • Appendages: Includes Eccrine glands (sweat all over), Apocrine glands (axilla/anogenital; decapitation secretion), Sebaceous glands (holocrine; absent in palms/soles), and hair follicles [8-11].

Essential Histopathologic Terms

  • Acanthosis: Epidermal thickening via stratum malpighii hyperplasia [12].
  • Acantholysis: Loss of cohesion between keratinocytes, leading to intraepidermal spaces [13].
  • Dyskeratosis: Premature/abnormal keratinization (e.g., "corps ronds" in Darier's disease) [13, 14].
  • Parakeratosis: Retention of nuclei in the horny layer [13].
  • Spongiosis: Intercellular edema, hallmark of acute dermatitis [2, 15].

Key Dermatoses

  • Psoriasis: Chronic scaling disorder with Auspitz sign (bleeding on scraping) and Munro microabscesses (neutrophils in parakeratotic layer) [16, 17].
  • Lichen Planus: Characterized by "saw-tooth" rete ridges, flat violaceous papules, and a band-like dermal infiltrate [18].
  • Pemphigus Vulgaris: Autoimmune; suprabasal acantholysis ("row of tombstones") due to IgG against cement substance [19, 20].
  • Bullous Pemphigoid: Subepidermal bullae without acantholysis; less severe than pemphigus [21, 22].
  • Dermatitis Herpetiformis: Associated with Gluten-sensitive enteropathy; IgA deposits at dermal papillae tips [23].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Melanocytes vs. Complexion: Racial differences in complexion depend on the melanin content per cell, not the total number of melanocytes [3, 4].
  • Merkel Cells: Specialized touch receptors located within the epidermis [5].
  • Auspitz Sign: Pinpoint bleeding after removing psoriasis scales due to suprapapillary thinning and dilated vessels [16, 17].
  • Koilocytosis: Vacuolated epidermal cells with viral cytopathic effects, characteristic of Verrucae (HPV) [24, 25].
  • PTCH Gene: Mutation on chromosome 9 associated with Nevoid Basal Cell Carcinoma Syndrome [26].
  • Clark’s Levels: Used for Malignant Melanoma prognosis based on the depth of anatomical invasion [27].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Differential Diagnosis Pitfall: Confusing Pemphigus Vulgaris with Bullous Pemphigoid. Reasoning: Pemphigus is intraepidermal (suprabasal) and involves acantholysis, making the bullae flaccid and prone to rupture. Pemphigoid is subepidermal and lacks acantholysis, resulting in tense bullae [20, 22].

Diagnostic Clue: If a patient presents with chronic, itchy vesicles and gastrointestinal distress, why consider Dermatitis Herpetiformis? Reasoning: The strong association with Celiac disease means both conditions often respond to a gluten-free diet [23].

Reflective Question: How does the Auspitz sign relate to the histological feature of suprapapillary thinning in Psoriasis? [17]

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The "Rodent" Ulcer

Scenario: A 60-year-old male presents with a persistent, waxy nodule on his forehead that has recently ulcerated. It has pearly, rolled-up margins [28].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC), Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC), Keratoacanthoma.
  • Prioritizing Interventions: Skin biopsy for histopathology.
  • Reasoning: The location (face/above ear-mouth line), waxy appearance, and pearly rolled margins are pathognomonic for Basal Cell Carcinoma [26, 29].

Case 2: The Malignant Mole

Scenario: A 45-year-old woman notices a pre-existing mole on her leg is changing color, has irregular borders, and is now 8 mm in diameter [30, 31].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Malignant Melanoma vs. Benign Junctional Nevus.
  • Reasoning: Follow the ABCD criteria: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Colour change, and Diameter >6 mm [30].
  • Intervention: Wide local excision; Sentinel lymph node biopsy to evaluate regional status [27].

Case 3: Decision Under Pressure (The Scalded Skin)

Scenario: A patient develops diffuse necrosis of the skin and mucosa after taking a new medication. The skin looks "scalded" and peels off in large sheets [32].

  • Immediate Diagnosis: Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN).
  • Action: Immediate admission to a burn unit, cessation of all non-essential drugs, and intensive fluid/electrolyte management [32].
  • Reasoning: This is a life-threatening medical emergency with high mortality due to loss of the skin's barrier function.

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Acantholysis and name one condition where it is a hallmark.
    Answer: Loss of cohesion between epidermal cells. Hallmark of Pemphigus Vulgaris [13, 19].
  2. Describe the histological features of Psoriasis.
    Answer: Regular elongation of rete ridges, suprapapillary thinning, absence of granular layer, parakeratosis, and Munro microabscesses [17].
  3. What is the "cambium layer" in Sarcoma Botryoides?
    Answer: It is a subepithelial layer of tumor cells found in embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma [33].
  4. Distinguish between Eccrine and Apocrine glands.
    Answer: Eccrine glands are all over the body and secrete sweat for thermoregulation. Apocrine glands are in specific areas (axilla), have larger lumina, and use "decapitation secretion" [8, 9].
  5. Short Essay: Pathogenesis and clinical features of Basal Cell Carcinoma.
    Answer: Locally invasive, slow-growing. Predisposed by UV exposure and PTCH gene mutations. Features "rodent ulcer" with pearly rolled margins and peripheral palisading on histology [26, 34].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A patient presents with flat-topped violaceous papules on the wrist. Biopsy shows "saw-tooth" rete ridges and a band-like lymphocytic infiltrate at the dermo-epidermal junction. What is the diagnosis?
    A) Psoriasis
    B) Lichen Planus
    C) DLE
    D) Pemphigoid
    Correct: B. These are classic clinical and histological features of Lichen Planus [18].
  2. Which HPV types are most commonly associated with Condyloma Acuminatum?
    A) 1, 2
    B) 5, 8
    C) 6, 11
    D) 16, 18
    Correct: C. HPV 6 and 11 are the primary causes of anogenital warts [25, 35].
  3. A 30-year-old male with chronic diarrhea develops itchy, grouped vesicles on his elbows. Immunofluorescence shows granular IgA deposits. Which diet should be recommended?
    A) Low protein
    B) Gluten-free
    C) High fiber
    D) Low fat
    Correct: B. This is Dermatitis Herpetiformis, associated with Celiac disease [23].
  4. Histology of a skin lesion reveals "shadow cells" (cells with lost nuclei) and areas of calcification in the dermis. What is the diagnosis?
    A) Trichoepithelioma
    B) Pilomatricoma
    C) Cylindroma
    D) Seborrheic Keratosis
    Correct: B. Shadow cells and calcification are characteristic of Pilomatricoma (Malherbe tumor) [36].
  5. Which of the following is the most important prognostic factor for Malignant Melanoma?
    A) Cell type (Spindle vs. Epithelioid)
    B) Presence of melanin
    C) Vertical thickness/Depth of invasion
    D) Patient age
    Correct: C. Breslow thickness and Clark’s levels regarding depth are primary prognosticators [27].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Availability Bias: Attributing every scaly lesion on the elbow to Psoriasis.
    Approach: Always check for the Auspitz sign. If absent, consider Lichen Simplex Chronicus or Chronic Dermatitis [16, 37].
  • Premature Closure: Diagnosing a white vulvar patch as "Leukoplakia" and stopping.
    Approach: Leukoplakia is a clinical term, not a diagnosis. One must biopsy to rule out Lichen Sclerosus or Bowen’s Disease (Carcinoma in situ) [38-40].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Skin Architecture: Epidermis (stratified squamous) and Dermis (fibrocollagenous) [1, 41].
  • Psoriasis: Munro microabscess, parakeratosis, Auspitz sign [17, 42].
  • Bullous Diseases: Pemphigus (suprabasal, acantholysis) vs. Pemphigoid (subepidermal) [20, 22].
  • UV-Related Cancer: BCC (locally invasive, PTCH) and SCC (invasive, keratin pearls) [26, 43].
  • Melanoma: Use ABCD rule; Clark’s level/Breslow depth is prognostic [27, 30].
  • Mycosis Fungoides: Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma; features Pautrier's microabscesses [44, 45].

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Normal structure of skin

  • Skin is the external organ that protects against mechanical trauma, UV radiation, and infection; it also participates in thermoregulation, fluid conservation and excretion, sensory perception, and appearance.
  • It has 2 major layers: epidermis and dermis. Dermal papillae project upward and create rete ridges of epidermis.

Epidermis: 5 layers from deep to superficial

  1. Basal cell layer (stratum germinativum): single layer of mitotically active keratinocytes; attached by desmosomes. Contains melanocytes and Langerhans cells. Skin color depends more on melanin content per melanocyte than on melanocyte number.
  2. Prickle cell layer (stratum spinosum / malpighii): polygonal squamous cells with intercellular bridges; tonofilaments contain PAS-positive precursor of keratin.
  3. Granular layer: 1–3 layers of flat cells with basophilic keratohyaline granules that are PAS-negative; thicker in palms and soles.
  4. Stratum lucidum: present only in palms and soles.
  5. Stratum corneum: anucleate keratinized layer.
  • Merkel cells are intraepidermal touch receptors.

Dermis

  • Has papillary dermis and reticular dermis.
  • Contains fibrocollagenous tissue, vessels, lymphatics, nerves, and adnexal structures.
  • Pacinian corpuscles: pressure; Meissner corpuscles: touch in palms/soles/fingertips; specialized corpuscles are important viva points.

Adnexal structures

  • Eccrine sweat glands: widespread, especially palms/soles/axillae; coiled tubular glands opening by pores.
  • Apocrine glands: axilla, anogenital area, ceruminous glands, Moll glands, mammary glands; show decapitation secretion.
  • Sebaceous glands
  • Hair follicles, arrector pili, and nails.

Dermatoses

Genetic dermatoses

  • Ichthyosis vulgaris: AD; scaly lesions on extensor surfaces; hyperkeratosis with thin/absent granular layer.
  • Sex-linked ichthyosis: X-linked recessive; hyperkeratosis with normal/thick granular layer and acanthosis.
  • Xeroderma pigmentosum: AR defect in DNA repair after UV damage; predisposes to SCC, BCC, and melanoma.
  • Darier disease: hyperkeratosis, papillomatosis, dyskeratosis, corps ronds and grains, suprabasal clefts with acantholytic cells.
  • Urticaria pigmentosa: extensive pigmented macules with numerous dermal mast cells.
  • Ataxia telangiectasia: papillary dermis with dilated blood vessels; also recurrent infections and lymphoma-leukemia risk.

Non-infectious inflammatory dermatoses

  • Dermatitis / eczema: inflammatory response to exogenous or endogenous triggers. Acute stage shows marked spongiosis and vesiculation; subacute stage shows smaller vesicles, parakeratosis, crusts; chronic stage shows hyperkeratosis, parakeratosis, acanthosis, elongated rete ridges, fibrosis.
  • Urticaria: transient pruritic wheals; dermal edema and perivascular mononuclear infiltrate. Key mechanism is mast-cell degranulation, not increase in mast cell number.
  • Miliaria crystallina: obstruction within stratum corneum; intracorneal/subcorneal vesicles continuous with sweat ducts.
  • Miliaria rubra: deeper sweat duct obstruction; spongiotic vesicles in stratum malpighii.
  • Panniculitis: inflammation of subcutaneous fat. Erythema nodosum classically gives tender nodules on shins.
  • Acne vulgaris: hair follicle blocked by keratin leading to comedones; papular acne has lymphocytes, pustular acne has neutrophils; rupture causes granulomatous reaction.

Infectious dermatoses

  • Impetigo: superficial bacterial infection with subcorneal pustule full of neutrophils; yellow crust clinically.
  • Verrucae (warts): HPV-related. Histology shows papillomatosis, acanthosis, hyperkeratosis/parakeratosis, inward-bent rete ridges, koilocytosis, clumped keratohyaline granules.
  • Molluscum contagiosum: poxvirus; cup-shaped lesion with intracytoplasmic eosinophilic molluscum bodies.
  • Viral exanthems: intraepidermal vesicles/bullae due to viral cytopathic effect, ballooning degeneration, multinucleate giant cells.
  • Superficial mycoses: fungal hyphae/arthrospores in stratum corneum; PAS stains fungi deep pink-red and Grocott stains fungi black.

Granulomatous diseases

  • Lupus vulgaris: cutaneous tuberculosis; apple-jelly nodules; upper dermal tubercles with epithelioid cells, giant cells, little or absent caseation.
  • Cutaneous sarcoidosis: non-caseating naked granulomas with paucity of lymphocytes.
  • Granuloma annulare: palisading granulomas around central collagen degeneration; association with diabetes mellitus.

Connective tissue dermatoses

  • Discoid lupus erythematosus / SLE skin lesions: hyperkeratosis with keratotic plugging, flattened rete ridges, hydropic degeneration of basal layer, adnexal lymphoid infiltrate, upper dermal edema; DIF shows granular Ig/complement along basement membrane.
  • Systemic sclerosis: thick dermal collagen extending into subcutis, chronic inflammation, thin epidermis, loss of rete ridges/adnexa, vascular wall thickening.
  • Lichen sclerosus et atrophicus: hyperkeratosis with follicular plugging, epidermal thinning, basal hydropic degeneration, upper dermal edema and hyaline collagen.

Non-infectious bullous dermatoses

  • Pemphigus vulgaris: autoimmune; suprabasal acantholytic blister, row of tombstones, flaccid bullae, oral lesions common.
  • Pemphigus vegetans: eosinophilic intraepidermal abscesses.
  • Pemphigus foliaceus: superficial subcorneal bullae with acantholytic cells.
  • Bullous pemphigoid: subepidermal, non-acantholytic tense bullae with eosinophils and peripheral regeneration.
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis: pruritic vesicles; neutrophilic microabscesses in papillary tips; associated with gluten-sensitive enteropathy; DIF shows granular IgA at papillary tips.
  • Erythema multiforme: hypersensitivity to infections/drugs; interface dermatitis with keratinocyte necrosis and later blistering; severe form includes Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis spectrum.

Scaling dermatoses

  • Psoriasis: sharply demarcated plaques with silvery scales; Auspitz sign; nail pitting and psoriatic arthritis may occur. Histology: regular acanthosis, elongated edematous papillae, suprapapillary thinning, absent granular layer, parakeratosis, Munro microabscesses.
  • Lichen planus: pruritic violaceous flat-topped papules; hyperkeratosis, focal hypergranulosis, saw-toothed rete ridges, liquefactive basal degeneration, band-like infiltrate.

Metabolic disease

  • Calcinosis cutis: metastatic, dystrophic, idiopathic, or subepidermal calcified nodule.

Tumours and tumour-like lesions

Benign epidermal lesions and cysts

  • Squamous papilloma: hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, papillomatosis.
  • Seborrheic keratosis: sharply demarcated exophytic lesion with straight-line demarcation from adjacent epidermis; papillomatosis, hyperkeratosis, acanthosis.
  • Fibroepithelial polyp (skin tag): fibrovascular core with hyperplastic epidermis.
  • Epidermal cyst: cyst wall made of true epidermis with laminated keratin.
  • Pilar cyst: abrupt keratinization without granular layer; common on scalp.
  • Dermoid cyst: epidermis plus adnexal structures in wall.
  • Steatocystoma multiplex: multiple inherited cysts with sebaceous lobules in wall.

Premalignant lesions

  • Solar (actinic) keratosis: SCC in situ on sun-exposed skin; hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, dysplasia, dyskeratosis.
  • Bowen disease: full-thickness epidermal carcinoma in situ; hyperkeratosis, parakeratosis, epidermal hyperplasia, bizarre dyskeratotic cells, but basement membrane remains intact.
  • Xeroderma pigmentosum: premalignant because UV-induced DNA damage cannot be repaired adequately.

Malignant tumours

  • Squamous cell carcinoma: related to sun exposure, immunosuppression, chronic scars/ulcers, radiation, carcinogens, HPV-related states. Histology shows invasive downward proliferation of atypical squamous cells with horn pearls in better differentiated lesions.
  • Basal cell carcinoma: slow-growing, locally invasive, rarely metastasizes; commonly on face above a line from ear lobe to mouth corner; basaloid cells with peripheral palisading and shrinkage artifact.
  • Metatypical / basosquamous carcinoma: features of both BCC and SCC; higher malignant potential.

Adnexal tumours

  • Hair follicle tumours: trichoepithelioma, pilomatricoma.
  • Sebaceous tumours: nevus sebaceous, sebaceous adenoma, sebaceous carcinoma.
  • Sweat gland tumours: eccrine poroma, hidradenoma, spiradenoma, papillary hidradenoma, cylindroma, sweat gland carcinoma.

Melanocytic tumours

  • Nevocellular nevi: junctional, compound, intradermal, spindle cell/epithelioid, blue nevus, dysplastic nevi.
  • Malignant melanoma: highly aggressive; important risk clues include change in mole, dysplastic nevus, family history, older age, many moles. ABCD rule = asymmetry, border irregularity, color variation, diameter >6 mm.
  • Immunomarkers: HMB-45, S-100, Melan-A.
  • Clark levels I–V indicate depth of invasion from epidermis to subcutis.

Dermal and migrant tumours

  • Dermatofibroma / malignant fibrous histiocytoma: storiform pattern.
  • Dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans: low-grade fibrosarcoma, locally recurrent, storiform spindle cells infiltrating fat.
  • Xanthomas: foamy histiocytes, often with hyperlipidemia.
  • Mycosis fungoides / CTCL: premycotic, infiltrative, tumor stages; epidermotropism and Darier-Pautrier microabscesses; malignant T cells with cerebriform nuclei.

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Spongiosis = eczema. If the stem says itchy erythematous vesicular lesion with intercellular edema, think dermatitis.
  • Acantholysis = pemphigus. Loss of cohesion produces intraepidermal blister.
  • Subepidermal tense blister with eosinophils = bullous pemphigoid.
  • Neutrophils at dermal papillary tips + gluten sensitivity = dermatitis herpetiformis.
  • Munro microabscesses + parakeratosis + absent granular layer = psoriasis.
  • Saw-toothed rete ridges + band-like infiltrate = lichen planus.
  • Koilocytes = HPV effect.
  • Molluscum bodies = molluscum contagiosum.
  • Naked granulomas = sarcoidosis.
  • Suprabasal blister with tombstones = pemphigus vulgaris.
  • Peripheral palisading of basaloid cells = basal cell carcinoma.
  • Horn pearls = squamous cell carcinoma.
  • ABCD rule + HMB-45 positivity = melanoma.

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Common diagnostic pitfalls

  • Do not confuse urticaria pigmentosa with ordinary urticaria: both involve mast cells functionally, but urticaria pigmentosa has increased dermal mast cells.
  • Do not label every blistering disease as pemphigus. First localize the blister: suprabasal, subcorneal, or subepidermal.
  • Do not mistake Bowen disease for invasive SCC if basement membrane is still intact.
  • Do not confuse seborrheic keratosis or benign nevus with melanoma solely because the lesion is pigmented.
  • In psoriasis, students often memorize scales and forget the histology triad: parakeratosis, absent granular layer, Munro microabscesses.

Bias recognition in dermatopathology

  • Anchoring bias: seeing a pruritic rash and prematurely diagnosing eczema without asking whether it is actually psoriasis, lichen planus, fungal infection, or dermatitis herpetiformis.
  • Availability bias: overcalling common acne when nodulocystic lesions or sudden severe onset may suggest drug-induced acneiform eruption or endocrine cause.
  • Representativeness bias: assuming all skin cancers must ulcerate; melanoma may begin as a flat pigmented lesion.
  • Premature closure: diagnosing wart in a plantar lesion without considering verrucous carcinoma.

Reflective questions

  • Why does localization of a blister matter more than its size?
  • How does knowing the exact epidermal layer involved help narrow the diagnosis?
  • When a lesion is pigmented, what features help you decide between benign nevus, seborrheic keratosis, and melanoma?
  • Why is immunofluorescence often more useful than routine H&E in some autoimmune blistering disorders?
  • What clinical clue tells you a skin lesion may actually reflect systemic disease, such as sarcoidosis, SLE, systemic sclerosis, or celiac disease?

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: Flaccid blister under pressure

A 42-year-old woman has painful oral erosions and flaccid skin bullae that rupture easily. Histology shows a suprabasal cleft with acantholytic cells and basal cells attached like a row of tombstones.

  1. Key problem representation: mucocutaneous blistering disease with fragile bullae.
  2. Differential diagnosis: pemphigus vulgaris, bullous pemphigoid, dermatitis herpetiformis, drug-induced blistering.
  3. Best diagnosis: pemphigus vulgaris because blister is suprabasal and acantholytic, with oral involvement.
  4. Reasoning under uncertainty: if only “blister” is remembered, bullous pemphigoid may be chosen incorrectly; histologic level of split resolves uncertainty.
  5. Priority intervention: stabilize skin barrier/fluid status, prevent secondary infection, confirm with immunologic testing, start disease-specific therapy promptly.

Case 2: Pruritic extensor vesicles in a young adult

A 29-year-old man has intensely itchy grouped vesicles over elbows and buttocks. He also has intermittent loose stools and weight loss. Biopsy shows neutrophilic microabscesses at papillary tips.

  1. Differential diagnosis: dermatitis herpetiformis, eczema, scabies, bullous pemphigoid.
  2. Best diagnosis: dermatitis herpetiformis.
  3. Why: extensor pruritic grouped vesicles, gluten-sensitive enteropathy association, papillary neutrophilic microabscesses, IgA deposition at papillary tips.
  4. Priority intervention: symptomatic relief, confirm immune pattern, assess for celiac disease, begin gluten-free diet.
  5. Cognitive trap: the word “herpetiformis” may mislead toward herpes infection; histology and GI association prevent that mistake.

Case 3: Decision-making under pressure — pigmented lesion

A 58-year-old man reports that a longstanding mole on his back has enlarged, become asymmetrical, developed irregular borders and multiple shades of brown-black, and now bleeds occasionally.

  1. Immediate concern: malignant melanoma until proven otherwise.
  2. Differential diagnosis: dysplastic nevus, seborrheic keratosis, pigmented basal cell carcinoma, melanoma.
  3. Decision under pressure: do not reassure based on old age of lesion; change over time is more important than duration.
  4. Reasoning steps: apply ABCD rule, examine ulceration/bleeding, assess regional nodes, arrange tissue diagnosis, evaluate depth because prognosis depends strongly on invasion.
  5. Bias to avoid: anchoring on “it has been there for years” or “it looks like a mole.” Melanoma can arise in a pre-existing nevus or de novo.

5. University-Level Exam Questions

1. Short answer

Define acanthosis, acantholysis, parakeratosis, spongiosis, and dyskeratosis.

Answer: Acanthosis is thickening of epidermis due to hyperplasia of stratum malpighii. Acantholysis is loss of cohesion between epidermal cells. Parakeratosis is retention of nuclei in stratum corneum. Spongiosis is intercellular edema in epidermis. Dyskeratosis is abnormal keratinization with rounded cells and pyknotic nuclei. These are classic vocabulary items for viva and image-based spotters.

2. Essay

Classify non-infectious bullous dermatoses and describe pemphigus vulgaris and bullous pemphigoid.

Answer outline: Classify into pemphigus, pemphigoid, dermatitis herpetiformis, erythema multiforme. Pemphigus vulgaris is autoimmune with suprabasal acantholytic blister and row of tombstones. Bullous pemphigoid is subepidermal non-acantholytic tense blister with eosinophils. The major differentiator is level of blister and presence/absence of acantholysis.

3. MCQ

Diagnostic histologic feature of psoriasis is:
A. Koilocytosis
B. Munro microabscesses
C. Suprabasal acantholysis
D. Naked granulomas

Correct answer: B. Munro microabscesses are neutrophil collections in parakeratotic layer and are highly characteristic of psoriasis. A indicates HPV, C indicates pemphigus vulgaris, D indicates sarcoidosis.

4. Short answer

Write the histologic features of lichen planus.

Answer: Hyperkeratosis, focal hypergranulosis, irregular acanthosis with saw-toothed rete ridges, liquefactive degeneration of basal layer, and a band-like mononuclear infiltrate closely hugging the basal layer.

5. Essay

Describe premalignant lesions of skin.

Answer: Important premalignant lesions include solar keratosis, Bowen disease, and xeroderma pigmentosum. Solar keratosis is SCC in situ on sun-exposed skin with hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, and dysplasia. Bowen disease is full-thickness epidermal carcinoma in situ with intact basement membrane. Xeroderma pigmentosum predisposes to multiple skin cancers because of defective DNA repair after UV injury.

6. MCQ

Peripheral palisading of basaloid cells is seen in:
A. SCC
B. Basal cell carcinoma
C. Seborrheic keratosis
D. Melanoma

Correct answer: B. Basal cell carcinoma shows nests of basaloid cells with peripheral palisading and shrinkage artifact. SCC instead shows malignant squamous cells with horn pearls.

7. Essay/Long answer

Discuss malignant melanoma with risk factors, morphology, and prognostic basis.

Answer outline: Risk factors include changing mole, dysplastic nevi, family history, many nevi, older age, and UV exposure. Clinically use ABCD rule. Histology shows atypical melanocytes with junctional activity and dermal invasion; markers include HMB-45, S-100, Melan-A. Prognosis depends mainly on stage and depth of invasion, nodal spread, ulceration, and distant metastasis.

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A patient with tense bullae and eosinophils in a subepidermal split most likely has:
    A. Pemphigus vulgaris
    B. Bullous pemphigoid
    C. Dermatitis herpetiformis
    D. Impetigo
    Answer: B. Tense subepidermal blisters with eosinophils are classic for bullous pemphigoid. Pemphigus is intraepidermal and acantholytic.
  2. A skin lesion shows koilocytosis and papillomatosis. Which pathogen is most likely?
    A. HSV
    B. HPV
    C. Poxvirus
    D. Staphylococcus
    Answer: B. Koilocytosis is a viral cytopathic effect of HPV and is highly associated with verrucae and condyloma.
  3. Which finding best distinguishes psoriasis from chronic eczema?
    A. Acanthosis
    B. Hyperkeratosis
    C. Munro microabscesses
    D. Perivascular mononuclear infiltrate
    Answer: C. Acanthosis and hyperkeratosis can occur in both, but Munro microabscesses strongly support psoriasis.
  4. A young patient has extreme UV sensitivity due to defective DNA repair. He is at high risk for:
    A. Only BCC
    B. Only SCC
    C. BCC, SCC, and melanoma
    D. Only melanoma
    Answer: C. Xeroderma pigmentosum predisposes to multiple skin cancers including SCC, BCC, and melanoma.
  5. A pruritic rash with granular IgA at papillary tips is associated with:
    A. Gluten-sensitive enteropathy
    B. Nephrotic syndrome
    C. Rheumatic fever
    D. Hyperparathyroidism
    Answer: A. Dermatitis herpetiformis is strongly linked to celiac disease and improves with a gluten-free diet.
  6. Which lesion is carcinoma in situ involving the full thickness of epidermis but with intact basement membrane?
    A. Solar keratosis only
    B. Bowen disease
    C. Basal cell carcinoma
    D. Keratoacanthoma
    Answer: B. Bowen disease is classic full-thickness epidermal dysplasia/carcinoma in situ.
  7. The strongest bedside clue for melanoma among pigmented lesions is:
    A. Uniform color and symmetry
    B. Diameter less than 4 mm
    C. Asymmetry, border irregularity, color variation, diameter over 6 mm
    D. Presence of scale alone
    Answer: C. This is the ABCD rule for melanoma screening.

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving Framework

  • Step 1: Localize the pathology. Epidermal, dermo-epidermal junction, dermis, subcutis, or adnexal?
  • Step 2: Identify the dominant process. Inflammatory, infectious, autoimmune, neoplastic, metabolic, granulomatous?
  • Step 3: Use one anchor histologic clue. Spongiosis, acantholysis, koilocyte, naked granuloma, horn pearl, palisading, Munro microabscess.
  • Step 4: Add one clinical clue. Pruritus, mucosal involvement, sun exposure, grouped vesicles, silvery scale, changing mole.
  • Step 5: Challenge your first diagnosis. Ask: what else could produce this same morphology?

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Know the 5 layers of epidermis and key cells: melanocytes, Langerhans cells, Merkel cells.
  • Spongiosis suggests eczema; acantholysis suggests pemphigus.
  • Psoriasis: parakeratosis, absent granular layer, Munro microabscesses, Auspitz sign.
  • Lichen planus: pruritic purple papules, saw-toothed rete ridges, band-like infiltrate.
  • Pemphigus vulgaris is suprabasal and acantholytic; bullous pemphigoid is subepidermal and tense.
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis is linked to celiac disease and shows IgA at papillary tips.
  • Verruca = HPV + koilocytes; molluscum = poxvirus + molluscum bodies.
  • Lupus vulgaris has tubercles; sarcoidosis has naked non-caseating granulomas.
  • Solar keratosis and Bowen disease are important premalignant/in situ lesions.
  • SCC forms horn pearls; BCC shows basaloid palisading; melanoma follows ABCD rule and spreads early.
  • Depth of invasion is a major prognostic determinant in melanoma.
  • Always combine morphology with clinical context instead of memorizing isolated buzzwords.
