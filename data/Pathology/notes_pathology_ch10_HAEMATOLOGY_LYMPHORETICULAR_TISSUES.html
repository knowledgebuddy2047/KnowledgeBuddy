Haematology and Erythroid Series: Comprehensive Study Guide

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Haematopoiesis and Bone Marrow

Haematopoiesis is the production of blood's formed elements: erythrocytes (O2 transport), leucocytes (defense), and thrombocytes (haemostasis) [1]. In embryos, it begins in the yolk sac, moves to the liver and spleen by the 3rd month, and primarily resides in the bone marrow from the 7th month onwards [2]. In adults, active marrow is limited to the central skeleton and proximal long bones [2, 3]. All blood cells arise from multipotent haematopoietic stem cells (HSC), identified by the CD34 marker [3].

Bone Marrow Examination

  • Aspiration: Provides better individual cell morphology; performed at the sternum or iliac crest using a Salah needle [4-6].
  • Trephine Biopsy: Provides superior architectural pattern and cellularity; performed at the posterior iliac crest using a Jamshidi needle [5-7]. Essential for "dry taps" seen in myelofibrosis or aplastic anaemia [6, 8].

Erythropoiesis and the Red Cell

The process is regulated by Erythropoietin (EPO), produced by the kidneys in response to anoxia [9]. The maturation sequence is: Proerythroblast → Basophilic erythroblast → Polychromatic erythroblast → Orthochromatic erythroblast → Reticulocyte → Mature RBC [10-12]. Reticulocytes contain ribosomal RNA and are the earliest RBC form in peripheral blood (Normal: 0.5–2.5%); they are a key indicator of marrow erythropoietic activity [12-14].

General Considerations of Anaemia

Anaemia is a reduction in Hb concentration below normal for age and sex (<13.0 g/dl in men, <11.5 g/dl in women) [15]. Clinical features like pallor, tachycardia, and dyspnoea result from tissue hypoxia and compensatory mechanisms like a rightward shift in the Hb-dissociation curve (increased 2,3-BPG) [16-18].

Classification of Anaemias

  • Morphologic: Based on Red Cell Indices (MCV, MCH, MCHC) [19].
    • Microcytic Hypochromic: IDA, Thalassaemia, Sideroblastic [19, 20].
    • Normocytic Normochromic: Acute blood loss, Haemolytic anaemias [21].
    • Macrocytic: B12 and Folate deficiency [21].
  • Pathophysiologic: Blood loss, impaired production, or increased destruction (haemolysis) [22, 23].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Red Cell Indices: MCV (size) is the most useful for initial classification. MCHC (Hb concentration) is reduced in IDA but often normal in macrocytic anaemias [24, 25].
  • PBF Clues: Target cells (Thalassaemia/Liver disease), Schistocytes (Microangiopathy), Spherocytes (Hereditary Spherocytosis/AIHA), and Hypersegmented Neutrophils (Megaloblastic Anaemia) [26-29].
  • Iron Profile: In IDA: Low Serum Iron, Low Ferritin, High TIBC. In Anaemia of Chronic Disease: Low Serum Iron, High/Normal Ferritin, Low/Normal TIBC [30-32].
  • The "Methyl-Folate Trap": B12 deficiency leads to a functional folate deficiency by trapping folate as methyl-THF, which cannot be converted to the active form for DNA synthesis [33, 34].
  • Sickle Cell Mutation: A single point mutation substituting Valine for Glutamic acid at the 6th position of the β-globin chain [35].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Differentiating Microcytic Anaemias: Many students mistakenly assume all microcytic hypochromic anaemias are Iron Deficiency. One must use the RDW and Mentzer Index. IDA typically has a high RDW (anisocytosis), while Thalassaemia trait has a low/normal RDW and a very low MCV disproportionate to the mild anaemia [25, 31, 36].

Reflective Question: Why does a patient with severe B12 deficiency present with neurological symptoms (subacute combined degeneration), but a patient with pure Folate deficiency typically does not? Hint: Look at the role of Adenosyl B12 in propionate metabolism and myelin maintenance [33, 37].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Heavy Menstruator

Scenario: A 25-year-old female presents with progressive pallor and dyspnoea for 4 weeks. She has a history of heavy, irregular menstrual bleeding and two recent abortions [38].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA), Anaemia of chronic blood loss [23, 39].
  • Prioritizing Interventions: 1. Confirm Hb and Ferritin levels. 2. Identify and stop the source of bleeding (Gynaecological consult). 3. Iron replacement therapy [40].
  • Reasoning: Reproductive-age females are the highest risk group for IDA due to blood loss and increased requirements [39].

Case Study 2: The "Thalassaemic Facies"

Scenario: A 3-year-old child presents with stunted growth, a protuberant abdomen, and "thalassaemic facies" (prominent forehead and cheeks). The mother is also mildly anaemic [41].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Thalassaemia Major, Sideroblastic Anaemia [42, 43].
  • Prioritizing Interventions: 1. Hb Electrophoresis (expect High HbF). 2. Start regular blood transfusion regimen. 3. Monitor for iron overload and start chelation [44, 45].
  • Decision-Making Under Pressure: If this child presents with sudden severe pallor and lethargy, consider an Aplastic Crisis (often triggered by Parvovirus B19), requiring urgent transfusion [46, 47].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Anaemia and give its morphologic classification.
    Answer: Hb <13 g/dl (men), <11.5 g/dl (women) [15]. Classifications: Microcytic hypochromic, Normocytic normochromic, and Macrocytic [19, 21].
  2. Compare Bone Marrow Aspiration and Trephine Biopsy.
    Answer: Aspiration is better for cell morphology (e.g., Megaloblasts); Trephine is better for architecture (e.g., Aplasia, Fibrosis) [6].
  3. Short Essay: Describe the peripheral blood findings in Megaloblastic Anaemia.
    Answer: Key findings include macro-ovalocytes, marked anisocytosis, poikilocytosis, and the pathognomonic hypersegmented neutrophils (>5 lobes) [28, 48].
  4. Which enzyme deficiency is associated with "bite cells" and Heinz bodies?
    A) Pyruvate Kinase B) G6PD C) Glucocerebrosidase D) Hexokinase
    Answer: B) G6PD. Oxidant stress causes Hb precipitation (Heinz bodies), which the spleen "bites" out [49, 50].
  5. True/False: Serum Ferritin is the most distinguishing feature between IDA and Anaemia of Chronic Disease.
    Answer: True. Ferritin is low in IDA and high/normal in Chronic Disease [51, 52].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 60-year-old male with a history of a total gastrectomy 5 years ago presents with paresthesia in his feet and a beefy red tongue. A PBF shows macrocytosis. What is the most likely cause?
    A) Folate deficiency
    B) Iron deficiency
    C) Vitamin B12 deficiency
    D) Aplastic anaemia
    Correct: C. Gastrectomy removes the source of Intrinsic Factor, leading to B12 malabsorption. Neurological symptoms are specific to B12 [37, 53, 54].
  2. A 10-year-old boy of Mediterranean descent presents with severe anaemia and splenomegaly. Hb electrophoresis shows 90% HbF and 5% HbA2. What is the diagnosis?
    A) β-Thalassaemia minor
    B) β-Thalassaemia major
    C) Sickle cell trait
    D) G6PD deficiency
    Correct: B. High HbF and low/absent HbA are hallmarks of β-Thalassaemia major [43, 44].
  3. A patient on treatment for tuberculosis (Isoniazid) develops microcytic anaemia. Bone marrow shows ringed sideroblasts. What is the mechanism?
    A) Iron deficiency
    B) Defective globin synthesis
    C) Impaired haeme synthesis (B6 antagonism)
    D) Chronic inflammation
    Correct: C. Isoniazid is a Vitamin B6 antagonist, which is required for ALA synthetase in the haeme pathway [55, 56].
  4. Which of the following is an expected finding in Intravascular Haemolysis?
    A) Splenomegaly
    B) Increased Haptoglobin
    C) Haemoglobinuria
    D) Decreased unconjugated bilirubin
    Correct: C. Lysis within vessels releases Hb directly into the plasma and then urine [57, 58].
  5. A 40-year-old woman with Rheumatoid Arthritis has a Hb of 10 g/dl. Her Ferritin is 300 ng/ml (Normal 30-250) and TIBC is 200 μg/dl (Normal 250-450). Diagnosis?
    A) Iron Deficiency Anaemia
    B) Sideroblastic Anaemia
    C) Anaemia of Chronic Disease
    D) Megaloblastic Anaemia
    Correct: C. High ferritin and low TIBC in the setting of chronic inflammation [32, 51, 52].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Availability Bias: A clinician may diagnose every microcytic anaemia as Iron Deficiency because it is the "commonest" [59]. Approach: Always check Mentzer's Index (MCV/RBC). If <13, suspect Thalassaemia trait; if >13, suspect IDA. Never start iron therapy without confirming low ferritin, as it may cause iron overload in Thalassaemia patients [60].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Bone Marrow: M/E ratio is normally 3:1 [61].
  • Anaemia: Always look at the MCV first to narrow the differential [19, 25].
  • IDA: Look for High TIBC and Low Ferritin [30].
  • Megaloblastic: Look for Hypersegmented Neutrophils and check B12 vs Folate [28, 62].
  • Sickle Cell: Vaso-occlusive crises and autosplenectomy are key clinical markers [63].
  • Thalassaemia Major: Severe microcytic anaemia requiring lifelong transfusions [45].
  • Aplastic Anaemia: Pancytopenia with a fatty, hypocellular marrow [64, 65].
