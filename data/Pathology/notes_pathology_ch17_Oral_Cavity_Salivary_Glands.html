Chapter 17: The Oral Cavity and Salivary Glands

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Oral Soft Tissues: Developmental and Inflammatory

The oral cavity is lined with squamous epithelium, keratinized over the hard palate and gingiva [1]. Fordyce’s granules are yellowish spots representing ectopic sebaceous glands [2]. Aphthous ulcers (canker sores) are the most common oral ulcerations, often recurrent and painful, possibly linked to stress or nutritional deficiencies [3]. Candidiasis (Thrush) is a common opportunistic infection in immunocompromised hosts, appearing as erythematous lesions [4].

Premalignant and Malignant Lesions

  • Leukoplakia: A white patch exceeding 5 mm that cannot be rubbed off [5]. It is strongly associated with tobacco use [6]. Histologically, it ranges from simple hyperkeratosis to severe dysplasia/carcinoma in situ [7].
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC): Comprises 90% of oral malignancies [8]. Strongest associations include tobacco, alcohol, and HPV (16, 18, 33) [9]. Common sites: lower lip, tongue, and floor of mouth [10].

Diseases of Teeth and Jaws

Dental Caries involves the decalcification of enamel and dentin by organic acids produced by acidogenic bacteria (Streptococci) in bacterial plaques [11]. Sequelae include pulpitis, apical granuloma, and periapical abscesses [12-14].

  • Radicular Cyst: Most common inflammatory cyst, arising from the apex of an erupted tooth due to pulpitis [15].
  • Ameloblastoma: Most common benign but locally invasive odontogenic tumor [16]. It often shows a follicular pattern with a "stellate reticulum" appearance [17].

Salivary Glands

Mumps (Epidemic Parotitis) is a viral infection characterized by a triad of parotitis, orchitis/oophoritis, and pancreatitis [18]. Pleomorphic Adenoma (Mixed Tumor) is the most common salivary gland tumor, containing both epithelial and mesenchymal (myxoid/chondroid) elements [19, 20]. Warthin’s Tumor is a monomorphic adenoma featuring eosinophilic epithelium and a dense lymphoid stroma [21].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Hairy Leukoplakia: A specific corrugated white lesion in AIDS patients, caused by EBV, but not typically premalignant [6].
  • Hutchinson’s Incisors: Peg-shaped, notched teeth diagnostic of congenital syphilis [22].
  • Ameloblastoma: Radiologically appears as multilocular bone destruction ("soap bubble" appearance) [16].
  • Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma (Cylindroma): Highly malignant tumor known for perineural invasion and a "cribriform" (Swiss cheese) histological pattern [23].
  • Sjögren’s Syndrome Triad: Xerostomia (dry mouth), Keratoconjunctivitis sicca (dry eyes), and Rheumatoid Arthritis [24].
  • Mucoepidermoid Carcinoma: The most common radiation-induced and most common malignant salivary gland tumor in children [25].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Differential Diagnosis Pitfall: Students often confuse a Mucocele with a Ranula.
Mechanism: A mucocele is a general term for a mucous retention cyst of minor glands [26]. A ranula is specifically a large mucocele on the floor of the mouth, often involving the sublingual gland [26].

Clinical Pearl: Why does SCC of the lip have a better prognosis than intraoral SCC?
Reasoning: Lip lesions are visible early and have less frequent initial metastasis compared to the tongue or soft palate, where metastasis occurs early and detection is often late [27].

Reflective Question: How does the presence of "stellate reticulum-like cells" in ameloblastoma reflect its embryological origin? [17]

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Roadside Tailor's Ulcer

Scenario: A 50-year-old male presents with a non-healing indurated ulcer in the right buccal vestibule and matted cervical lymphadenopathy. He has chewed tobacco (khaini/zarda) since adulthood, keeping the bolus in the same area [28].

  • Differential: Squamous Cell Carcinoma, Dysplastic Leukoplakia, Syphilitic Chancre.
  • Reasoning: Long-term tobacco contact (irritative etiology) leading to chronic ulceration and lymph node involvement suggests Squamous Cell Carcinoma [8, 9].
  • Step-by-Step: 1. Biopsy the ulcer edge. 2. FNAC of lymph nodes. 3. Imaging (CT/MRI) for local spread [29].

Case 2: The "Soap-Bubble" Jaw Mass

Scenario: A 40-year-old female presents with a slow-growing, painless swelling in the molar-ramus region of the mandible. X-ray shows multilocular destruction of the bone [16].

  • Diagnosis: Ameloblastoma.
  • Reasoning: The location (molar-ramus) and characteristic "soap-bubble" multilocular X-ray findings are classic [16].
  • Management: Locally invasive, so wide surgical excision is required to prevent recurrence [16].

Case 3: Decision Under Pressure (Dental Emergency)

Scenario: A patient with a chronic dental abscess suddenly develops high fever, severe headache, and proptosis (bulging eye).

  • Urgent Risk: Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis [14].
  • Action: Immediate hospitalization, IV antibiotics, and imaging.
  • Reasoning: Infections from the upper teeth can track through the venous drainage into the cavernous sinus, becoming life-threatening [14].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Short Essay: Describe the pathogenesis and stages of dental caries.
    Answer: Caries is caused by refined carbs + saliva + acidogenic bacteria (Streptococci) producing organic acids [11]. This decalcifies enamel (chalky white spots) and then dentin [30]. Sequelae include pulpitis and abscesses [12].
  2. Define Leukoplakia. What are its histological types?
    Answer: A white plaque >5 mm that can't be rubbed off or diagnosed as another disease [5]. Types: 1. Hyperkeratotic (orderly hyperplasia) and 2. Dysplastic (cytologic atypia, loss of polarity) [7].
  3. Distinguish between Radicular and Dentigerous cysts.
    Answer: Radicular arises from an erupted tooth's apex due to inflammation [15]. Dentigerous arises from an unerupted tooth's enamel, common in 3rd molars [31].
  4. What are the morphological features of Pleomorphic Adenoma?
    Answer: "Mixed" appearance with epithelial ducts/sheets and mesenchymal elements like myxoid, mucoid, and pseudocartilage [20, 32].
  5. List the oral manifestations of AIDS.
    Answer: Candidiasis, Hairy leukoplakia, Kaposi's sarcoma, and non-Hodgkin's lymphoma [33, 34].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 7-year-old child presents with bilateral parotid swelling, pain on eating, and abdominal pain. What is the most likely additional finding?
    A) Endocarditis B) Orchitis C) Arthritis D) Glomerulonephritis
    Correct: B (Orchitis). Part of the mumps triad (Parotitis, Orchitis, Pancreatitis) [18, 35].
  2. A 55-year-old male heavy smoker has a biopsy of a parotid mass showing "slit-like cystic spaces containing milky fluid" and lymphoid tissue with germinal centers. Diagnosis?
    A) Pleomorphic Adenoma B) Mucoepidermoid Carcinoma C) Warthin's Tumor D) Ameloblastoma
    Correct: C (Warthin’s Tumor). The lymphoid stroma and cystic spaces are pathognomonic [21, 36].
  3. Biopsy of a jaw tumor reveals nests of cells with peripheral palisading of columnar cells and a central loose "stellate reticulum." The diagnosis is:
    A) Odontoma B) Radicular Cyst C) Ameloblastoma D) Squamous Cell Carcinoma
    Correct: C (Ameloblastoma). These are the hallmark histological features [17].
  4. A 30-year-old female presents with dry eyes, dry mouth, and painful joints. Which biopsy is most likely to confirm the diagnosis?
    A) Major Salivary Gland B) Minor Salivary Gland C) Buccal Mucosa D) Gingiva
    Correct: B (Minor Salivary Gland). Used to confirm Sjögren’s syndrome [18, 24].
  5. Which histological finding in oral leukoplakia carries the highest risk of malignant transformation?
    A) Hyperkeratosis B) Parakeratosis C) Erythroplasia D) Orthokeratosis
    Correct: C (Erythroplasia). It indicates more marked atypia and higher malignancy risk [7].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Anchoring Bias: Assuming all "white patches" are simple leukoplakia from smoking.
    Approach: Always biopsy white patches because 4-6% may already be carcinomatous; nodular or speckled forms are higher risk [7, 37].
  • Search Satisfaction: Diagnosing a parotid tumor as "benign" and stopping.
    Approach: Be aware that 5% of mixed tumors undergo malignant transformation (Carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma), especially if there's a sudden rapid increase in size or facial palsy [38].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Oral Cancer: Lower lip has best prognosis; tongue/palate has worst [27].
  • Leukoplakia: Premalignant white patch; tobacco is the main etiologic factor [6].
  • Ameloblastoma: Locally invasive; multilocular "soap-bubble" X-ray; follicular histology [16, 17].
  • Mumps: Viral parotitis with risks of orchitis and pancreatitis [18].
  • Salivary Tumors: Parotid is commonest site (85%); Pleomorphic Adenoma is commonest tumor (benign) [19, 39].
  • Caries: Decalcification by acidogenic Streptococci [11].
