Chapter 5: Inflammation and Healing

Chunk 1: Introduction & Vascular Events

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Inflammation is defined as the local response of living mammalian tissues to injury from any agent.”
  • “Cardinal signs of inflammation are: rubor, tumor, calor, dolor, and functio laesa.”
  • “Acute inflammation is of short duration (less than 2 weeks)… Chronic inflammation is of longer duration.”
  • “Triple response: red line, flare, wheal.”
  • “Exudative inflammatory oedema results from increased vascular permeability.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Definition & Causes: Inflammation is a protective response to microbial, immunological, physical, chemical, or inert agents. Important distinction: inflammation ≠ infection.
  • Types: Acute (short, neutrophil-dominant) vs. Chronic (long, lymphocyte/macrophage-dominant). Subacute is intermediate.
  • Vascular Events: Sequence includes transient vasoconstriction → vasodilatation → hydrostatic pressure rise → stasis → leucocyte margination/emigration.
  • Triple Response: Demonstrates redness, heat, swelling, pain via vasodilatation and fluid transudation.
  • Exudate Formation: Explained by Starling’s hypothesis; endothelial leakiness causes protein-rich exudate.

Chunk 2: Patterns of Vascular Permeability & Cellular Events

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Contraction of endothelial cells is the most common mechanism of increased leakiness.”
  • “Delayed and prolonged leakage is seen in sunburns mediated by ultraviolet radiation.”
  • “Direct injury to endothelial cells causes cell necrosis and physical gaps.”
  • “Leucocyte-mediated endothelial injury results from proteolytic enzymes and toxic oxygen species.”
  • “Exudation of leucocytes is the most important feature of inflammatory response.”
  • “Rolling and adhesion are mediated by selectins, integrins, and immunoglobulin superfamily molecules.”
  • “Emigration involves neutrophils crossing basement membrane by collagenase activity.”
  • “Chemotaxis is mediated by LT-B4, complement components (C5a, C3a), IL-8, and bacterial products.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Patterns of permeability: Five mechanisms – endothelial contraction, cytoskeletal reorganisation, direct injury, leucocyte-mediated injury, and neovascularisation. Each has distinct timing and mediators.
  • Exudation of leucocytes: Central to acute inflammation; neutrophils dominate early, followed by monocytes/macrophages.
  • Steps in leucocyte migration: Margination → rolling → adhesion → emigration → chemotaxis. Mediated by CAMs (selectins, integrins, ICAM, VCAM, PECAM).
  • Chemotaxis: Directed migration of leucocytes toward chemical signals; critical for localisation of inflammatory response.

Chunk 3: Phagocytosis – Recognition, Engulfment, Killing

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Phagocytosis is defined as the process of engulfment of solid particulate material by the cells.”
  • “Opsonisation means preparing for eating.”
  • “Main opsonins: IgG, C3b, and lectins.”
  • “Phagosome fuses with lysosome to form phagolysosome.”
  • “Respiratory burst requires NADPH oxidase.”
  • “MPO-dependent killing is the most potent antibacterial system in polymorphs.”
  • “Reactive oxygen metabolites are useful in eliminating microbes that grow within phagocytes e.g. M. tuberculosis.”
  • “Nitric oxide is another potent mechanism of microbial killing.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Phagocytic cells: Neutrophils (microphages) and macrophages are the main phagocytes in inflammation.
  • Steps: Recognition & attachment (via opsonins), engulfment (pseudopod formation → phagosome), killing & degradation (oxidative and non-oxidative mechanisms).
  • Oxidative killing: Involves respiratory burst, NADPH oxidase, H2O2, and MPO-halide system producing hypohalous acids.
  • Non-oxidative killing: Lysosomal enzymes, defensins, nitric oxide contribute to microbial lysis.
  • Extracellular mechanisms: Degranulation and immune-mediated lysis also play roles outside the cell.

Chunk 4: Intracellular & Extracellular Killing Mechanisms

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Respiratory burst requires NADPH oxidase.”
  • “MPO-dependent killing via H2O2-MPO-halide system is the most potent antibacterial mechanism.”
  • “MPO-independent killing occurs through Haber-Weiss and Fenton reactions.”
  • “Reactive oxygen metabolites are particularly useful in eliminating microbes that grow within phagocytes e.g. M. tuberculosis.”
  • “Nitric oxide is another potent mechanism of microbial killing.”
  • “Extracellular mechanisms include degranulation and immune-mediated lysis.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Oxidative killing: MPO-dependent (hypohalous acids) vs MPO-independent (hydroxyl radicals). Central to neutrophil bactericidal activity.
  • Non-oxidative killing: Lysosomal hydrolases, defensins, nitric oxide synthase products. Important in macrophages lacking MPO.
  • Extracellular killing: Degranulation continues outside the cell; immune-mediated cytolysis also contributes.
  • Clinical relevance: Defects in NADPH oxidase → Chronic Granulomatous Disease; MPO deficiency → impaired killing.

Chunk 5: Mediators of Acute Inflammation

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Mediators of acute inflammation are chemical substances which initiate, amplify, and terminate the inflammatory response.”
  • “They may be derived from plasma or from cells.”
  • “Histamine and serotonin are the principal vasoactive amines.”
  • “Arachidonic acid metabolites include prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and lipoxins.”
  • “Complement system components C3a and C5a are potent chemotactic and anaphylatoxic agents.”
  • “Cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-1 play a central role in inflammation.”
  • “Nitric oxide is a soluble gas mediator with vasodilator and antimicrobial properties.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Sources: Mediators are either plasma-derived (complement, kinins, coagulation factors) or cell-derived (mast cells, macrophages, platelets, endothelium).
  • Vasoactive amines: Histamine and serotonin cause vasodilatation and increased vascular permeability.
  • Arachidonic acid metabolites: Prostaglandins (vasodilatation, pain, fever), leukotrienes (chemotaxis, bronchospasm), lipoxins (anti-inflammatory).
  • Complement system: C3a, C5a → chemotaxis, increased permeability, mast cell degranulation.
  • Cytokines: TNF-α, IL-1 → fever, leukocyte activation, adhesion molecule expression.
  • Nitric oxide: Vasodilatation, inhibits platelet aggregation, microbial killing.

Chunk 6: Outcomes of Acute Inflammation

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Acute inflammation may resolve, heal by fibrosis, progress to suppuration, or become chronic.”
  • “Resolution occurs when the cause is eliminated and tissue returns to normal.”
  • “Organisation and fibrosis follow when tissue destruction is extensive.”
  • “Suppuration results in abscess formation due to pyogenic bacteria.”
  • “Progression to chronic inflammation occurs when the stimulus persists.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Resolution: Complete restoration of normal tissue structure and function when injury is mild and cause is eliminated.
  • Healing by fibrosis: Occurs when tissue damage is severe or exudate cannot be cleared; granulation tissue replaces damaged tissue → scar formation.
  • Suppuration: Pyogenic organisms (e.g., staphylococci) cause pus formation and abscess development.
  • Progression to chronic inflammation: Persistent stimulus or repeated injury leads to chronic inflammatory response with lymphocytes, plasma cells, macrophages.

Chunk 7: Morphologic Patterns of Acute Inflammation

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Morphologic patterns of acute inflammation depend upon the type of exudate and tissue response.”
  • “Serous inflammation is characterised by outpouring of thin fluid.”
  • “Fibrinous inflammation results when vascular permeability is increased and fibrinogen passes out.”
  • “Suppurative or purulent inflammation is characterised by pus formation.”
  • “Membranous inflammation is marked by formation of pseudomembrane.”
  • “Haemorrhagic inflammation occurs when blood escapes into tissues.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Serous inflammation: Watery exudate, seen in viral infections, burns, blisters.
  • Fibrinous inflammation: Fibrin-rich exudate, common in pericarditis, pleuritis; may organise into scar tissue.
  • Suppurative (purulent): Pus formation due to pyogenic bacteria; abscess formation typical.
  • Membranous inflammation: Pseudomembrane composed of necrotic epithelium and exudate, e.g., diphtheria, pseudomembranous colitis.
  • Haemorrhagic inflammation: Severe vascular injury with RBC extravasation, seen in anthrax, viral haemorrhagic fevers.

Chunk 8: Chronic Inflammation – Features & Mechanisms

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Chronic inflammation is of longer duration and occurs after delay, either after the causative agent of acute inflammation persists for a long time, or the stimulus induces chronic inflammation from the beginning.”
  • “Characteristic feature of chronic inflammation is presence of lymphocytes, plasma cells, and macrophages.”
  • “Granulation tissue formation and granulomatous inflammation are specific features.”
  • “Chronic active inflammation is the type in which acute exacerbations occur during the course of disease.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Definition: Chronic inflammation lasts weeks to months, often following unresolved acute inflammation or persistent stimuli.
  • Cellular features: Dominated by lymphocytes, plasma cells, macrophages (instead of neutrophils in acute inflammation).
  • Tissue response: Granulation tissue formation, fibrosis, and granulomatous inflammation in specific conditions.
  • Chronic active inflammation: Alternating phases of acute flare-ups and chronic persistence, e.g., chronic bronchitis, rheumatoid arthritis.

Chunk 9: Granulomatous Inflammation – Definition, Causes, Morphology

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Granulomatous inflammation is a distinctive pattern of chronic inflammation characterised by granuloma formation.”
  • “Granuloma is a microscopic aggregation of epithelioid cells, often surrounded by lymphocytes.”
  • “Multinucleated giant cells are formed by fusion of macrophages.”
  • “Granulomatous inflammation occurs in tuberculosis, leprosy, syphilis, sarcoidosis, Crohn’s disease.”
  • “Caseating granulomas are typical of tuberculosis.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Definition: Granulomatous inflammation is a chronic inflammatory response with granuloma formation, a hallmark of certain infections and immune-mediated diseases.
  • Causes: Infectious (tuberculosis, leprosy, syphilis, fungal infections) and non-infectious (sarcoidosis, Crohn’s disease, foreign body reactions).
  • Morphology: Granulomas consist of epithelioid macrophages, giant cells, lymphocytes, fibroblasts. Caseating necrosis is seen in TB; non-caseating granulomas in sarcoidosis.
  • Giant cells: Langhans-type (peripheral nuclei arrangement) vs foreign body-type (haphazard nuclei arrangement).

Chunk 10: Healing – Definition, Types, Processes

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • Healing is the process by which lost tissue is replaced by regeneration or repair.
  • Regeneration is replacement of lost cells by identical cells.
  • Repair is replacement of lost tissue by fibrous tissue (scar formation).
  • Healing occurs by primary intention or secondary intention.
  • Granulation tissue is the hallmark of repair.

2. Passage Highlights

  • Definition: Healing restores tissue integrity after injury, either by regeneration (same cell type) or repair (fibrosis).
  • Regeneration: Possible in labile and stable cells (epithelium, liver, kidney) if supporting stroma is intact.
  • Repair: Occurs when regeneration is not possible; fibroblasts and collagen deposition lead to scar formation.
  • Primary intention: Healing of clean surgical wounds with minimal tissue loss; edges approximated → minimal scar.
  • Secondary intention: Healing of large, infected, or gaping wounds; granulation tissue fills defect → larger scar.
  • Granulation tissue: Composed of proliferating fibroblasts, new capillaries, inflammatory cells; essential for repair.

Chunk 13: Healing in Specialised Tissues – Bone, Nerve, Myocardium

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Myocardial cells are permanent cells; healing occurs by fibrosis.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Bone healing: Fracture healing involves haematoma formation, organisation into granulation tissue, callus formation, ossification, and remodelling. Requires intact periosteum and good vascular supply.

  • Clinical relevance: Bone fractures can heal completely, but CNS injuries and myocardial infarction result in permanent functional loss due to fibrosis.

Chunk 14: Regeneration – Mechanisms and Examples

1. Highlight Lines & Words

  • “Regeneration is replacement of lost cells by identical cells.”
  • “It occurs in labile and stable cells but not in permanent cells.”
  • “Stem cells and growth factors play a central role in regeneration.”
  • “Regeneration requires intact supporting stroma.”
  • “Examples include regeneration of liver, kidney, and skin epithelium.”

2. Passage Highlights

  • Definition: Regeneration restores tissue by proliferation of surviving cells or stem cells, replacing lost cells with identical ones.
  • Cell types: Labile cells (continuously dividing, e.g., epithelium, bone marrow), stable cells (divide on demand, e.g., liver, kidney), permanent cells (neurons, cardiac muscle – no regeneration).
  • Mechanisms: Controlled by growth factors (EGF, PDGF, FGF, VEGF) and stem cell activity. Requires intact extracellular matrix and stroma.
  • Examples: Liver regeneration after partial hepatectomy, epidermal regeneration after abrasion, compensatory hypertrophy in kidney.
  • Clinical relevance: Regeneration is limited in permanent tissues; damage in CNS or myocardium heals by fibrosis instead.

1. Explanation Notes of the Full Chapter

This chapter examines inflammation, defined as the local response of living mammalian tissues to injury [1]. It is a protective defense reaction intended to eliminate injurious agents and remove necrotic debris, followed by healing [1, 2]. While typically protective, inflammation can sometimes be harmful, as seen in anaphylaxis or chronic diseases like rheumatoid arthritis [2].

  • Types of Inflammation:
    • Acute: Short duration (under 2 weeks), characterized by fluid/plasma accumulation and neutrophils [3, 4].
    • Chronic: Longer duration, featuring lymphocytes, plasma cells, macrophages, and often granulation tissue [3].
  • Vascular and Cellular Events: Acute inflammation involves immediate haemodynamic changes (vasoconstriction then persistent vasodilation) and altered vascular permeability (leakiness), leading to inflammatory oedema or exudate [5-7]. Cellular events include leucocyte exudation (margination, rolling, adhesion, emigration) and phagocytosis [8-10].
  • Chemical Mediators: These substances (e.g., histamine, prostaglandins, cytokines, and complement proteins) trigger and regulate the inflammatory response [10-12].
  • Healing: Occurs via regeneration (proliferation of original parenchymal cells) or repair (replacement by connective tissue/fibrosis) [13]. The outcome depends on whether the damaged cells are labile, stable, or permanent [14-16].

2. Core Concepts & High-Quality Explanations

Cardinal Signs of Inflammation

Named by Celsus and Virchow, these five signs are: Rubor (redness), Tumor (swelling), Calor (heat), Dolor (pain), and Functio laesa (loss of function) [4, 17].

Starling’s Hypothesis in Inflammation

Normally, fluid balance is maintained between intravascular hydrostatic pressure and colloid osmotic pressure [7]. In inflammation, the endothelial lining becomes "leaky," decreasing intravascular osmotic pressure and increasing interstitial osmotic pressure, which results in exudative inflammatory oedema [18].

Virchow’s Triad and Thrombosis

Direct injury to endothelium can trigger thrombosis, involving platelets and fibrin, which can further increase vascular permeability [19].

The Granuloma

A granuloma is a circumscribed collection of modified macrophages called epithelioid cells, often with multinucleated giant cells, rimmed by lymphocytes [20]. It forms when the body fails to digest a persistent antigen, such as M. tuberculosis [21, 22].

3. Critical Thinking Focus

Assumptions and Common Pitfalls

  • Assumption: "Inflammation is always beneficial." Reality: It can cause significant harm, such as fibrous adhesions leading to intestinal obstruction or chronic joint destruction [2].
  • Pitfall: Confusing infection with inflammation. Infection is the invasion of microbes, whereas inflammation is the body's response to that invasion or other injuries [2].
  • Reasoning Bias: Assuming all acute inflammation presents with neutrophils. For example, typhoid fever is an acute process but shows a lymphocytic response [23].

Reflective Questions

  1. Why does a "soft tubercle" (caseation necrosis) indicate a more advanced or severe tuberculous lesion than a "hard tubercle"? [24, 25]
  2. How does the "cell cycle" status of an organ (labile vs. permanent) dictate whether it will heal by regeneration or scarring? [15, 26]

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case Study 1: The Infected Wound

Scenario: A 25-year-old male presents with a jagged, dirty laceration on his leg from a construction site. The wound is left open rather than sutured.

  • Problem-Solving: This wound will undergo healing by second intention (secondary union) [27].
  • Clinical Reasoning: Because the wound is "unclean" and has a large defect, it requires the formation of exuberant granulation tissue from the base upward [27, 28].
  • Intervention: Surgical debridement is necessary to remove necrotic tissue and prevent bacterial toxins from delaying healing [29].

Case Study 2: Persistent Productive Cough

Scenario: A 40-year-old female presents with a 3-week history of productive cough, night sweats, and weight loss. Sputum staining reveals red, rod-shaped bacilli.

  • Diagnosis: Secondary pulmonary tuberculosis [30, 31].
  • Mechanism: The rod-shaped organisms are acid-fast bacilli (AFB), confirmed by Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) staining [32, 33].
  • Morphology: Histopathology would likely show Ghon’s complex or apical fibrocaseous lesions with central caseation necrosis [34-36].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Inflammation and list its cardinal signs.
    • Answer: Inflammation is the local response of living tissues to injury [1]. Signs: Redness, swelling, heat, pain, and loss of function [4].
  2. Contrast Healing by First Intention and Second Intention.
    • Answer: First intention is for clean, sutured surgical wounds with minimal tissue loss [37, 38]. Second intention is for large, open, or infected wounds where healing occurs via extensive granulation tissue and wound contraction [27, 38].
  3. Describe the stages of Phagocytosis.
    • Answer: 1. Recognition and Attachment: Microbes are coated with opsonins (e.g., IgG, C3b). 2. Engulfment: Formation of a phagolysosome. 3. Killing and Degradation: Via oxidative (MPO-dependent/independent) or non-oxidative mechanisms [10, 39].
  4. What are the components of a Tuberculous Granuloma?
    • Answer: Central caseation necrosis, surrounded by epithelioid cells, Langhans' giant cells, a rim of lymphocytes, and an outer layer of fibroblasts [34, 40].
  5. Explain the significance of the "Clear Zone" in Leprosy histopathology.
    • Answer: In lepromatous leprosy, a subepidermal "clear zone" separates the lepra cell infiltrate from the epidermis [41, 42]. This is absent in tuberculoid leprosy, where the granuloma erodes the basal layer [43].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A patient with a thermal burn develops blisters within minutes. Which mechanism of increased vascular permeability is primarily responsible?
    • A) Direct endothelial injury
    • B) Contraction of endothelial cells
    • C) Leucocyte-mediated injury
    • D) Neovascularisation
    • Correct Answer: B. This is an immediate transient response (15-30 min) mediated by histamine and bradykinin affecting venules [44, 45].
  2. Which mediator is a potent chemotactic agent for neutrophils derived from the lipo-oxygenase pathway?
    • A) Prostaglandin E2
    • B) Histamine
    • C) Leukotriene B4
    • D) Thromboxane A2
    • Correct Answer: C. LTB4 is a potent chemotactic agent and stimulates phagocytic cell adherence [46, 47].
  3. A 30-year-old male with a history of intravenous drug use is diagnosed with a disease characterized by "non-caseating naked granulomas." What is the most likely diagnosis?
    • A) Tuberculosis
    • B) Leprosy
    • C) Sarcoidosis
    • D) Syphilis
    • Correct Answer: C. Sarcoidosis features non-caseating "naked" granulomas (paucity of lymphocytes) [48, 49].
  4. A "Saddle-shaped nose" deformity is a classic finding in which stage of infection?
    • A) Primary Syphilis
    • B) Secondary Syphilis
    • C) Tertiary Syphilis
    • D) Congenital Syphilis
    • Correct Answer: D. It results from the destruction of the bridge of the nose in congenital syphilis [50, 51].
  5. Which cell type is considered a "Permanent Cell" that cannot regenerate after birth?
    • A) Hepatocyte
    • B) Neuron
    • C) Fibroblast
    • D) Squamous epithelial cell
    • Correct Answer: B. Neurons of the CNS lose their ability to proliferate around birth; injury results in gliosis rather than regeneration [15, 52].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Cognitive Bias: Representativeness Heuristic.
    • Example: Diagnosing every case of granulomatous inflammation as Tuberculosis in an endemic area.
    • Structured Approach: Use AFB staining and culture to confirm M. tuberculosis and rule out atypical mycobacteria, leprosy, or sarcoidosis [53-55].
  • Bias: Confirmation Bias.
    • Example: Only looking for the "Red line" in a suspected Triple Response and ignoring the flare or wheal [56].
    • Structured Approach: Systematically evaluate all components of the response to confirm hemodynamic sequence [56].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Inflammation is a protective but potentially damaging local response to injury [1, 2].
  • Acute inflammation features vascular leakiness and neutrophil recruitment; chronic features mononuclear cells and tissue destruction [3, 4, 57].
  • Chemical mediators like histamine and arachidonic acid metabolites (prostaglandins/leukotrienes) orchestrate the response [58, 59].
  • Granulomatous inflammation is a specific chronic response to poorly digestible agents [20, 22].
  • Healing depends on the regenerative capacity of cells (labile, stable, permanent) and the extent of tissue loss (primary vs. secondary intention) [15, 38, 60].
