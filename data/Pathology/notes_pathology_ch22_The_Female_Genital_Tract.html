Comprehensive Exam-Oriented Notes: Female Genital Tract Pathology

These notes cover Chapter 22 from Harsh Mohan Pathology, focusing on vulva, vagina, cervix, uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries, and placenta. Key mechanisms, clinical correlations, and high-yield facts are emphasized for USMLE, PLAB, and university exams.

Full Chapter Explanation Notes

The chapter details pathology of the female genital tract starting from vulva (ectodermal origin) to ovaries and placenta. Vulva shows Bartholin's cyst/abscess from ductal obstruction, non-neoplastic disorders like lichen sclerosus (atrophic, post-menopausal pruritus, hyperkeratosis, dermal homogenization) and squamous hyperplasia (itchy plaques, acanthosis without atypia). Vagina has vaginitis (Candida, Trichomonas) and rare tumors like sarcoma botryoides (grape-like in children).

Cervix: Chronic cervicitis common, CIN/SIL from HPV (high-risk 16/18 integrate, inactivate p53/Rb via E6/E7), progressing to squamous carcinoma (80%, fungating). Endometrium cycles: proliferative (oestrogen), secretory (progesterone); pathologies include hyperplasia (oestrogen-driven, atypical precancerous), carcinoma (post-menopausal, PTEN mutation).

Ovary tumors: Surface epithelial (60%, serous/mucinous cystadenoma/carcinoma, borderline), germ cell (15%, dermoid cysts benign), sex cord-stromal (5%, granulosa-theca oestrogen-secreting). Placenta: Hydatidiform mole (complete androgenetic 46XX, grape-like vesicles).

Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Vulva: Lichen sclerosus - post-menopausal, kraurosis vulvae; VIN from HPV 16/18, 3-5% genital cancers.
  • Cervix: HPV high-risk integrates → CIN1 (koilocytes, low-risk HPV6/11) → CIN3 (full thickness); Pap smear/Bethesda: L-SIL (CIN1), H-SIL (CIN2/3); FIGO staging key for exams.
  • Endometrium: Hyperplasia types: simple (1% risk), complex atypical (25% → Ca); Endometrial Ca - Type I (hyperplasia, PTEN), Type II (atrophic, p53).
  • Ovary: Serous (bilateral malignant), mucinous (unilateral, large); Dermoid (ectodermal, Rokitansky protuberance); Dysgerminoma = seminoma; AFP in yolk sac, hCG in chorioca.
  • Tubes: PID → pyosalpinx/hydrosalpinx; Ectopic ampullary most common.

Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Common pitfalls: Confusing lichen sclerosus (not premalignant) with VIN (HPV-driven); mistaking Arias-Stella (pregnancy) for clear cell Ca; bilateral mucinous = metastatic (not primary).

  • Reflective questions:
    • Why does CIN start at squamocolumnar junction? (Metaplastic epithelium vulnerable to HPV).
    • Patient with postmenopausal bleeding: Hyperplasia vs Ca? (Atypia on biopsy key).
    • Young girl with abdominal mass: Dermoid vs germ cell malignancy? (AFP/hCG markers).

Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: Vulvar Pruritus (Routine)

65yo postmenopausal woman with intense vulvar itch, white plaques, introital narrowing. Biopsy: epidermal thinning, dermal homogenization.

  • DDx: Lichen sclerosus, squamous hyperplasia, VIN, Ca.
  • Priority: Biopsy rules out VIN/Ca; topical androgens/steroids.
  • Reasoning: Clinical + histo (no atypia) → lichen sclerosus; monitor for progression.

Case 2: Postmenopausal Bleeding (Uncertainty)

58yo obese diabetic with spotting. USG: thickened endometrium. Biopsy: complex hyperplasia with atypia.

  • DDx: Hyperplasia, polyp, endometrial Ca, atrophic.
  • Priority: Hysterectomy (25% risk Ca); progesterone if fertility desire.
  • Uncertainty: PTEN mutation risk; serial biopsy if conservative.

Case 3: Abdominal Mass under Pressure (Emergency)

25yo with acute pain, ruptured ectopic pregnancy suspected but mass found. Ruptured dermoid cyst intra-op discovered.

  • DDx: Ectopic, torsion, cyst rupture, malignancy.
  • Priority: Laparotomy, hemostasis, staging if malignant.
  • Pressure decision: Frozen section if suspicious; benign → cystectomy.

University-Level Exam Questions

  1. MCQ: Most common vulvar lesion in post-menopausal? A) VIN B) Lichen sclerosus C) Bartholin abscess D) SCC B) Lichen sclerosus - Pruritic, atrophic, not premalignant.
  2. Short Answer: Differentiate CIN grades histologically. CIN1: <1/3 thickness; CIN2: 1/3-2/3; CIN3: full thickness atypia, no invasion.
  3. Essay: Pathogenesis of cervical Ca (outline HPV role). Epidemiology (promiscuity), HPV16/18 integration → E6/E7 inactivate p53/Rb → progression LGSIL→HGSIL→invasive; Pap screening reduces incidence.
  4. MCQ: Endometrial hyperplasia with highest Ca risk? A) Simple B) Complex atypical C) Cystic D) Retrogressive B) 20-25% progress to Ca; oestrogen unopposed.
  5. Short Answer: Gross features of serous vs mucinous cystadenoma. Serous: smaller, serous fluid, papillary; Mucinous: larger, gelatinous, multilocular.
  6. MCQ: Ovarian tumor with Call-Exner bodies? A) Dermoid B) Granulosa C) Dysgerminoma D) Yolk sac B) Oestrogen-secreting, microfollicular.
  7. Essay: PID complications in fallopian tubes. Acute: pyosalpinx; Chronic: hydrosalpinx, infertility, ectopic risk; TB: caseating granulomas.

Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive biases: Anchoring (postmenopausal bleed = Ca, miss atrophy); Confirmation (HPV+ = CIN, ignore low-risk); Availability (common PID miss TB in endemic).

  • Avoidance approach: ABCDE (Age, Bleeding history, Colposcopy/Pap, Dilatation curettage, Exam/USG); Structured DDx table; Multidisciplinary input.

Summary & Key Takeaways

  • HPV central to lower tract (vulva/vagina/cervix) Ca; Screen Pap/Bethesda.
  • Oestrogen drives endometrial hyperplasia/Ca; Progesterone opposes.
  • Ovary: Benign young (dermoid), malignant old (epithelial); Markers: AFP (yolk sac), hCG (germ cell/chorioca), inhibin (granulosa).
  • FIGO staging must-know for all.
  • PID/TB → infertility/ectopic; Mole → monitor hCG.

1. Revision

The Vulva and Vagina

The vulva consists of ectodermal structures, making its inflammatory conditions similar to general skin diseases [1]. Key conditions include Bartholin’s Cyst and Abscess, where bacterial infections (notably gonorrhoeal) cause duct obstruction [2]. Chronic inflammation leads to large, often asymptomatic, fluid-filled cysts [3]. Non-neoplastic epithelial disorders, previously called "vulvar dystrophy," include Lichen Sclerosus (thin, shiny skin, common in post-menopausal women) and Squamous Hyperplasia (thickened white lesions) [4-6]. Neoplasms like Condyloma Acuminatum (anogenital warts) are induced by HPV types 6 and 11 and show characteristic koilocytosis [7, 8].

In the vagina, primary diseases are rare [9]. Sarcoma Botryoides is a notable malignant tumor in children under five, presenting as grape-like masses [10]. Histologically, it features a cambium layer of tumor cells lying beneath the epithelium [11].

The Cervix

Cervicitis is common and can be specific (e.g., TB, Syphilis) or nonspecific (mixed vaginal flora), often causing leucorrhoea [12-14]. Squamous Intraepithelial Lesions (SIL/CIN) are precursors to invasive cancer [15].

  • L-SIL (CIN-1): Mild dysplasia, often involving HPV 6 and 11 [16, 17].
  • H-SIL (CIN-2/3): Moderate to severe dysplasia/carcinoma in situ, involving high-risk HPV types 16 and 18 [16-18].
Mechanism: High-risk HPV E6 and E7 proteins inactivate p53 and RB-1 suppressor genes, leading to uncontrolled cell proliferation [19].

Corpus Uteri: Endometrium and Myometrium

Adenomyosis is the presence of benign endometrial tissue within the myometrium, while Endometriosis refers to ectopic endometrial tissue outside the uterus (most commonly the ovaries, forming "chocolate cysts") [20-22]. Endometrial Hyperplasia results from unopposed estrogen; complex atypical hyperplasia carries a 20-25% risk of progressing to adenocarcinoma [23-25]. Leiomyomas (fibroids) are the most common uterine tumors—benign, estrogen-dependent, and characterized by a whorled pattern [26-28].

Ovaries and Fallopian Tubes

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) is an ascending infection often caused by C. trachomatis or N. gonorrhoeae, leading to salpingitis, pyosalpinx, or hydrosalpinx [29-31]. Ovarian tumors are classified by origin: Surface Epithelium (Serous, Mucinous), Germ Cell (Dermoid Cyst, Dysgerminoma), or Sex Cord-Stromal (Granulosa cell) [32-34]. Krukenberg tumor is a bilateral signet-ring cell carcinoma metastatic to the ovaries, usually from the stomach [35].

Gestational Trophoblastic Disease

Hydatidiform Mole is characterized by hydropic villi and trophoblastic proliferation [36, 37]. Complete moles (46,XX) are paternal in origin and have a higher risk of progressing to Choriocarcinoma, a highly malignant but chemotherapy-responsive tumor [38-41].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Koilocytosis: Perinuclear vacuolisation, pathognomonic for HPV infection [8].
  • Schiller’s Test: Areas of the cervix lacking glycogen (cancerous) fail to stain brown with iodine [42].
  • Psammoma Bodies: Concentric calcifications seen in serous cystadenocarcinomas [43].
  • Schiller-Duval Bodies: Structures resembling rat placental endodermal sinuses; diagnostic for Yolk Sac (Endodermal Sinus) Tumors [44].
  • Call-Exner Bodies: Rosette-like structures found in Granulosa Cell Tumors [45, 46].
  • i(12p): Isochromosome 12p is found in 90% of germ cell tumors [47, 48].
  • "Swiss-cheese" Pattern: Gross appearance of cystic glandular hyperplasia [49, 50].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Differentiating White Lesions: Clinicians use "leukoplakia" as a clinical term for white patches, but it can represent anything from benign vitiligo to invasive carcinoma [4]. Pathologists must use specific nomenclature like Lichen Sclerosus to guide treatment (e.g., androgens for Lichen Sclerosus vs. corticosteroids for Squamous Hyperplasia) [6].

The Malignancy Hallmark in Uterine Smooth Muscle: While cellular atypia is noted, the mitotic count is the hallmark for distinguishing Leiomyosarcoma from its benign counterpart. Specifically, >10 mitoses per 10 high-power fields (HPF) is diagnostic [51].

Reflective Question: Why does a Complete Mole carry a higher risk for choriocarcinoma than a Partial Mole? (Consider the 46,XX androgenetic karyotype and the degree of circumferential trophoblastic proliferation) [38, 39, 52].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Post-Menopausal Bleeding (Diagnostic Challenge)

Scenario: A 50-year-old woman presents with foul-smelling vaginal discharge and post-coital spotting for 6 months. She had menopause 5 years ago. Examination reveals an ulcerated, friable cervix that bleeds on contact [53].

Differential Diagnosis: Invasive Cervical Carcinoma, Endometrial Carcinoma extending to cervix, Chronic Severe Cervicitis [53, 54].

Interventions: 1. Colposcopy-directed biopsy (gold standard). 2. Pap smear for cytology. 3. Pelvic imaging to assess stage and spread [55-57].

Case 2: Acute Abdomen (Decision Under Pressure)

Scenario: A 28-year-old woman presents with sudden, sharp lower abdominal pain and fainting. She is pale, tachycardic, and hypotensive (85/50 mmHg). Her last period was 7 weeks ago.

Diagnosis: Ruptured Ectopic Tubal Pregnancy [58].

Reasoning Under Pressure: In a woman of reproductive age with amenorrhoea and shock, ruptured ectopic pregnancy is a surgical emergency due to intraperitoneal hemorrhage [58]. Immediate Action: Laparotomy and fluid resuscitation; do not wait for imaging if the patient is unstable.

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Describe the microscopic features of Lichen Sclerosus.
    Answer: 1. Hyperkeratosis. 2. Epidermal thinning with loss of rete ridges. 3. Dermal collagen degeneration (amorphous). 4. Mid-dermis chronic inflammation [6].
  2. Explain the Bethesda System for reporting cervical cytology.
    Answer: It classifies abnormal squamous cells into L-SIL (low-grade, including CIN-1 and HPV changes) and H-SIL (high-grade, including CIN-2/3 and carcinoma in situ) [16, 59].
  3. Distinguish between Adenomyosis and Endometriosis.
    Answer: Adenomyosis is endometrial tissue within the myometrium; Endometriosis is endometrial tissue outside the uterus (e.g., ovaries, ligaments) [20, 21].
  4. Define the "cambium layer" in Sarcoma Botryoides.
    Answer: Groups of round to fusiform malignant cells lying characteristically immediately underneath the vaginal epithelium [11].
  5. What are the two hallmark morphological features of a Hydatidiform Mole?
    Answer: 1. Enlarged, hydropic chorionic villi. 2. Circumferential trophoblastic proliferation [37, 60].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 4-year-old girl is found to have bulky, polypoid "grape-like" masses protruding from her vagina. Histology shows rhabdomyoblasts and a subepithelial cambium layer. What is the diagnosis?
    A. Squamous Cell Carcinoma
    B. Gartner’s Duct Cyst
    C. Sarcoma Botryoides
    D. Clear Cell Adenocarcinoma
    Correct: C. This is the classic presentation of embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma in children under 5 [10, 11].
  2. A 60-year-old obese female with a history of hypertension presents with postmenopausal bleeding. Biopsy reveals complex glandular crowding with nuclear atypia. What is the most likely risk of progression to invasive cancer?
    A. 1%
    B. 3%
    C. 25%
    D. 70%
    Correct: C. Complex atypical hyperplasia has a 20-25% malignant transformation rate [25].
  3. Which of the following is the most significant risk factor for the development of cervical cancer?
    A. Multiple sexual partners
    B. Early age of sexual activity
    C. High-risk HPV (16, 18) infection
    D. Cigarette smoking
    Correct: C. While all are risk factors, persistent infection with high-risk HPV types is the primary etiological factor [61-63].
  4. A placenta shows hydropic villi and some fetal parts. Karyotype is 69,XXY. What is the diagnosis?
    A. Complete Mole
    B. Partial Mole
    C. Choriocarcinoma
    D. Invasive Mole
    Correct: B. Triploidy (69,XXY/XXX) and the presence of an embryo are diagnostic for a partial mole [39, 64, 65].
  5. Which tumor is characterized by bilateral signet-ring cells and a storiform stroma in the ovary?
    A. Serous Cystadenocarcinoma
    B. Dysgerminoma
    C. Krukenberg Tumor
    D. Granulosa Cell Tumor
    Correct: C. This is a metastatic tumor, typically from the stomach [35, 66].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Availability Bias: Clinicians may attribute post-menopausal bleeding solely to "senile atrophy" because it is common. Solution: Always maintain a high index of suspicion for endometrial carcinoma and perform an endometrial biopsy to avoid "premature closure" of the diagnostic process [67-69].

Confirmation Bias: Attributing acute pelvic pain solely to PID. Solution: In any woman of reproductive age, always rule out ectopic pregnancy with a beta-hCG test and ultrasound before assuming an inflammatory cause [58].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Cervix: HPV 16/18 integrates into the genome; Pap smears detect precancerous SIL [16, 19, 70].
  • Uterus: Fibroids are common, benign, and whorled [26, 28]. Unopposed estrogen drives hyperplasia and endometrioid cancer [24, 69, 71].
  • Ovary: Surface epithelial tumors are most common; Germ cell tumors (Dermoids) are common in young women [32, 48].
  • Placenta: Moles are non-invasive; Choriocarcinoma is highly malignant but highly curable [38, 40, 41].
  • Emergency: Sudden amenorrhoea + shock = Ruptured ectopic pregnancy until proven otherwise [58].
