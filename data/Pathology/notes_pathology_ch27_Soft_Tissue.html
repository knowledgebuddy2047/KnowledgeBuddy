Chapter 27: Soft Tissue Tumours

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Introduction and Scope

Soft tissue tumours are defined as non-epithelial extra-skeletal tissues, excluding the reticuloendothelial system, glia, and organ-specific supporting tissues [1, 2]. These tissues include fibrous, adipose, muscle, synovial, vascular, and neuroectodermal elements [1]. While most are mesodermal, peripheral nerves are ectodermal [3]. Benign soft tissue tumours are approximately 100 times more common than sarcomas [3].

General Features of Malignancy

  • Location: Deep-seated lesions are more likely to be malignant than superficial ones [3].
  • Size & Growth: Large size and rapid growth are indicators of malignancy [3].
  • Vascularity: Malignant tumours frequently show increased vascularity, while benign ones are selectively avascular [3].
  • Metastasis: Dissemination is primarily haematogenous, most commonly to the lungs and liver; lymph node involvement is usually late [3].

Diagnostic Framework

Diagnosis is primarily based on histogenesis determined by cell patterns and types [4, 5]:

  • Cell Patterns:
    • Herringbone pattern: Characteristic of fibrosarcoma [4, 6].
    • Storiform/Cart-wheel pattern: Spindle cells radiating from a center; seen in fibrohistiocytic tumours [4, 7].
    • Biphasic pattern: Combination of epithelial-like and spindle cells, seen in synovial sarcoma [4, 8].
  • Key Histochemical Markers (IHC): Vimentin (mesenchymal marker), SMA (smooth muscle), Desmin (skeletal muscle), S-100 (nerve fibres), and Factor VIII (vascular endothelium) [9].

Grading and Staging

Grading is based on cytologic atypia, mitotic count, and tumour necrosis [10]. Staging typically uses Enneking's (Grade/Location) or AJC (TNM/Grade) systems [2, 11].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Lipoblasts: The hallmark cell for diagnosing liposarcoma; they may be univacuolated or multivacuolated [12, 13].
  • Chicken-wire pattern: Conspicuous meshwork of capillaries characteristic of myxoid liposarcoma [12].
  • Cambium Layer: A distinctive subepithelial layer of tumour cells found in botryoid rhabdomyosarcoma [14, 15].
  • Rhabdomyosarcoma: The commonest soft tissue sarcoma in children and young adults [16, 17].
  • Myositis Ossificans: Known as a "pseudomalignant" tumour because it mimics osteosarcoma but shows characteristic peripheral maturation into woven bone [18].
  • Genetic Markers: t(11;22) for Ewing’s sarcoma (EWSR1-FLI1) and t(X;18) for synovial sarcoma (SS18-SSX1) [19, 20].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Misidentifying Myositis Ossificans as Osteosarcoma.
Reasoning: Myositis ossificans shows a "zoning" phenomenon where the most mature bone is at the periphery, whereas osteosarcoma has haphazard malignant osteoid production throughout [18].

Assumption in Fibromatosis: These are "intermediate" tumours.
Reasoning: While desmoid tumours (deep fibromatosis) appear histologically bland, they are locally aggressive and highly recurrent, even though they do not metastasize [21-23].

Reflective Question: Why is the "Storiform pattern" significant in distinguishing fibrohistiocytic tumours from simple fibrosarcomas? [4, 7, 24]

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Enlarging Thigh Mass

Scenario: A 55-year-old male presents with a large (8 cm), painless, deep-seated mass in the right thigh that has been growing for 3 months. MRI shows an infiltrative mass within the skeletal muscle [25, 26].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Malignant Fibrous Histiocytoma (MFH/Pleomorphic Sarcoma), Liposarcoma, Fibrosarcoma [25, 27, 28].
  • Prioritizing Interventions: 1. Core needle biopsy for histology and IHC. 2. Staging scans (Chest CT) to check for haematogenous spread to lungs [3, 29].
  • Reasoning: In adults, MFH is the most common soft tissue sarcoma and often presents as a deep, enlarging mass in the extremities [25].

Case 2: "Grapes" in the Vagina

Scenario: A 4-year-old girl is brought in due to a "grape-like" gelatinous mass protruding from her vagina [14, 30].

  • Diagnosis: Botryoid Rhabdomyosarcoma [17, 30].
  • Histological Key: Look for the cambium layer (hypercellular zone under the epithelium) and rhabdomyoblasts [14, 15].
  • Management: Highly malignant; requires aggressive multi-modal therapy [16].

Case 3: Decision Under Pressure (Post-Traumatic Swelling)

Scenario: An athlete presents with a firm, painful mass in the thigh following a single severe injury. X-ray shows a gritty mass with a radiolucent center and a calcified rim [18, 31].

  • Critical Decision: Do not rush to diagnose Osteosarcoma.
  • Action: Review the X-ray for peripheral maturation and correlate with the history of trauma [18, 31].
  • Reasoning: Surgery for a "pseudomalignant" Myositis Ossificans is curative, but mistaking it for sarcoma could lead to unnecessary radical amputation [18].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Soft Tissue Tumours and list their primary tissues of origin.
    Answer: Non-epithelial extra-skeletal tissues including fibrous, adipose, muscle, synovial, vascular, and peripheral/autonomic neuroectodermal tissues [1].
  2. Distinguish between Keloid and Hypertrophic Scar.
    Answer: Keloids are progressive, recurrent overgrowths with thick hyalinised collagen bands; hypertrophic scars are more cellular, have thinner collagen, and generally don't recur after excision [32].
  3. Describe the "Biphasic Pattern" in Synovial Sarcoma.
    Answer: It consists of cleft/gland-like structures lined by epithelial-like cells and interlacing fascicles of spindle cells [4, 8].
  4. Short Essay: Pathogenesis and morphology of Desmoid Tumours.
    Answer: Deep-seated fibromatoses often linked to trauma, oestrogen (pregnancy), or Gardner's syndrome [22, 33]. Grossly firm/whorled; microscopically bland fibroblasts in fascicles but locally aggressive and infiltrative [23].
  5. What are the three features used for pathological grading of sarcomas?
    Answer: 1. Tumour differentiation (cytologic atypia), 2. Mitotic count, and 3. Tumour necrosis [10].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 10-year-old boy presents with a mass in his arm. Biopsy reveals t(11;22) and the presence of small round blue cells. Which gene fusion is most likely?
    A) PAX3-FOXO1A
    B) EWSR1-FLI1
    C) SS18-SSX1
    D) FUS-DDIT3
    Answer: B. This is diagnostic of Ewing’s sarcoma/PNET [19, 20].
  2. A 60-year-old male has a deep-seated thigh mass. Microscopy shows spindle cells in a "Herring-bone" pattern. What is the most likely diagnosis?
    A) Liposarcoma
    B) Synovial Sarcoma
    C) Fibrosarcoma
    D) Rhabdomyosarcoma
    Answer: C. The Herring-bone pattern is the classic descriptor for fibrosarcoma [4, 6].
  3. A 45-year-old woman presents with a soft, movable, painless subcutaneous mass on her back. Histology shows mature adipocytes with a thin fibrous capsule. The most likely diagnosis is:
    A) Lipoma
    B) Myxoid Liposarcoma
    C) Hibernoma
    D) Angiolipoma
    Answer: A. Lipoma is the commonest soft tissue tumour, often solitary, subcutaneous, and composed of mature fat [34, 35].
  4. Immunohistochemistry of a spindle cell tumour shows positivity for Desmin. Which tissue of origin does this suggest?
    A) Adipose
    B) Nerve
    C) Skeletal Muscle
    D) Vascular Endothelium
    Answer: C. Desmin is a specific marker for skeletal muscle cells [9].
  5. Which variant of liposarcoma is characterized by a "chicken-wire" capillary pattern and t(12;16)?
    A) Well-differentiated
    B) Myxoid
    C) Round Cell
    D) Pleomorphic
    Answer: B. Both the "chicken-wire" pattern and the t(12;16) translocation are specific to myxoid liposarcoma [12, 36].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Cognitive Bias (Search Satisfaction): Attributing a mass in a young athlete solely to trauma.
    Strategy: Always consider that trauma often merely brings a pre-existing tumour to the patient's attention [37, 38]. Perform imaging to ensure the lesion isn't a sarcoma.
  • Anchoring Bias: Relying only on histological "blandness" to predict clinical behavior.
    Strategy: Remember that Intermediate (locally aggressive) tumours like desmoids look benign but act aggressively. Base treatment on the WHO classification category rather than just cytologic appearance [21, 23].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • General Rule: Deep, large, and fast-growing soft tissue masses are sarcomas until proven otherwise [3, 38].
  • Fibrous: Fibrosarcoma (Herringbone); Nodular Fasciitis (Reactive/Pseudo-sarcomatous) [6, 39].
  • Adipose: Liposarcoma hallmark is the lipoblast [12, 40].
  • Muscle: Rhabdomyosarcoma is the leading pediatric soft tissue sarcoma [16, 17].
  • Synovial Sarcoma: Characterized by a biphasic pattern and t(X;18) [8, 41].
  • IHC: Essential for distinguishing mesenchymal tumours; Vimentin is the broad marker [9].
  • Metastasis: Primarily haematogenous to the lungs [3].

Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Common pitfalls: mistaking reactive (nodular fasciitis, myositis ossificans) or fibromatosis for sarcoma due to mitoses/atypia; assuming all lipomas benign (atypical lipomatous = well-diff liposarcoma). Deep location biases towards malignancy but superficial sarcomas exist (e.g., DFSP). Assumption: trauma causes tumours (brings attention, not causal).
