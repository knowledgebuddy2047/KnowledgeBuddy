Chapter 25: The Endocrine System – Comprehensive Exam Notes

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Basic Concepts & Neuroendocrine System

  • Neuroendocrine System: Forms a link between endocrine glands and the nervous system [1]. It is often called the APUD cell system (Amine Precursor Uptake and Decarboxylation) or dispersed neuroendocrine system [1].
  • Hormone Classification: Group I (Cell-surface receptors) includes amino acid derivatives and neuropeptides; Group II (Intracellular/Nuclear receptors) includes large proteins, steroids, and vitamin derivatives [2].
  • Pathologic Processes: Endocrine abnormalities arise from Hyperfunction (adenomas, hyperplasia), Hypofunction (destruction, atrophy, genetic defects), or Peripheral Hormone Resistance [3, 4].

Pituitary Gland (Hypophysis)

  • Anatomy: Situated in the sella turcica [5]. Anterior lobe (Adenohypophysis) is an ectodermal derivative of Rathke's pouch; Posterior lobe (Neurohypophysis) is a neural tissue downgrowth [5, 6].
  • Adenohypophysis Cells: Somatotrophs (GH), Lactotrophs (PRL), Thyrotrophs (TSH), Gonadotrophs (FSH/LH), and Corticotrophs (ACTH/MSH) [7].
  • Neurohypophysis: Stores ADH (Vasopressin) for water reabsorption and Oxytocin for milk ejection and uterine contraction [8].

Adrenal Gland

  • Adrenal Cortex Layers:
    • Zona Glomerulosa: Synthesis of Mineralocorticoids (Aldosterone) [9].
    • Zona Fasciculata: Synthesis of Glucocorticoids (Cortisol) [9].
    • Zona Reticularis: Synthesis of Androgens [10].
  • Adrenal Medulla: Part of the dispersed neuroendocrine system; secretes catecholamines (epinephrine/norepinephrine) [11].

Thyroid Gland

  • Synthesis: Involves iodine trapping, oxidation (peroxidase), iodination of tyrosine, and coupling to form T3 and T4 [12].
  • Thyroiditis: Includes Hashimoto’s (autoimmune destruction with Hurthle cells) [13, 14] and de Quervain’s (granulomatous/viral, self-limiting) [15, 16].
  • Malignancy: Papillary Carcinoma is most common (ground-glass nuclei, psammoma bodies) [17, 18]; Medullary Carcinoma originates from C-cells and secretes calcitonin [19].

Endocrine Pancreas & Diabetes

  • Islet Cells: Beta cells (70%) secrete Insulin; Alpha cells (20%) secrete Glucagon; Delta cells (5-10%) secrete Somatostatin [20].
  • Diabetes Mellitus (DM): Chronic hyperglycaemia due to insulin deficiency (Type 1) or resistance (Type 2) [21].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Sheehan’s Syndrome: Postpartum ischaemic necrosis of the anterior pituitary due to hypotensive shock [22].
  • Gigantism vs. Acromegaly: Gigantism occurs before epiphyseal closure; Acromegaly occurs after cessation of bone growth [23, 24].
  • Pheochromocytoma "10% Rule": 10% familial, 10% malignant, 10% extra-adrenal [25].
  • Auspitz Sign? No: In Thyroid pathology, focus on Psammoma bodies in Papillary Carcinoma and Amyloid stroma in Medullary Carcinoma [18, 26].
  • Waterhouse-Friderichsen Syndrome: Acute primary adrenal insufficiency caused by septicaemia and haemorrhagic necrosis [27].
  • Insulitis: Lymphocytic infiltrate in islets, characteristic of early Type 1 DM [28, 29].
  • HbA1C: Monitors glycaemic control over the preceding 90–120 days [30, 31].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

  • Mechanism of Resistance: In Type 2 DM, hyperglycaemia is not due to beta-cell destruction but a failure of cells to respond to insulin (insulin resistance) combined with impaired secretion [32, 33].
  • Pitfall: Mistaking Secondary Hyperparathyroidism for primary. Primary hyperparathyroidism presents with hypercalcaemia, while secondary (often due to renal failure) presents with hypocalcaemia [34, 35].
  • Reflective Question: Why does Hashimoto’s Thyroiditis sometimes present with alternate episodes of hyperthyroidism (Hashitoxicosis)? (Consider the role of inhibitory vs. stimulatory TSH-receptor antibodies) [36, 37].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Protruding Jaw

Scenario: A 45-year-old male presents with increasing size of hands and feet, coarsening facial features, and "prognathism" (protruding lower jaw) [24]. He also complains of visual field defects.

  • Differential Diagnosis: Somatotroph (GH-secreting) Adenoma (Acromegaly), Paget's disease of bone [23, 24].
  • Reasoning: Adult onset and characteristic "acral" growth point to Acromegaly. Visual defects suggest a large macroadenoma (>1cm) compressing the optic chiasma [24, 38].
  • Intervention: IGF-1 levels, Glucose suppression test for GH, and Pituitary MRI [23].

Case 2: Sudden Hypotensive Shock (Decision Under Pressure)

Scenario: A patient with known meningococcal septicaemia suddenly develops profound hypotension, petechial rashes, and signs of acute circulatory collapse [27].

  • Emergency Diagnosis: Waterhouse-Friderichsen’s Syndrome (Acute adrenal crisis) [27].
  • Decision Under Pressure: This is a medical emergency. Immediate administration of intravenous steroids and fluids is required even before waiting for cortisol levels, as the condition is fatal if untreated [27, 39].
  • Mechanism: Grossly haemorrhagic and necrotic adrenal cortex (adrenal apoplexy) leads to total mineralocorticoid and glucocorticoid deficiency [27].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

1. Describe the morphological features of Sheehan’s Syndrome.
Answer: Early stages show ischaemic necrosis and haemorrhage in the anterior pituitary; later, necrotic tissue is replaced by fibrous tissue [22, 40].

2. Distinguish between Type 1 and Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus regarding pathogenesis.
Answer: Type 1 is immune-mediated destruction of beta-cells leading to absolute insulin deficiency. Type 2 involves insulin resistance and impaired insulin secretion [41-43].

3. Short Note: Conn’s Syndrome.
Answer: Primary hyperaldosteronism caused by an adrenal adenoma or hyperplasia. Characterised by hypertension, hypokalaemia, and low plasma renin levels [44-46].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 30-year-old woman presents with weight loss, heat intolerance, and "exophthalmos." Serum TSH is low, T3/T4 are high. What is the most likely diagnosis?
    • A) Hashimoto's Thyroiditis
    • B) Graves' Disease
    • C) de Quervain's Thyroiditis
    • D) Riedel's Thyroiditis
    Correct: B. Exophthalmos is a hallmark feature of Graves’ Disease (Diffuse toxic goitre) [47, 48].
  2. A 5-year-old child presents with a large abdominal mass. Urine tests show elevated VMA and HVA. Histology reveals Homer-Wright rosettes. What is the diagnosis?
    • A) Pheochromocytoma
    • B) Renal Cell Carcinoma
    • C) Neuroblastoma
    • D) Nephroblastoma
    Correct: C. Neuroblastoma is common in children under 5, involves abdominal masses, and shows pseudorosettes and catecholamine metabolites (VMA/HVA) in urine [49-51].
  3. Which histological feature is pathognomonic for Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma?
    • A) Amyloid stroma
    • B) Hurthle cells
    • C) Ground-glass (Orphan Annie eye) nuclei
    • D) Small, round blue cells
    Correct: C. Optically clear or ground-glass nuclei are characteristic of Papillary Carcinoma [18, 52].
  4. A patient with medullary thyroid carcinoma is found to also have a pheochromocytoma and parathyroid hyperplasia. Which MEN syndrome is this?
    • A) MEN 1
    • B) MEN 2A
    • C) MEN 2B
    • D) MEN 3
    Correct: B. MEN 2A (Sipple’s syndrome) includes the triad of medullary thyroid cancer, pheochromocytoma, and hyperparathyroidism [53].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

  • Availability Bias: A clinician may diagnose "simple obesity" in a patient with truncal fat and moon face.
    Approach: Consider Cushing’s Syndrome; look for purple striae and test for hyperglycaemia or hypertension, as truncal obesity is a classic feature [54].
  • Search Satisfaction: Finding a thyroid nodule and assuming it is a benign goitre.
    Approach: Use FNAC (Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology) routinely to rule out occult malignancies like Papillary Carcinoma, which often presents as an asymptomatic solitary nodule [17, 55].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Pituitary: Adenohypophysis (Rathke’s pouch); Neurohypophysis stores ADH/Oxytocin [5, 8].
  • Cushing's: Excessive cortisol; truncal obesity, moon-face, hypertension [44, 54].
  • Addison's: Chronic primary adrenal insufficiency; hyperpigmentation, hypotension [39, 56].
  • Thyroid Cancer: Papillary (commonest, psammoma bodies); Medullary (C-cells, amyloid) [17, 26, 57].
  • Diabetes: Type 1 (Autoimmune, ketoacidosis risk); Type 2 (Insulin resistance, obesity linked) [41-43].
  • MEN 1: 3 Ps - Parathyroid, Pancreatic islets, Pituitary [58].

Chapter 25: The Endocrine System

These notes convert the chapter into structured, exam-focused revision material. They cover core mechanisms, pathology patterns, clinicopathologic reasoning, high-yield facts, cases, and exam-style questions for university, USMLE, and PLAB preparation.

Full Chapter Explanation Notes

1. Basic endocrine framework

  • The endocrine system and nervous system jointly regulate growth, differentiation, homeostasis, and reproduction.
  • The neuroendocrine system links neural and endocrine signaling; classic dispersed neuroendocrine cells include GI neuroendocrine cells, adrenal medulla, thyroid C cells, pancreatic islets, and atrial natriuretic peptide-secreting atrial cells.
  • Major endocrine organs in this chapter: pituitary, adrenals, thyroid, parathyroids, gonads, pancreatic islets.
  • Broad pathologic patterns repeat across endocrine organs: hyperfunction, hypofunction, and hormone resistance.
  • Hyperfunction commonly results from hyperplasia, adenoma/carcinoma, ectopic hormone production, autoimmune stimulation, or iatrogenic causes.
  • Hypofunction commonly results from autoimmune destruction, infection, surgery/radiation, infarction/hemorrhage, developmental defects, enzyme defects, or nutritional deficiency.
  • Feedback loops are essential: most endocrine disorders can be reasoned through by asking whether the problem is primary gland disease, pituitary/hypothalamic disease, or peripheral resistance.

2. Pituitary gland

  • Anterior pituitary (adenohypophysis) arises from Rathke pouch; posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis) is neural in origin.
  • Anterior pituitary cells: somatotrophs (GH), lactotrophs (PRL), corticotrophs (ACTH), thyrotrophs (TSH), gonadotrophs (FSH/LH).
  • Posterior pituitary stores hypothalamic hormones: ADH and oxytocin.
  • Hyperpituitarism is usually due to functioning adenoma. Key syndromes: gigantism/acromegaly, hyperprolactinemia, pituitary Cushing disease.
  • Gigantism = GH excess before epiphyseal closure, causing proportionate overgrowth. Acromegaly = GH excess after closure, causing enlarged hands/feet, prognathism, coarse facies, macroglossia, skin thickening.
  • Hyperprolactinemia causes amenorrhea-galactorrhea and infertility in women; reduced libido/impotence in men.
  • SIADH-like state from inappropriate ADH release produces concentrated urine, hyponatremia, and fluid expansion; classic association is paraneoplastic small-cell carcinoma.
  • Hypopituitarism usually appears only after destruction of >75% anterior pituitary, showing the gland’s large reserve.
  • Sheehan syndrome = postpartum ischemic necrosis of enlarged pituitary after obstetric shock/hemorrhage; first clue is failure of lactation.
  • Pituitary dwarfism = childhood GH deficiency with proportionate short stature and normal intelligence, unlike cretinism.
  • Diabetes insipidus = ADH deficiency leading to massive dilute urine, polydipsia, and low urine specific gravity.
  • Pituitary adenomas are classified functionally: prolactinoma is most common; others include somatotroph, corticotroph, gonadotroph, thyrotroph, mixed, null cell, and plurihormonal adenomas.
  • Pressure effects of macroadenoma: headache, visual field defects from optic chiasm compression, cranial nerve dysfunction, hypopituitarism.
  • Craniopharyngioma arises from Rathke pouch remnants, often cystic with “machinery oil” fluid and histologic resemblance to ameloblastoma.

3. Adrenal gland

  • Cortex has 3 layers: zona glomerulosa (aldosterone), zona fasciculata (cortisol and some sex steroids), zona reticularis (glucocorticoids and androgens).
  • Medulla is neuroendocrine tissue producing catecholamines; metabolites include metanephrine, normetanephrine, VMA, HVA.
  • Cushing syndrome = chronic hypercortisolism from pituitary ACTH excess, adrenal tumor/hyperplasia, ectopic ACTH, or iatrogenic steroids.
  • Pituitary Cushing disease shows high ACTH, bilateral adrenal hyperplasia, and suppression with high-dose dexamethasone.
  • Adrenal Cushing syndrome shows low ACTH and no suppression with dexamethasone.
  • Ectopic ACTH syndrome classically comes from small-cell lung carcinoma and also does not suppress with dexamethasone.
  • Clinical Cushing features: central obesity, moon facies, buffalo hump, skin thinning, purple striae, muscle wasting, osteoporosis, hypertension, glucose intolerance, menstrual disturbance, psychiatric symptoms.
  • Conn syndrome = primary hyperaldosteronism, usually adenoma or bilateral hyperplasia, causing hypertension, hypokalemia, weakness, polyuria, and low plasma renin.
  • Adrenogenital syndrome = excess adrenal androgens; causes virilization in females, precocious puberty in boys, genital ambiguity in girls.
  • Addison disease = chronic primary adrenal insufficiency after destruction of >90% cortex. Causes include autoimmune adrenalitis and tuberculosis.
  • Addison features: weakness, weight loss, hypotension, hyperpigmentation, GI symptoms, low sodium, high potassium, acidosis, hypoglycemia.
  • Secondary adrenal insufficiency due to ACTH deficiency differs by absence of hyperpigmentation and relatively preserved aldosterone.
  • Acute adrenal crisis can follow bilateral adrenalectomy, septicemia (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome), sudden steroid withdrawal, or stress in chronic insufficiency.
  • Adrenal cortical adenoma is usually small, yellow, benign, often nonfunctional; carcinoma is larger, invasive, and often functional.
  • Pheochromocytoma is a catecholamine-producing medullary tumor. Think episodic or sustained hypertension, headache, palpitations, sweating; diagnose with 24-hour urinary catecholamine metabolites.
  • Histology of pheochromocytoma: zellballen nesting pattern and chromogranin positivity.
  • Neuroblastoma is a malignant neural crest tumor in young children, often adrenal, with Homer-Wright pseudorosettes and elevated urinary VMA/HVA.
  • Favorable neuroblastoma prognosis: age <2 years, extra-abdominal location, schwannian/ganglionic differentiation, lower stage, lack of N-MYC amplification.

4. Thyroid gland

  • Thyroid follicles synthesize T3 and T4 via iodine trapping, oxidation, iodination, coupling, endocytosis of colloid, and proteolysis of thyroglobulin.
  • Functional histology matters: resting follicles are large and colloid-rich; hyperactive follicles are lined by tall cells with scalloped colloid.
  • Useful tests: serum T3/T4, TSH, TRH, thyroglobulin, calcitonin, radioactive iodine uptake, and hot vs cold nodule assessment.
  • Hyperthyroidism = hypermetabolic state due to excess thyroid hormone. Major causes: Graves disease, toxic multinodular goiter, toxic adenoma.
  • Symptoms: nervousness, weight loss despite appetite, heat intolerance, tremor, tachycardia, cardiomegaly, warm moist skin; in Graves disease, exophthalmos is important.
  • Thyroid storm is life-threatening decompensated thyrotoxicosis with fever, arrhythmia, and coma, often triggered by surgery, stress, trauma, or infection.
  • Hypothyroidism presents as cretinism in infancy/childhood and myxedema in adults.
  • Cretinism causes severe intellectual and physical developmental delay if untreated early. Causes include thyroid dysgenesis, dyshormonogenesis, fetal drug/iodide exposure, and iodine deficiency.
  • Myxedema presents with cold intolerance, lethargy, constipation, puffiness, hair loss, slow speech, and nonpitting edema due to mucopolysaccharide accumulation.
  • Hashimoto thyroiditis is autoimmune chronic lymphocytic thyroiditis: diffuse goiter, lymphoid infiltration with germinal centers, Hurthle cell change, hypothyroidism, and autoantibodies.
  • Hashimoto may rarely transiently produce hyperthyroid symptoms (hashitoxicosis) and is associated with increased risk of thyroid lymphoma, not a clear increase in carcinoma.
  • Subacute granulomatous (de Quervain) thyroiditis is painful, often post-viral, self-limited, and shows giant-cell granulomatous reaction to colloid.
  • Riedel thyroiditis causes a stony hard thyroid with fibrosis extending into adjacent tissues, mimicking malignancy and causing compressive symptoms.
  • Graves disease is autoimmune hyperthyroidism with diffuse goiter and ophthalmopathy. TSI stimulates TSH receptor; TGI drives follicular growth.
  • Graves histology: diffuse hyperplasia, papillary infoldings, tall follicular epithelium, scant watery scalloped colloid, vascular stroma with lymphocytes.
  • Goiter is compensatory enlargement due to hormone deficiency, usually iodine deficiency. Repeated hyperplasia-involution cycles cause multinodular goiter.
  • Important neoplasms: follicular adenoma; carcinomas—papillary (most common, best prognosis), follicular, medullary, anaplastic.

5. Parathyroid and pancreatic islets

  • Parathyroid chief cells secrete PTH, which increases serum calcium and decreases serum phosphate.
  • PTH acts on bone (increases resorption), kidney (increases calcium reabsorption, decreases phosphate reabsorption), and indirectly gut (via active vitamin D).
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism results from parathyroid adenoma/hyperplasia and classically causes hypercalcemia.
  • The chapter also emphasizes diabetes mellitus under endocrine pathology. Type 1 DM is autoimmune β-cell destruction; type 2 DM is a multifactorial disorder dominated by insulin resistance and impaired secretion.
  • Insulin synthesis: preproinsulin → proinsulin → insulin + C-peptide. C-peptide helps distinguish endogenous from exogenous insulin.
  • Acute diabetic emergencies: DKA, hyperosmolar nonketotic coma, hypoglycemia. Late complications: atherosclerosis, microangiopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy, retinopathy, infections.
  • Islet cell tumors include insulinoma and gastrinoma; gastrinoma is associated with Zollinger-Ellison syndrome and may occur in MEN1.

6. MEN and polyglandular autoimmune syndromes

  • MEN1 (Wermer): parathyroid, pancreatic islets, pituitary; mutation in MEN1/menin.
  • MEN2A: medullary thyroid carcinoma, pheochromocytoma, hyperparathyroidism; RET mutation.
  • MEN2B: medullary thyroid carcinoma, pheochromocytoma, mucosal neuromas, intestinal ganglioneuromatosis, marfanoid habitus; RET mutation.
  • PGA type I: candidiasis, hypoparathyroidism, adrenal insufficiency. PGA type II: adrenal insufficiency + autoimmune thyroiditis + type 1 DM.

Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

Feedback logic

  • High target-gland hormone with low trophic hormone suggests primary gland disease.
  • Low target hormone with high trophic hormone suggests primary gland failure.
  • Low target hormone with low trophic hormone suggests pituitary/hypothalamic disease.

Hyperplasia vs adenoma

  • Diffuse or bilateral enlargement often reflects trophic stimulation.
  • Discrete benign mass suggests adenoma.
  • Autonomous hormone secretion usually suppresses upstream trophic hormones.

Autoimmunity pattern

  • Hashimoto destroys gland → hypothyroidism.
  • Graves stimulates TSH receptor → hyperthyroidism.
  • Autoimmune diseases often cluster: type 1 DM, Addison disease, thyroid disease.

Exam pearls

  • Failure of lactation after postpartum hemorrhage = Sheehan syndrome.
  • Hyperpigmentation points toward primary adrenal insufficiency, not secondary.
  • Painful thyroid = think de Quervain; painless goitrous hypothyroidism = think Hashimoto.

Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Common pitfalls

  • Pitfall 1: Equating all thyroid enlargement with hyperthyroidism. Many goiters are euthyroid or hypothyroid.
  • Pitfall 2: Missing secondary causes because only the end-organ hormone is considered. Always pair end-organ hormone with trophic hormone.
  • Pitfall 3: Assuming hypotension with fatigue is simple dehydration; hyperpigmentation and hyperkalemia should trigger suspicion of Addison disease.
  • Pitfall 4: Overcalling pheochromocytoma from any hypertension. Think of catecholamine symptoms, episodic surges, and biochemical confirmation first.
  • Pitfall 5: Missing medication history—steroids, dopamine antagonists, iodides, and antithyroid drugs can create endocrine syndromes.

Bias recognition

  • Anchoring bias: fixing on one diagnosis early, e.g., obesity + hypertension = essential hypertension, while ignoring striae and proximal myopathy suggesting Cushing syndrome.
  • Availability bias: thinking every tachycardic anxious patient has panic disorder; tremor, weight loss, and heat intolerance should reopen thyroid testing.
  • Premature closure: diagnosing postpartum fatigue as “normal post-delivery exhaustion,” missing Sheehan syndrome when lactation fails.

Structured antidotes

  1. Define the syndrome first: hyperfunction, hypofunction, mass effect, or metabolic emergency.
  2. Localize the lesion: target gland, pituitary/hypothalamus, ectopic source, or receptor resistance.
  3. Use paired labs: hormone + regulator.
  4. Search for signature clues: pigmentation, ophthalmopathy, painful gland, episodic symptoms, postpartum context.
  5. Review drugs, malignancy clues, autoimmune associations, and family history.
Reflective questions
What findings make you localize disease to the pituitary rather than the target gland? Which endocrine emergencies kill early because of electrolyte or cardiovascular instability? When does a “normal” postpartum complaint become pathologic?

Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: Postpartum collapse and failure to lactate

A 26-year-old woman had severe postpartum hemorrhage. Two weeks later she reports inability to breastfeed, fatigue, amenorrhea, and dizziness.

  • Step 1: syndrome recognition — postpartum endocrine hypofunction.
  • Step 2: differential diagnosis — Sheehan syndrome, postpartum thyroiditis, severe anemia, depression, chronic infection.
  • Step 3: highest-yield clue — failure of lactation strongly suggests prolactin deficiency due to pituitary ischemic necrosis.
  • Step 4: mechanism — pregnancy enlarges pituitary; shock causes ischemic necrosis of anterior pituitary.
  • Step 5: immediate priorities — assess hemodynamic status, electrolytes, cortisol deficiency risk, and thyroid status before long-term hormone replacement.
  • Likely diagnosis — Sheehan syndrome.
  • Reasoning under uncertainty — if only fatigue were present, this could be nonspecific; lactation failure plus postpartum hemorrhage sharply narrows localization.

Case 2: Painful thyroid after viral illness

A 35-year-old woman develops fever, neck pain radiating to the jaw, and tender thyroid enlargement after an upper respiratory infection. She has transient palpitations and sweating.

  • Differential diagnosis — de Quervain thyroiditis, Graves disease, suppurative thyroiditis, hemorrhage into thyroid nodule.
  • Most useful discriminator — painful tender thyroid strongly favors subacute granulomatous thyroiditis.
  • Mechanism — destructive inflammation releases preformed hormone, causing transient hyperthyroid phase.
  • Expected course — often self-limited, with later recovery and possible temporary hypothyroid phase.
  • Intervention priority — symptom control, monitoring thyroid function, avoid overdiagnosing primary hyperthyroid states that require very different treatment.

Case 3: Decision-making under pressure — severe hypertension in theatre

A 42-year-old patient scheduled for abdominal surgery suddenly develops extreme hypertension, tachycardia, sweating, and arrhythmia during induction of anesthesia.

  • Immediate differential diagnosis — pheochromocytoma crisis, anesthetic complication, thyroid storm, panic/autonomic surge, drug reaction.
  • Clues favoring pheochromocytoma — explosive catecholamine pattern, cardiovascular instability, possible occult adrenal medullary tumor.
  • Prioritizing interventions — stabilize airway/breathing/circulation, control hypertensive crisis, stop provoking triggers, then confirm biochemically with catecholamine metabolites once stabilized.
  • Bias trap — attributing the event purely to “operative stress” and missing a lethal endocrine tumor.
  • Clinical lesson — in unstable patients, treat physiology first, but keep endocrine catastrophe in the active differential.

University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Short note: Sheehan syndrome.

    Answer: Sheehan syndrome is postpartum ischemic necrosis of the anterior pituitary, usually after severe obstetric hemorrhage or hypotensive shock. The enlarged pregnancy pituitary is vulnerable to ischemia. Earliest clue is failure of lactation due to prolactin deficiency, followed by amenorrhea, loss of axillary/pubic hair, hypothyroidism, and adrenal insufficiency. Morphology shows early ischemic necrosis and hemorrhage, later fibrosis.

  2. Essay: Etiopathogenesis and clinical features of Cushing syndrome.

    Answer: Cushing syndrome means chronic hypercortisolism. Causes are pituitary ACTH excess (Cushing disease), adrenal adenoma/carcinoma/hyperplasia, ectopic ACTH production, and iatrogenic steroid therapy. Clinical features include central obesity, moon facies, buffalo hump, purple striae, skin thinning, muscle wasting, osteoporosis, hypertension, diabetes/glucose intolerance, menstrual changes, and psychiatric symptoms. Localization depends on ACTH levels and dexamethasone suppression response.

  3. MCQ: A woman has painless diffuse goiter, hypothyroidism, anti-thyroid antibodies, lymphoid follicles with germinal centers, and Hurthle cells. Diagnosis?

    Answer: Hashimoto thyroiditis. Why correct: autoimmune hypothyroid goiter with lymphoid follicles and Hurthle change is classic. Why others are wrong: Graves gives hyperthyroidism and scalloped colloid; de Quervain is painful and granulomatous; Riedel shows dense fibrosis extending into adjacent tissue.

  4. Short answer: Differentiate primary and secondary adrenal insufficiency.

    Answer: Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison disease) is due to adrenal destruction, so cortisol and often aldosterone are low, ACTH is high, and hyperpigmentation is present. Secondary adrenal insufficiency results from low ACTH, so hyperpigmentation is absent and aldosterone is often preserved because it is largely regulated by renin-angiotensin.

  5. Essay: Pathology of pituitary adenomas.

    Answer: Pituitary adenomas are the most common pituitary tumors and may be microadenomas or macroadenomas. Functionally they are classified as prolactin, GH, ACTH, gonadotroph, TSH, mixed, null cell, or plurihormonal tumors. Clinically they produce either hormone excess syndromes or mass effects such as headache, hypopituitarism, and visual defects. Histologically they may show diffuse, sinusoidal, or papillary patterns.

  6. MCQ: A child with adrenal mass has small round blue cells and Homer-Wright rosettes. Most likely diagnosis?

    Answer: Neuroblastoma. Why correct: adrenal/abdominal tumor in young child with rosettes is classic. Why not pheochromocytoma: pheochromocytoma occurs more often in adults and shows zellballen pattern. Why not ganglioneuroma: mature benign tumor with mature ganglion cells, not primitive round cells.

  7. Short note: Graves disease.

    Answer: Graves disease is autoimmune hyperthyroidism characterized by diffuse toxic goiter, thyrotoxicosis, and ophthalmopathy. Autoantibodies stimulating TSH receptor cause hormone overproduction and gland enlargement. Histology shows diffuse hyperplasia, papillary infoldings, and scant scalloped colloid. Clinically there is weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia, tremor, and eye signs.

PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 32-year-old woman has amenorrhea, galactorrhea, and infertility. MRI shows a pituitary microadenoma. Which cell type is most likely involved?
    Answer: Lactotroph adenoma. Reasoning: Hyperprolactinemia classically causes amenorrhea-galactorrhea and infertility. A microadenoma strongly supports prolactinoma.
  2. A patient with chronic steroid therapy abruptly stops treatment and presents with hypotension, vomiting, hypoglycemia, and hyperkalemia. What is the best explanation?
    Answer: Acute adrenal crisis. Reasoning: Chronic exogenous steroids suppress HPA axis; sudden withdrawal may precipitate adrenal insufficiency, especially during stress.
  3. A 40-year-old woman has weight loss, heat intolerance, tremor, exophthalmos, and low TSH. What is the most likely mechanism?
    Answer: Autoantibodies stimulating the TSH receptor. Reasoning: Graves disease causes thyroid overactivity via thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins.
  4. A patient has hypertension, hypokalemia, low renin, and adrenal cortical lesion. Which diagnosis is most likely?
    Answer: Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn syndrome). Reasoning: The combination of HTN + hypokalemia + low renin localizes autonomous aldosterone excess to adrenal disease.
  5. A woman presents with fatigue, weight loss, hyperpigmentation, hyponatremia, and hyperkalemia. Which lab pattern best fits?
    Answer: Low cortisol, high ACTH. Reasoning: This is primary adrenal insufficiency. Hyperpigmentation requires elevated ACTH/MSH-related activity.
  6. A painful enlarged thyroid follows a viral URI. Labs initially show raised thyroid hormones. What is the most appropriate diagnosis?
    Answer: Subacute granulomatous thyroiditis. Reasoning: Pain and post-viral onset are the key clues; hormone excess is from destructive release, not increased synthesis.
  7. A child has proportionate short stature, delayed puberty, normal intelligence, and recurrent hypoglycemia. Which disorder best explains this?
    Answer: Pituitary dwarfism. Reasoning: GH deficiency before growth completion gives proportionate dwarfism with normal mental development.

Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Most endocrine questions become easy when you localize disease by feedback loops.
  • Sheehan syndrome = postpartum pituitary necrosis; first clue is failure of lactation.
  • Primary adrenal insufficiency gives hyperpigmentation; secondary usually does not.
  • Pheochromocytoma = catecholamine tumor with hypertension and elevated urinary catecholamine metabolites.
  • Graves = hyperthyroidism + diffuse goiter + ophthalmopathy; Hashimoto = autoimmune hypothyroid goiter with Hurthle cells.
  • Painful thyroid suggests de Quervain; stony hard fixed thyroid suggests Riedel or malignancy.
  • Goiter is usually a compensatory response to thyroid hormone deficiency, especially iodine deficiency.
  • Pituitary adenoma, adrenal cortical lesions, thyroid disorders, parathyroid disease, and islet pathology all reappear in MEN/PGA syndromes.
  • For exams, memorize the signature clue for each disease: lactation failure, hyperpigmentation, exophthalmos, painful thyroid, zellballen pattern, Homer-Wright rosettes.
