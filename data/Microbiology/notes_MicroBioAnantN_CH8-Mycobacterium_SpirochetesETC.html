Exam-Oriented Medical Notes: Mycobacteria, Spirochetes, and More

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Mycobacterium tuberculosis Complex

Mycobacteria are slender, non-motile, non-sporing, aerobic rods [1, 2]. They possess a lipid-rich cell wall containing mycolic acid, making them acid-fast bacilli (AFB); they resist decolorization by 20% sulphuric acid [2, 3].

  • Pathogenesis: Infection occurs via inhalation of droplet nuclei [4]. Cell-mediated immunity (CMI) is the primary defense mechanism [5, 6].
    • Primary TB: Characterized by the Ghon focus (subpleural lesion) and enlarged hilar lymph nodes, together forming the primary complex [7, 8].
    • Post-primary TB: Resulting from reactivation or reinfection, typically affecting the upper lobes with frequent cavitation [9].
  • Diagnosis: Sputum microscopy (ZN stain) is the mainstay [10, 11]. Lowenstein-Jensen (LJ) medium is the gold standard for culture, appearing as dry, rough, "eugonic" colonies in 2–8 weeks [12-14].

Mycobacterium leprae

The causative agent of leprosy, M. leprae is an obligate intracellular parasite that cannot be grown in cell-free media [15, 16]. It shows a predilection for cooler tissues like skin and peripheral nerves [17].

  • Classification: Based on the host's CMI. Tuberculoid (TT) leprosy shows high CMI and few bacilli (paucibacillary), while Lepromatous (LL) leprosy shows deficient CMI and abundant bacilli (multibacillary) [18, 19].
  • Morphology: Characterized by globi (clusters of bacilli) with a "cigar bundle" appearance in Virchow's "lepra cells" [16].

Spirochetes

These are motile, flexible, spiral bacteria with endoflagella [20].

  • Treponema pallidum (Syphilis): Progresses through Primary (hard chancre), Secondary (rashes, condylomata), and Tertiary (gumma, neurosyphilis) stages [21, 22].
  • Leptospira: Zoonotic infection (Weil’s disease) characterized by fever, jaundice, and renal failure [23].
  • Borrelia: Causes relapsing fever due to antigenic variation [24, 25].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Acid Fastness: Due to unsaponifiable mycolic acid wax in the cell wall [3]. M. tuberculosis is also alcohol-fast [3].
  • Niacin Test: Human M. tuberculosis is niacin-positive, which differentiates it from M. bovis [14, 26].
  • Fried Egg Colonies: Pathognomonic appearance of Mycoplasma on solid media [27, 28].
  • Sulfur Granules: Bacterial colonies found in the discharge of Actinomycosis lesions [29, 30].
  • VDRL vs. TPHA: VDRL is a non-specific screening test (detects reagin); TPHA is a specific confirmatory treponemal test [31-33].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Assuming a negative Mantoux (Tuberculin) test rules out TB.
Reasoning: Anergy (false negatives) can occur in miliary TB, severe malnutrition, HIV infection, or immunosuppression because these conditions impair the CMI required for a positive skin reaction [34, 35].

Reflective Question: Why is M. leprae non-cultivable in standard laboratory media while M. tuberculosis is, despite both being Mycobacteria? (Consider its extreme long generation time of 12–13 days and obligate intracellular nature [16, 36]).

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Chronic Cougher (Decision-Making Under Pressure)

A 50-year-old male presents with a 2-month history of evening rise in temperature, weight loss, and blood-tinged sputum. Chest X-ray shows apical infiltrates [37].
Differential: Pulmonary TB vs. Fungal infection (Aspergilloma) vs. Malignancy.
Management: Sputum AFB is positive. Under pressure: In high-burden areas, start DOTS therapy (HRZE) immediately to prevent community transmission [37, 38].

Case 2: The Painless Ulcer

A 20-year-old truck driver presents with a painless, indurated genital ulcer and rubbery inguinal lymphadenopathy [39].
Diagnosis: Primary Syphilis.
Reasoning: The "hard chancre" is typically painless and avascular [21]. VDRL is reactive at 1:64 [39]. Treatment with Penicillin is curative [39, 40].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Acid-Fastness. Describe the principle of ZN Staining.
    Answer: Acid-fastness is the ability of certain bacteria to resist decolorization by mineral acids after staining [2, 3]. In ZN staining, carbol fuchsin is the primary stain (heated to penetrate the wax), 20% sulphuric acid is the decolorizer, and methylene blue is the counterstain [10, 41].
  2. Classify the spectrum of Leprosy based on the Ridley-Jopling scale.
    Answer: TT (Tuberculoid), BT (Borderline Tuberculoid), BB (Mid-borderline), BL (Borderline Lepromatous), and LL (Lepromatous) [42].
  3. Short Note: Primary Complex in Tuberculosis. (See Section 1 [7, 8]).
  4. What are "Sulphur Granules" and in which condition are they found?
    Answer: They are white/yellowish bacterial colonies (networks of Gram-positive filaments) found in Actinomycosis discharge [30, 43].
  5. Describe the laboratory diagnosis of Mycoplasma pneumoniae.
    Answer: "Fried egg" colonies on PPLO agar, cold agglutination test (non-specific but suggestive at titres ≥ 1:32), and PCR [27, 44, 45].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A patient with treated syphilis returns with a positive VDRL but a negative TPHA. What is the most likely explanation?
    Correct Answer: Biological False Positive (BFP).
    Reasoning: BFP reactions occur when non-specific tests (VDRL) are positive due to other conditions (malaria, SLE, etc.) but treponemal-specific tests (TPHA) are negative [33, 46, 47].
  2. Which of the following is the most sensitive non-specific test for primary atypical pneumonia?
    Correct Answer: Cold agglutination test.
    Reasoning: It detects macroglobulin antibodies that agglutinate human group O RBCs at 4°C [45].
  3. A 25-year-old female develops a "reversal reaction" (Type 1 Lepra Reaction) during leprosy treatment. This is an example of:
    Correct Answer: Type IV Hypersensitivity (Delayed-type).
    Reasoning: It is a cell-mediated immune response with an influx of lymphocytes into lesions [48].
  4. Which medium is specifically used to differentiate M. tuberculosis from M. bovis via the growth of the former?
    Answer: Medium containing T2H (thiophene-2 carboxylic acid), which inhibits M. bovis but not M. tuberculosis [14, 49].
  5. A 60-year-old HIV patient presents with pneumonia. Sputum reveals weakly acid-fast, branching, filamentous bacilli. The most likely organism is:
    Correct Answer: Nocardia asteroides.
    Reasoning: Nocardia is aerobic and weakly acid-fast, unlike the non-acid-fast Actinomyces [50-52].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias: Confirmation Bias
Clinicians in TB-endemic areas may incorrectly diagnose all lung infiltrates as Tuberculosis, missing Systemic Nocardiosis in immunocompromised patients (e.g., HIV+).
Structured Approach:

  • Use a "Modified" ZN stain (1% sulphuric acid) to detect Nocardia, as the standard 20% acid used for TB will decolorize it [51, 53].
  • Always perform a Gram stain; Nocardia is Gram-positive, whereas M. tuberculosis stains poorly [53, 54].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • TB: Mycolic acid (Acid-fastness), Th1 immunity, LJ medium (Slow growth), DOTS therapy [2, 3, 5, 38, 55].
  • Leprosy: CMI determines phenotype; TT (High CMI, few bacilli), LL (Low CMI, many globi) [18, 19].
  • Syphilis: T. pallidum; Primary chancre is painless; VDRL for screening, TPHA for confirmation [21, 31, 56].
  • Mycoplasma: No cell wall (penicillin resistant); fried egg colonies; walking pneumonia [27, 57, 58].
  • Actinomycetes: Actinomyces (anaerobic, sulfur granules); Nocardia (aerobic, weakly acid-fast) [30, 50, 52].
