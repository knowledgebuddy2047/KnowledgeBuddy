Exam-Oriented Study Notes: Pathogenic Cocci and Bacilli

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

A. Gram-Positive Cocci

  • Staphylococcus: Gram-positive cocci arranged in grape-like clusters [1].
    • S. aureus: Coagulase-positive, produces golden-yellow pigment, and is a major human pathogen causing localized suppurative lesions [1-3].
    • Coagulase-Negative Staphylococci (CoNS): Includes S. epidermidis (device-associated infections, biofilms) and S. saprophyticus (UTIs in young women) [4-6].
  • Streptococcus: Gram-positive cocci in chains or pairs [7].
    • S. pyogenes (Group A): Beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-sensitive. Causes pharyngitis, impetigo, and non-suppurative sequelae like Rheumatic Fever and Glomerulonephritis [8-10].
    • S. agalactiae (Group B): Leading cause of neonatal meningitis and sepsis [11].
    • Viridans Group: Alpha-hemolytic, commensals of the mouth; can cause subacute bacterial endocarditis (SABE) [12, 13].
  • Pneumococcus (S. pneumoniae): Gram-positive, lanceolate-shaped diplococci [14]. Characterized by bile solubility and optochin sensitivity [14, 15].

B. Gram-Negative Cocci (Neisseria)

  • N. meningitidis: Capsulated, oxidase-positive, ferments glucose and maltose. Major cause of epidemic meningitis [16-18].
  • N. gonorrhoeae: Non-capsulated (in most forms), ferments only glucose. Causes the STI gonorrhea [16, 18, 19].

C. Gram-Positive Bacilli

  • Corynebacterium diphtheriae: Slender, pleomorphic rods with "Chinese letter" or cuneiform arrangement [20]. Contains metachromatic (volutin) granules [20].
  • Bacillus anthracis: Large, non-motile, spore-forming rods. Causative agent of Anthrax (cutaneous black eschar) [21-23].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Catalase Test: Differentiates Staphylococcus (+) from Streptococcus (-) [24, 25].
  • Coagulase Test: Differentiates S. aureus (+) from other Staphylococci (-) [26, 27].
  • M Protein: The primary virulence factor of S. pyogenes; it inhibits phagocytosis [25, 28].
  • Diphtheria Toxin Mechanism: Inhibits protein synthesis by inactivating Elongation Factor-2 (EF-2) [29, 30].
  • MecA Gene: Regulates resistance in MRSA by altering the target site to PBP2a [24, 31].
  • Quellung Reaction: Capsule swelling seen in S. pneumoniae when mixed with specific antiserum [32, 33].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Relying solely on Gram stains for throat swabs. Commensal Neisseria or Streptococci are part of the normal flora; therefore, microscopy alone is often of no value for throat specimens [17, 34, 35].

Reflective Question: Why is the Diphtheria antitoxin prioritized over antibiotics in acute treatment? Reasoning: The life-threatening symptoms are driven by the exotoxin already in circulation; antibiotics kill the bacteria but do not neutralize the toxin already bound to tissues [36, 37].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The "Draughtsman" Colony

Scenario: A 60-year-old man presents with high-grade fever, productive cough, and lobar consolidation on X-ray. Sputum culture shows alpha-hemolytic "draughtsman" colonies [38-40].

  1. Differential Diagnosis: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, S. aureus.
  2. Step-by-Step: Perform Optochin sensitivity and Bile solubility tests. S. pneumoniae will be sensitive/soluble [15, 33, 41].
  3. Intervention: Start Penicillin (if sensitive) or 3rd generation Cephalosporin [33, 38, 42].

Case 2: Decision Under Pressure

Scenario: A 7-year-old child presents with a thick grey-white pseudomembrane over the tonsils and "bull neck" appearance (cervical lymphadenopathy). The child is struggling to breathe [43-45].

  • Urgent Action: Do not wait for lab results (Albert's stain or culture). Immediate administration of Diphtheria Antitoxin (ADS) and securing the airway (emergency tracheostomy if necessary) is mandatory [36, 37, 46].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define Co-agglutination.
    Answer: A procedure where Protein-A-bearing Staphylococci are coated with IgG antiserum. If mixed with the corresponding antigen, the cocci agglutinate. Used for grouping Streptococci and typing Gonococci [47, 48].
  2. Compare and contrast Rheumatic Fever and Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN).
    Answer: Rheumatic fever usually follows throat infection, requires prior sensitization, and necessitates long-term penicillin prophylaxis. PSGN can follow either throat or skin infection (pyoderma) and prophylaxis is not typically indicated [10, 49, 50].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 20-year-old male presents with purulent urethral discharge. Gram stain shows Gram-negative diplococci within polymorphs. What is the most appropriate selective medium for culture?
    A) Blood Agar
    B) MacConkey Agar
    C) Thayer-Martin Medium
    D) Loeffler's Serum Slope
    Correct: C. Thayer-Martin is the selective medium for N. gonorrhoeae as it inhibits normal flora [51, 52].
  2. A 15-year-old boy presents with vomiting 6 hours after eating a pastry. There is no fever. What is the most likely mechanism?
    A) Infection by Salmonella
    B) Preformed Enterotoxin by S. aureus
    C) Toxin production in the gut by V. cholerae
    D) Invasive infection by E. coli
    Correct: B. The short incubation period (2-6 hours) and absence of fever are classic for S. aureus preformed enterotoxin [53-55].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias: Availability Bias. A clinician may assume every sore throat is "Strep Throat" (Group A) and prescribe penicillin, potentially missing C. diphtheriae or viral causes like Mononucleosis.

Approach to Avoid Bias: Use a structured clinical decision rule (e.g., Centor Criteria) and confirm with rapid antigen detection tests or culture before assuming the diagnosis [56, 57].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Staphylococci: Clusters, Catalase (+). S. aureus is Coagulase (+) [1, 27].
  • Streptococci: Chains, Catalase (-). Beta-hemolytic Group A is Bacitracin-sensitive [7, 8].
  • Neisseria: Gram-negative diplococci. N. meningitidis ferments Maltose; N. gonorrhoeae does not [18, 58].
  • C. diphtheriae: Toxin inhibits EF-2; presents with pseudomembrane; requires urgent antitoxin [29, 36, 59].
  • B. anthracis: Spore-forming, non-motile, causes black eschars [22, 23, 60].
