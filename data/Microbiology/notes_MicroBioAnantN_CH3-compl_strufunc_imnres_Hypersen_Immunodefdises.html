Immunology Master Module: Comprehensive Exam-Oriented Notes

This module synthesizes essential concepts from the Complement System, Lymphoid Anatomy, Immune Response, Hypersensitivity, and Immunodeficiency Diseases based on authoritative medical sources [1-5].

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

The Complement System

The complement system is a triggered enzyme cascade of over 30 serum proteins that mediates inflammation and cytolysis [1, 6, 7]. It operates via three pathways:

  • Classical Pathway: Triggered by antigen-antibody complexes (IgM or IgG). C1 binds to the Fc piece of the immunoglobulin, leading to the formation of C3 convertase (C4b2a) [8-10].
  • Alternative Pathway: An antibody-independent, primitive innate defense. Triggered by microbial surfaces (e.g., zymosan, endotoxins). It involves Factors B, D, and Properdin [11-13].
  • Lectin Pathway: Activated by Mannose-binding lectin (MBL) binding to mannose on pathogen surfaces, utilizing MASP-1 and MASP-2 proteases [13-15].

The Membrane Attack Complex (MAC): All pathways converge at the activation of C5. C5b-C9 proteins form a 100 Å diameter "hole" in the target cell membrane, leading to osmotic lysis [16, 17].

Anatomy of the Immune System

  • Primary Lymphoid Organs: Thymus (T-cell maturation/selection) and Bone Marrow (B-cell origin and maturation) [18-20].
  • Secondary Lymphoid Organs: Lymph nodes (filter lymph) and Spleen (filter blood) [21-23].
  • MHC (HLA): Class I (A, B, C) presents to CD8+ T-cells; Class II (DR, DQ, DP) presents to CD4+ T-cells [24-26].

Immune Response and Hypersensitivity

Humoral (AMI) involves B-cells/Antibodies; Cell-mediated (CMI) involves T-cells [27, 28]. Hypersensitivity (Gell and Coombs) is classified into four types:

  • Type I (Immediate): IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation (e.g., Anaphylaxis) [29, 30].
  • Type II (Cytotoxic): IgG/IgM binding to cell-surface antigens (e.g., Hemolytic disease of the newborn) [31, 32].
  • Type III (Immune Complex): Deposition of Ag-Ab complexes (e.g., Serum sickness, Arthus reaction) [33, 34].
  • Type IV (Delayed): T-cell mediated (e.g., Tuberculin test, Contact dermatitis) [35, 36].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Opsonization: C3b and C4b coat particles to promote phagocytosis via Complement Receptors (CR1) [37-39].
  • Anaphylatoxins: C3a and C5a cause mast cell degranulation, increasing vascular permeability and smooth muscle contraction [10, 40, 41].
  • MHC Restriction: T-cells only recognize antigens presented with self-MHC molecules [42-44].
  • Hereditary Angioneurotic Edema: Caused by C1 esterase inhibitor (C1INH) deficiency, leading to unrestrained breakdown of C4 and C2 [45-47].
  • DiGeorge Syndrome: Congenital thymic aplasia (3rd/4th pharyngeal pouches) resulting in deficient CMI and neonatal tetany [48, 49].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Assuming all low-antibody states are primary immunodeficiencies. Reasoning: Always rule out secondary causes like malnutrition, nephrotic syndrome (increased loss), or malignancy [50-52].

Reflective Question: Why does a deficiency in early classical pathway components (C1, C2, C4) predispose patients to autoimmune diseases like SLE rather than just infections? [45, 53]

4. Clinical Case Studies

Case 1: Recurrent Infections in an Infant

Scenario: A 9-month-old male presents with his third episode of Streptococcus pneumoniae pneumonia. Labs show IgG < 10% of normal; B-cells are absent in peripheral blood. Tonsils are atrophic [54-56].

Differential Diagnosis: Bruton’s X-linked Agammaglobulinemia vs. Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID) [57, 58].

Reasoning: The age (post-maternal antibody decay) and male sex strongly suggest Bruton's [57]. CVID usually presents later (15-35 years) [59].

Case 2: Emergency Response - "Decision Under Pressure"

Scenario: A patient receives penicillin and immediately develops shortness of breath, hypotension, and a "pounding" sensation in the head [60, 61].

Intervention: Immediate intramuscular Adrenaline (0.5 ml of 1:1000). Delaying for laboratory confirmation of IgE levels can be fatal [61, 62].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Essay: Describe the components and steps of the Classical Complement Pathway. [63]
    Answer: Focus on C1qrs binding to Ag-Ab complexes, C3 convertase formation (C4b2a), and the final MAC (C5b-9) [9, 10, 17].
  2. Short Answer: List the functions of the Spleen. [64]
    Answer: Graveyard for effete RBCs, reserve tank for blood, and systemic filter for bloodborne antigens [23].
  3. Short Note: Differences between Primary and Secondary Immune Response. [4]
    Answer: Primary has long lag, IgM predominant, lower affinity. Secondary is rapid, IgG predominant, higher affinity [65, 66].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. Question: A 24-year-old male with chronic back pain and stiffness is found to be positive for HLA-B27. He is at highest risk for which condition?
    A) Rheumatoid Arthritis
    B) Ankylosing Spondylitis
    C) Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
    D) Multiple Sclerosis
    Reasoning: HLA-B27 has a strong association with Ankylosing Spondylitis [44, 67].
  2. Question: Which complement fragment is primarily responsible for chemotaxis of neutrophils?
    A) C3b
    B) C4a
    C) C5a
    D) C1q
    Reasoning: C5a is both an anaphylatoxin and a potent chemotactic factor [10, 40].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias: Search Satisficing. Diagnosing a patient with "atopy" because they have hay fever, while missing an underlying Selective IgA deficiency [68-70].

Avoidance Strategy: Use a structured approach to recurrent infections—always screen for the four major arms: B-cells, T-cells, Phagocytes, and Complement [54, 71].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Complement: C3 is the central component; C5b-9 is the effector (MAC) [11, 17].
  • Antibody Classes: IgM and IgG (1, 2, 3) are the only classes that fix complement [8].
  • T-cell Subsets: TH1 promotes CMI (IFN-gamma); TH2 promotes AMI (IL-4, 5, 6) [72].
  • Primary Deficiencies: Suspect B-cell defects if infections start after 6 months of age [57].
  • Hypersensitivity: Type I is immediate (IgE); Type IV is delayed (T-cells) [30, 73].
