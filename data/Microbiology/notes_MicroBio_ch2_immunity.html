Immunology: Comprehensive Study Notes

Tailored for MBBS, PLAB, USMLE, and International Medical Board Exams.

1. Introduction to Immunity

Immunity refers to the resistance exhibited by a host towards injury caused by microorganisms and their products [1]. It is broadly classified into two types: Innate (Native) and Acquired (Adaptive) [2].

Key Comparison: Innate vs. Acquired Immunity

Feature Innate Immunity Acquired Immunity
Occurrence Present by birth; genetic make-up [2, 3] Acquired during life [4]
Specificity Non-specific (general resistance) [3] Highly specific to antigens [5, 6]
Memory Absent [3] Present (Secondary response) [6, 7]
Exposure effect Not affected by prior contact [3] Enhanced by repeated contact [7]

Factors Affecting Innate Immunity

  • Age: The very young and very old are more susceptible to infections [8]. For example, Tinea capitis often undergoes spontaneous cure with the onset of puberty due to hormonal influences [9, 10].
  • Hormones: Endocrine disorders like diabetes mellitus and hypothyroidism enhance susceptibility [11].
  • Nutrition: Malnutrition reduces both humoral and cell-mediated immune processes [12].

2. Antigens and Immunogenicity

An antigen is a substance that reacts specifically with antibodies or T-cell receptors [13]. An immunogen is a substance capable of inducing a detectable immune response [14].

High-Yield Concept: All immunogens are antigens, but not all antigens are immunogens [14].

Key Antigenic Determinants

  • Epitope: The smallest unit of antigenicity; can be linear (sequential amino acids) or conformational (formed by folding) [15, 16].
  • Haptens: Low-molecular-weight substances that are not immunogenic by themselves but become so when coupled with a carrier protein [17, 18].
  • Superantigens: Proteins that activate a large number of T-cells irrespective of their antigenic specificity by binding outside the MHC-binding groove [19, 20]. Clinical example: Staphylococcal enterotoxins in food poisoning [21].

3. Antibodies (Immunoglobulins)

Antibodies are glycoprotein molecules secreted by plasma cells that bind specifically to epitopes [22].

Immunoglobulin Classes (GAMED)

Class Key Features for Exams
IgG Most abundant (80%); only class that crosses the placenta [23, 24]. Participates in complement fixation and opsonization [25].
IgA Main component of local/mucosal immunity; present in colostrum, saliva, and tears [26, 27]. Exists as a dimer with a J-chain and Secretory piece [28, 29].
IgM Largest (pentamer); "Millionaire molecule" [30]. First to appear in primary response and in the fetus [25, 31]. Highly effective in agglutination [25].
IgD Acts primarily as a surface receptor on B-lymphocytes [32, 33].
IgE Mediates Type I Hypersensitivity (atopy/anaphylaxis) [33, 34]. Binds to mast cells and basophils [33].

4. The Complement System

A group of serum proteins that act in a cascade to mediate immune/inflammatory reactions [35, 36].

  • Classical Pathway: Activated by Antigen-Antibody complexes (IgM or IgG) [37, 38].
  • Alternative Pathway: Activated by microbial surfaces (e.g., endotoxins) without antibody involvement [39, 40].
  • Lectin Pathway: Activated by Mannose-binding lectin (MBL) [41, 42].
  • Membrane Attack Complex (MAC): Comprised of C5b-C9; creates pores in cell membranes leading to lysis [43, 44].

5. Hypersensitivity Reactions (Coombs and Gell Classification)

Type Mechanism Examples
I (Immediate) IgE mediated; mast cell degranulation [45, 46] Anaphylaxis, Atopy (Hay fever, Asthma) [47, 48]
II (Cytotoxic) IgG/IgM mediated cell surface damage [46, 48] Hemolytic disease of the newborn, transfusion reactions [48, 49]
III (Immune Complex) Antigen-antibody complex deposition [50, 51] Serum sickness, Arthus reaction, SLE [51-53]
IV (Delayed) T-cell mediated (sensitized CD4/CD8) [51, 54] Tuberculin test, Contact dermatitis (Poison ivy) [51, 55]

Practice Questions

Section A: Objective Type (MCQs)

1. Which immunoglobulin class is the first to be synthesized by the fetus?
A. IgG
B. IgA
C. IgM
D. IgE
Answer: C (IgM) [31]

2. The "White Graft Response" is a result of:
A. Delayed hypersensitivity
B. Hyperacute rejection due to pre-existing antibodies
C. T-cell mediated cytotoxicity
D. Lack of HLA matching
Answer: B [56, 57]

3. Which of the following is an immunologically privileged site?
A. Liver
B. Cornea
C. Kidney
D. Spleen
Answer: B [58, 59]

Section B: Subjective Type (Short Answer Questions)

  1. Explain the principle of the "Prozone Phenomenon" in serological tests.
    Hint: It is caused by an excess of antibody, leading to inhibition of lattice formation and a false-negative result [60].
  2. Define "Haptens" and give their clinical significance.
    Hint: They are low-MW substances that are not immunogenic alone but can cause hypersensitivity when bound to tissue proteins (e.g., penicillin) [18, 61].
  3. Discuss the clinical features of DiGeorge Syndrome.
    Hint: It involves congenital aplasia of the thymus, resulting in deficient cell-mediated immunity (CMI) and enhanced susceptibility to viral/fungal infections [62, 63].
