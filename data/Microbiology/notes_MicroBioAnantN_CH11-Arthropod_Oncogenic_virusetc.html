Comprehensive Medical Exam Notes: Advanced Virology

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Arthropod- and Rodent-borne Viral Infections (Arboviruses)

Arboviruses are a varied group of RNA viruses that multiply in hematophagous insect vectors (mosquitoes, ticks) and are transmitted to vertebrates [1, 2]. They primarily cause three clinical entities: fevers with or without rashes (e.g., Dengue), hemorrhagic fevers (e.g., Yellow Fever, Kyasanur Forest Disease), and encephalitis (e.g., Japanese Encephalitis) [3, 4]. Most infections are zoonoses maintained in nature by animal-vector cycles [5].

Rhabdoviruses (Rabies)

The Rabies virus is a bullet-shaped, enveloped virus with a single-stranded, negative-sense RNA genome [6, 7]. It is almost invariably fatal once symptoms appear [8, 9]. Pathogenesis involves the virus multiplying at the site of a bite, then traveling centripetally via axons to the CNS at a speed of approximately 3 mm per hour [10]. After multiplying in the brain, it spreads centrifugally to the salivary glands [10].

Hepatitis Viruses

Viral hepatitis is caused by taxonomically unrelated viruses (A, B, C, D, E, and G) that share hepatotropism [11, 12].

  • HAV & HEV: Transmitted via the fecal-oral route; typically cause acute, self-limiting disease [13-15]. HEV has a high fatality rate (20-40%) in pregnant women [15, 16].
  • HBV & HCV: Transmitted parenterally and sexually; frequently lead to chronic infection, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma [15, 17-21].
  • HDV: A defective virus requiring HBsAg for replication [22].

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) & AIDS

HIV belongs to the Lentivirus subgroup of Retroviridae [23]. It contains two copies of single-stranded RNA and the enzyme reverse transcriptase, which transcribes viral RNA into DNA that integrates into the host genome as a provirus [24, 25]. The primary target is the CD4+ T lymphocyte, leading to its depletion and subsequent susceptibility to opportunistic infections and malignancies [26-28].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Dengue Biphasic Fever: Often presents as a "saddleback" fever with severe myalgia ("break-bone fever") and retrobulbar pain [29, 30].
  • Negri Bodies: Pathognomonic intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies (round/oval, purplish-pink) found postmortem in the hippocampus and cerebellum of rabies victims [31, 32].
  • HBV Serology: HBsAg is the first marker; HBeAg indicates high infectivity; Anti-HBs indicates immunity [33-35].
  • The HIV Window Period: The time (2-8 weeks) between infection and the appearance of detectable antibodies, during which a patient is highly infectious but seronegative [36, 37].
  • NACO Strategies: Strategy I is for transfusion safety (one test); Strategy II is for surveillance/symptomatic diagnosis; Strategy III is for asymptomatic diagnosis (three different tests) [38-41].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Assuming a negative antibody test in the first week of a suspected Arbovirus or HIV infection rules out the disease [37, 42].
Reasoning: Seroconversion takes time. In early HIV, p24 antigen detection or RT-PCR is required for diagnosis during the window period [43-45]. In arboviruses, viral isolation or PCR is more specific than serology in the early viremic phase [5, 46, 47].

Reflective Question: Why is HEV particularly dangerous in the third trimester of pregnancy compared to HAV, despite both being enterically transmitted? (Focus on the high maternal mortality noted in clinical studies) [16].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The High-Fever Traveler

A 40-year-old male presents with high fever, severe headache, and petechiae five days after returning from a tropical region. Tourniquet test is positive, and platelet count is 30,000/mm³ [29].
Differential: Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF) vs. Malaria vs. Chikungunya [29, 48, 49].
Intervention: Immediate fluid replacement and monitoring of hematocrit [50]. Decision Under Pressure: Avoid Aspirin/NSAIDs due to the risk of gastritis and platelet dysfunction in DHF [50].

Case 2: The Street Dog Encounter

A 5-year-old child is bitten on the neck by a stray dog. The dog is available for observation [51].
Differential Diagnosis: Category III Rabies exposure [52].
Intervention: Immediate wound toileting with soap and water [53]. Administer Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG) infiltrated at the wound site and start active immunization (vaccine) on days 0, 3, 7, 14, and 28 [52-54]. Note: If the dog remains healthy for 10 days, vaccination can be discontinued [52, 55].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Describe the laboratory diagnosis of HIV according to NACO guidelines.
    Answer: Diagnosis involves three strategies. Strategy I (blood screening) requires one highly sensitive test. Strategy II (symptomatic cases) requires two different tests. Strategy III (asymptomatic) requires three tests [38, 40, 56].
  2. What are Negri bodies? Mention their diagnostic significance.
    Answer: They are intracytoplasmic, round, or oval purplish-pink structures with basophilic granules found postmortem in the brain (hippocampus/cerebellum). They are pathognomonic for Rabies [31, 32].
  3. Differentiate between HAV and HEV epidemiology.
    Answer: Both are fecal-oral. HAV is common in children and often subclinical; HEV is common in young adults and has a high case fatality rate in pregnant women [15, 16, 57, 58].
  4. Define "Window Period" in HIV infection.
    Answer: The 2-8 week period after infection before antibodies appear, where the patient is infectious but serological tests for antibodies are negative [36, 37].
  5. Explain the importance of the HBeAg marker in Hepatitis B.
    Answer: HBeAg is a marker of active viral replication and high infectivity [35, 59].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 30-year-old woman presents with delirium and pharyngeal spasms when attempting to drink water three months after a dog bite. Brain tissue postmortem is likely to show:
    Correct Answer: Negri Bodies.
    Reasoning: This clinical picture describes "hydrophobia," characteristic of the acute neurological phase of Rabies [31, 60, 61].
  2. A healthcare worker suffers a needle-stick injury from a patient who is HBsAg positive and HBeAg positive. What is the most important immediate step?
    Correct Answer: Hepatitis B Immune Globulin (HBIG) and active vaccination.
    Reasoning: Combined immunization is recommended for high-risk exposures to prevent infection [62].
  3. Which HIV marker is the earliest to appear in the blood following a massive infection?
    Correct Answer: p24 Antigen.
    Reasoning: The p24 core antigen appears within about two weeks, before antibodies develop [43, 44].
  4. A 25-year-old pregnant woman in her third trimester presents with jaundice and acute liver failure during a water-borne epidemic. What is the most likely pathogen?
    Correct Answer: Hepatitis E Virus (HEV).
    Reasoning: HEV is associated with high mortality in late pregnancy [15, 16].
  5. A patient presents with high-grade fever, retro-orbital pain, and a maculopapular rash. Lab results show a 22% increase in hematocrit and a platelet count of 40,000/mm³. This indicates:
    Correct Answer: Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever.
    Reasoning: Hemoconcentration (rise in hematocrit >20%) and thrombocytopenia are key diagnostic criteria for DHF [63].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias: Availability Bias
Example: In India, a clinician might assume all jaundice cases in children are HAV, potentially missing HBV or HCV if they don't perform a full hepatitis panel [14, 58, 64].
Structured Approach:

  • Always perform a full viral hepatitis panel (HBsAg, Anti-HBc, Anti-HAV IgM, Anti-HCV) in any patient with icteric illness [64].
  • In HIV testing, never rely on a single screening test for diagnosis; follow NACO Strategy III for asymptomatic patients to avoid false-positive results [40, 65, 66].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Arboviruses: Primarily zoonotic; Dengue is the most important vector-borne disease worldwide [4, 5, 29].
  • Rabies: Preventable by immediate post-exposure prophylaxis (Wound cleaning + RIG + Vaccine); 100% fatal once clinical [8, 52, 67].
  • Hepatitis B: HBsAg is the primary screening marker; vaccine-preventable [17, 33].
  • HIV: CD4 depletion leads to AIDS; p24 is the early antigen marker; RT-PCR is the most sensitive nucleic acid test [26, 44, 45, 68, 69].
  • Oncogenic Viruses: HPV (Cervical cancer) and HBV/HCV (Hepatocellular carcinoma) are major viral causes of human cancer [70-72].
