Exam-Oriented Medical Notes: Medical Mycology

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Introduction and Classification

Fungi are eukaryotic protists with rigid cell walls containing chitin, mannan, and other polysaccharides [1]. They differ from bacteria by having a true nucleus, nuclear membrane, and paired chromosomes [1]. While most are saprophytes, they are increasingly significant as opportunistic pathogens due to the use of antibiotics, steroids, and immunosuppressives [2].

  • Morphological Classification: Fungi are grouped into Yeasts (e.g., Cryptococcus), Yeast-like fungi (e.g., Candida), Moulds (e.g., Aspergillus), and Dimorphic fungi (e.g., Histoplasma) [3, 4].
  • Systemic Classification: Based on sexual spore formation: Phycomycetes (non-septate hyphae), Ascomycetes (ascospores), Basidiomycetes (basidiospores), and Fungi imperfecti (no known sexual phase) [5, 6].
  • Pathogenic Classification: Divided into Primary pathogens (cause infection in healthy hosts, e.g., dimorphic fungi) and Opportunistic pathogens (infect those with immune defects like HIV or diabetes) [7].

Superficial and Cutaneous Mycoses

Superficial mycoses are limited to the dead layers of the skin (stratum corneum) and elicit no inflammatory response [8, 9]. Examples include Pityriasis versicolor (caused by Malassezia furfur, presenting as depigmented macules) and Tinea nigra [10-12]. Dermatophytosis (ringworm) involves keratinized structures (skin, hair, nails) and is caused by Microsporum, Trichophyton, and Epidermophyton [13, 14].

Subcutaneous Mycoses

These chronic infections often follow trauma [15, 16].

  • Mycetoma (Madura foot): Characterized by localized swelling, multiple sinuses, and discharge containing granules [16, 17]. It can be Eumycetoma (fungal) or Actinomycetoma (bacterial/actinomycetes) [18].
  • Sporotrichosis: Caused by Sporothrix schenckii; typically involves "lymphocutaneous" spread after a "rose gardener's" injury (thorn prick) [19, 20].
  • Rhinosporidiosis: Produces friable polyps in the nose or mouth [21].

Systemic and Opportunistic Mycoses

Systemic mycoses are caused by thermally dimorphic fungi (yeast at 37°C, mould at 25°C) and usually start as respiratory infections [22, 23]. Opportunistic mycoses like Candidiasis, Aspergillosis, and Cryptococcosis are major causes of morbidity in immunocompromised patients [23, 24]. Zygomycosis (Mucormycosis) is a life-threatening, angio-invasive infection especially seen in poorly controlled diabetics [25, 26].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Dimorphism: High-yield concept where fungi exist as moulds in the environment (25°C) and yeasts in human tissues (37°C) [5, 23].
  • Nagler's-like Reactions/Diagnostics:
    • Germ Tube Test: Rapid identification for Candida albicans (Reynolds-Braude phenomenon) [27].
    • India Ink: Used for negative staining to visualize the capsule of Cryptococcus neoformans [28, 29].
    • Wood’s Lamp: Microsporum infections show bright greenish-yellow fluorescence [30].
  • Sclerotic Bodies: Diagnostic round, dark brown, yeast-like bodies seen in Chromoblastomycosis [31].
  • Asteroid Body: A yeast cell surrounded by eosinophilic radiating substance, diagnostic for Sporotrichosis [32].
  • "Mariner’s Wheel" Appearance: Characteristic multiple budding yeast of Paracoccidioides brasiliensis [33, 34].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Common Pitfall: Mistaking Dermatophytosis (fungal) for Dermatomycosis. Reasoning: Dermatophytosis specifically refers to infections by the three genera of dermatophytes (Microsporum, Trichophyton, Epidermophyton), whereas dermatomycosis includes skin lesions by any other fungi, such as Candida [13].

Reflective Question: Why are Zygomycetes (Mucor/Rhizopus) so dangerous in diabetic patients with ketoacidosis? (Hint: Focus on their angio-invasive nature and affinity for acidic, high-glucose environments [25, 26]).

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Chronic Foot Swelling

A 45-year-old farmer presents with a 2-year history of a slow-growing, painless swelling on his left foot. On examination, there are multiple discharging sinuses. The discharge contains black granules [16, 35].
Differential Diagnosis: Eumycetoma vs. Actinomycetoma [18, 36].
Prioritizing Interventions: 1. Gram stain and KOH mount of the granules to differentiate fungal from bacterial causes [16, 35]. 2. Culture on SDA [36].
Reasoning: Black granules are highly suggestive of Eumycetoma (e.g., Madurella mycetomatis), whereas soft, white/yellow granules suggest Actinomycetoma [36].

Case 2: The Immunocompromised Headache (Decision-Making Under Pressure)

A 28-year-old HIV-positive patient presents with a severe headache, neck rigidity, and altered sensorium [37].
Diagnostic Step: Immediate Lumbar Puncture. India Ink preparation of CSF shows capsulated budding yeast [29, 37].
Decision-Making Under Pressure: Do not wait for cultures. Start Amphotericin B and Flucytosine immediately to prevent mortality from Cryptococcal meningitis [37, 38].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Describe the laboratory diagnosis of fungal infections. [39, 40]
    Answer Summary: Includes microscopy (KOH, PAS, India Ink), culture (Sabouraud’s Dextrose Agar), serology (antigen detection), and newer molecular methods like PCR [28, 41-43].
  2. What are Dimorphic Fungi? Enumerate four examples. [23]
    Answer Summary: Fungi that exist as moulds at 25°C and yeasts at 37°C. Examples: Histoplasma capsulatum, Blastomyces dermatitidis, Paracoccidioides brasiliensis, Coccidioides immitis [23].
  3. Short Note: SDA (Sabouraud’s Dextrose Agar). [39, 40, 44]
    Answer Summary: A specific medium for fungi with an acidic pH (5.4). Often contains antibiotics (chloramphenicol) to inhibit bacteria and cycloheximide to inhibit contaminant moulds [39, 44, 45].
  4. Differentiate between Eumycetoma and Actinomycetoma based on granules. [36]
    Answer Summary: Eumycetoma granules are mostly hard, brown/black or white, and composed of broad septate hyphae. Actinomycetoma granules are soft, white/yellow/red, and composed of thin filaments (<1 µm) [16, 36].
  5. Explain the term "Id Reaction" (Dermatophytids). [46]
    Answer Summary: Sterile vesicular lesions at sites distant from a dermatophyte infection, caused by hypersensitivity to fungal antigens [46].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 35-year-old male with a history of poorly controlled diabetes presents with proptosis and a black, necrotic eschar in the nasal cavity. Microscopic examination of a biopsy shows broad, non-septate, ribbon-like hyphae branching at right angles. What is the diagnosis?
    Answer: Rhinocerebral Zygomycosis (Mucormycosis).
    Reasoning: The clinical triad of diabetes, nasal eschar, and broad non-septate hyphae is classic for Mucor or Rhizopus [26, 47].
  2. A rose gardener presents with nodular lesions along the lymphatics of his right arm following a thorn prick. Culture at 25°C shows flower-like clusters of conidia. What is the causative agent?
    Answer: Sporothrix schenckii.
    Reasoning: This is the classic presentation of lymphocutaneous sporotrichosis [20, 48].
  3. Which antifungal agent works by inhibiting ergosterol biosynthesis?
    Answer: Azoles (e.g., Ketoconazole, Itraconazole).
    Reasoning: Azoles specifically target ergosterol synthesis, whereas Polyenes bind to existing sterols to perturb the cell membrane [49, 50].
  4. A sputum sample from a patient with suspected Histoplasmosis shows tuberculate macroconidia. This finding was obtained from which type of culture?
    Answer: Mould phase culture at 25°C.
    Reasoning: Tuberculate macroconidia are characteristic of the mycelial (mould) phase of H. capsulatum [51, 52].
  5. A patient presents with creamy white patches on the tongue that, when scraped, leave a red oozing surface. This condition is most likely:
    Answer: Oral Thrush (Candidiasis).
    Reasoning: The "red oozing surface" after removal of white patches is a hallmark of mucosal Candida infection [53, 54].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Bias: Availability Bias
Clinicians often diagnose any scaly, itchy circular lesion as Tinea (Dermatophytosis).
Problem-Solving Approach: To avoid bias, perform a KOH mount. A true dermatophyte infection must show branching hyaline septate hyphae [55]. If negative, consider Eczema or Psoriasis, which can mimic the "ringworm" appearance [13, 55].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Fungi are eukaryotic with cell walls containing chitin [1, 56].
  • Dimorphism is key for systemic pathogens (Mould at 25°C, Yeast at 37°C) [23, 57].
  • Dermatophytes (Microsporum, Trichophyton, Epidermophyton) infect skin, hair, and nails [13, 58].
  • Opportunistic infections (Candida, Cryptococcus, Aspergillus, Mucor) are critical in immunocompromised states [23, 59].
  • Standard Diagnosis: Direct microscopy with 10-20% KOH for "clearing" keratin [41, 55].
  • Primary Treatment: Polyenes (Amphotericin B) for systemic disease; Azoles for less severe or topical infections [49, 60].
