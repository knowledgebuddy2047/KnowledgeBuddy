Medical Microbiology: Immunology & Infection Foundation

Full Chapter Explanation Notes

1. Dynamics of Infection

Infection is the lodgement and multiplication of a parasite in or on host tissues [1]. It is a common natural event, whereas infectious disease is a rare consequence where tissue injury occurs [1].

  • Classifications:
    • Primary: First time a parasite infects a host [1].
    • Secondary: A new parasite infects a host already weakened by a pre-existing disease [2].
    • Focal Sepsis: Localized infection (e.g., appendix, tonsils) causing generalized effects [2].
    • Nosocomial: Hospital-acquired infections [2].
    • Iatrogenic: Physician-induced infections resulting from procedures [3].
  • Sources & Transmission: Humans are the most common source, often via carriers (asymptomatic individuals who harbor pathogens) [4, 5]. Zoonoses are animal diseases transmitted to humans (e.g., plague from rats, rabies from dogs) [6]. Transmission occurs via contact (direct/indirect fomites), inhalation (droplet nuclei), ingestion (contaminated food/water), or inoculation (bites/needles) [7-9].
  • Virulence Factors: Virulence is the degree of pathogenicity in a specific strain [10]. Key determinants include adhesins (pili/fimbriae for attachment), invasiveness (spreading via enzymes like hyaluronidase), and toxigenicity [11-16].

2. Host Resistance: Innate & Acquired Immunity

Innate Immunity is the resistance an individual possesses by virtue of genetic makeup, unaffected by prior contact [17, 18].

  • Mechanisms: Physical barriers like intact skin (bactericidal fatty acids) and mucosa (mucociliary elevator); chemical factors like lysozyme in tears and sebum; and cellular factors like phagocytosis and Natural Killer (NK) cells [19-23].
  • Inflammation & Fever: Non-specific defense responses. Fever stimulates interferon production and may directly inhibit pathogens like T. pallidum [24, 25].
Acquired Immunity is developed during life and features specificity, diversity, memory, and self-recognition [23, 24].
  • Active: Host produces antibodies; long-lasting with a lag phase [26, 27].
  • Passive: Ready-made antibodies received; immediate protection but transient (no memory) [28, 29].

3. Antigens & Immunogens

An antigen reacts with immune products; an immunogen induces an immune response [30, 31].

  • Haptens: Small molecules that are antigenic but only become immunogenic when linked to a carrier protein [32, 33].
  • Epitope: The smallest unit of antigenicity (antigenic determinant) [34].
  • Superantigens: Proteins (e.g., staphylococcal enterotoxins) that non-specifically activate massive numbers of T-cells, causing systemic cytokine release [35, 36].

4. Antibodies (Immunoglobulins)

Glycoproteins produced by plasma cells [37]. Basic structure consists of two Heavy (H) and two Light (L) chains [38].

  • IgG: 80% of total; crosses the placenta; provides long-term immunity [39, 40].
  • IgA: Predominant in secretions (tears, saliva, colostrum) as a dimer with a secretory piece and J-chain [40, 41].
  • IgM: Large pentamer ("millionaire molecule"); first to appear in primary response; potent agglutinator [42, 43].
  • IgE: Mediates Type I hypersensitivity and response to parasites [44].

5. Antigen-Antibody Reactions

Occur in three stages: Primary (invisible), Secondary (visible - e.g., precipitation, agglutination), and Tertiary (in vivo tissue damage) [45-49].

  • Lattice Hypothesis: Multivalent antigens and bivalent antibodies form a visible lattice in the zone of equivalence [50, 51].
  • Precipitation: Soluble antigen + antibody (e.g., VDRL for syphilis) [52, 53].
  • Agglutination: Particulate antigen + antibody (e.g., Widal for typhoid, Coombs test for Rh) [54-56].
  • ELISA & RIA: Highly sensitive labeled assays for detecting minute quantities of antigens/antibodies [57, 58].

Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Exotoxins vs. Endotoxins: Exotoxins are actively secreted proteins, highly toxic, and can be converted to toxoids [59, 60]. Endotoxins are Lipid A components of Gram-negative cell walls, heat-stable, and released only upon cell lysis [11, 59].
  • The Window Period: The time during a primary infection when antibodies are below detectable levels (lag phase), leading to potential false-negative tests [27].
  • Prozone Phenomenon: False negative in agglutination/precipitation tests due to antibody excess, preventing lattice formation [61, 62].
  • IgA & Local Immunity: Secretory IgA forms an "antibody paste" on mucosal surfaces, preventing pathogen adherence [41, 63].
  • Adjuvants: Substances (like aluminum phosphate) added to killed vaccines to enhance the immune response [64, 65].

Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Assumption vs. Reality: It is often assumed that "Passive Immunity" is always inferior to "Active." Reasoning: While passive immunity lacks memory, it is the only viable option for immediate protection in emergencies (e.g., post-exposure rabies or tetanus prophylaxis) [66, 67].

Reflective Question: Why does a person who recovered from measles have lifelong immunity, while they can get the common cold multiple times a year? (Consider the role of antigenic variation and the number of different viruses causing cold symptoms) [68, 69].

Common Pitfall: Interpreting a positive IgM test in a newborn as a sign of past maternal infection. Correction: IgM cannot cross the placenta; its presence in a newborn indicates an intrauterine infection of the fetus itself [70].

Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The "Sterile" Complication

Scenario: A 30-year-old female presents with a rigid jaw and muscle spasms 10 days after a deep puncture wound from a rusty nail. The wound was cleaned with alcohol at a local clinic.
Step 1 (Differential): Tetanus (Clostridium tetani).
Step 2 (Reasoning): Alcohol kills vegetative cells but not spores. Tetanus spores survive for decades in soil and are inoculated into deep wounds [9, 71, 72].
Step 3 (Intervention): Administer Tetanus Immune Globulin (TIG) for immediate passive immunity and Tetanus Toxoid (TT) for long-term active immunity (Combined Immunization) [73, 74].

Case 2: The Neonatal Mystery

Scenario: A 2-week-old infant is tested for HIV because the mother is HIV-positive. The ELISA (antibody test) is positive. The father is distraught, but the pediatrician suggests further testing.
Discussion: A positive ELISA in a neonate often reflects maternal IgG crossing the placenta, which can persist for months [40, 65].
Decision: Perform a PCR or specialized IgM test to confirm if the infant is actually infected [70, 75].

Case 3: Decision-Making Under Pressure

Scenario: During a typhoid screening in a rural camp, a highly symptomatic patient has a negative Widal test. The lab technician reports "no clumps" at low dilutions but notices some clumping at very high dilutions.
Reasoning: This is the Prozone Phenomenon. The patient's antibody levels are so high that they inhibit the reaction [61].
Action: Order serial dilutions of the serum to get an accurate titer and confirm the diagnosis [61, 76].

University-Level Exam Questions

1. Define Virulence and list four major determinants. Answer: Virulence is the degree of pathogenicity of a specific microbial strain [10]. Determinants include: (1) Adhesion (pili/fimbriae), (2) Invasiveness (enzymes like hyaluronidase), (3) Toxigenicity (Exo/Endotoxins), and (4) Plasmids/Bacteriophages carrying resistance/toxin genes [11-16, 59].

PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

1. A patient with a suspected Gram-negative septicemia develops fever, hypotension, and disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). Which bacterial component is the most likely trigger?
A. Peptidoglycan
B. Teichoic acid
C. Lipid A
D. Flagellin Correct: C. Lipid A is the toxic moiety of endotoxin (LPS) in Gram-negative bacteria, responsible for the "endotoxic shock" syndrome [11, 59, 60].
2. A 5-year-old child who has not been vaccinated is exposed to Diphtheria. To provide "instant" protection, which of the following should be administered?
A. Diphtheria Toxoid
B. Formaldehyde-treated toxin
C. Equine Antitoxin
D. Killed vaccine Correct: C. Preformed antibodies (Antitoxin) provide artificial passive immunity, which is immediate, whereas toxoids require a lag period to stimulate the host's system [66, 67, 79].
3. Which antibody class is the first to be synthesized by the human fetus at approximately 20 weeks of gestation?
A. IgG
B. IgA
C. IgM
D. IgE Correct: C. IgM is the earliest immunoglobulin synthesized by the fetus and is the first to appear in a primary immune response [65, 70].

Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Confirmation Bias: A clinician may see a positive Widal test (agglutination) and immediately diagnose Typhoid, ignoring that the patient was vaccinated last month.
Structured Approach:

  • Always differentiate between "Anamnestic responses" (prior exposure/vaccine) and current infection [67].
  • Look for a rising titer in paired sera rather than a single high titer [80].
  • Correlate lab results with clinical "Stages" (Primary, Secondary, Tertiary) [45-49].

Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Infection vs. Disease: Lodgement vs. Injury [1].
  • Virulence: Sum of adhesion, invasion, and toxin production [11].
  • Innate Immunity: First line of defense (Skin, Phagocytes, Lysozyme) [17, 81, 82].
  • Active Immunity: Host makes antibodies; has memory [26, 27].
  • Passive Immunity: Host receives antibodies; immediate but short-lived [28, 66].
  • IgG: Crosses placenta; IgM: First responder; IgA: Mucosal guard [78, 83].
  • Serology: Study of antigen-antibody reactions in vitro [45].
