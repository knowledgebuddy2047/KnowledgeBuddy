Exam-Oriented Medical Notes: General Virology & Viral Pathogenesis

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Introduction to Viruses

Viruses occupy a "twilight zone" between living and non-living; they are essentially "living chemicals" that lack cellular organization [1, 2]. They are obligate intracellular parasites, containing only one type of nucleic acid (DNA or RNA, never both), and are completely dependent on the host cell's synthetic machinery for protein and nucleic acid synthesis [2, 3].

Morphology and Structure

  • Size: Viruses are much smaller than bacteria, ranging from 20 nm (Parvovirus) to 300 nm (Poxvirus) [4].
  • Capsid: A protein coat made of subunits called capsomers [5, 6]. The capsid protects the nucleic acid and mediates attachment to host cells [5, 6].
  • Symmetry: Three types exist: Icosahedral (e.g., Adenovirus), Helical (e.g., Influenza), and Complex (e.g., Poxvirus) [7, 8].
  • Envelope: A lipoprotein outer layer derived from the host cell membrane during budding [8]. Enveloped viruses are susceptible to lipid solvents like ether and bile salts [9, 10].

Viral Replication Cycle

  1. Adsorption: Specific attachment between viral surface proteins and host cell receptors [11].
  2. Penetration: Via viropexis (phagocytosis-like) or envelope fusion with the plasma membrane [12].
  3. Uncoating: Release of the viral genome into the cell, often facilitated by lysosomal enzymes [13].
  4. Biosynthesis: Synthesis of viral mRNA, "early" proteins (enzymes), genome replication, and "late" proteins (structural) [14].
  5. Maturation: Assembly of daughter virions in the nucleus or cytoplasm [15].
  6. Release: Through cell lysis (bacterial viruses and some animal viruses like Polio) or budding (enveloped viruses) [16].

Pathogenesis & Host Response

Viruses spread through the body via blood (viremia) or nerves (e.g., Rabies) [17, 18]. The host defends itself using Interferons (IFN)—host-coded proteins that inhibit viral translation [19, 20]. IFNs are species-specific but not virus-specific [21].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • Baltimore Classification: Categorizes viruses into six classes based on their mRNA transcription strategy [22, 23].
  • Eclipse Phase: The period from penetration to the appearance of mature daughter virions, where the virus seems to disappear from the cell [24].
  • Antigenic Drift vs. Shift: Drift consists of gradual mutations causing periodic epidemics; Shift is a drastic change (genetic reassortment) leading to pandemics (specific to Influenza A) [25].
  • Inclusion Bodies: Diagnostic histological features; e.g., Negri bodies (Rabies), Guarnieri bodies (Vaccinia), and "Owl's eye" (Cytomegalovirus) [26, 27].
  • Polio Vaccines: Salk (IPV) is killed/injected and provides systemic immunity; Sabin (OPV) is live/oral and provides mucosal (IgA) immunity and herd immunity [28-30].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

Pitfall: Assuming a single positive antibody titer confirms an active viral infection.
Reasoning: Because viral antibodies are common in the general population, a definitive diagnosis requires demonstrating a four-fold rise in titer between paired sera (acute and convalescent samples) [31, 32].

Assumption: "Live vaccines are always superior to killed vaccines."
Reasoning: While live vaccines (like OPV) induce more durable immunity, they carry a risk of reversion to virulence (Vaccine-Derived Poliovirus) and are generally contraindicated in pregnancy and immunodeficiency [33-35].

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Weakened Limb (Decision-Making Under Pressure)

A 10-year-old boy presents with fever, headache, and sore throat, followed by sudden onset weakness in his right leg and inability to walk. He has no history of childhood vaccinations [36].
Differential Diagnosis: Paralytic Poliomyelitis, Guillain-Barré Syndrome, Transverse Myelitis.
Priority Intervention: Isolation of the patient and reporting to public health authorities.
Reasoning Under Pressure: In an unimmunized child with asymmetrical flaccid paralysis, Poliomyelitis must be suspected immediately. Stool samples must be collected for viral culture, and Pulse Polio measures should be reviewed for the community [37-39].

Case 2: The Winter Respiratory Crisis

A 60-year-old woman with chronic obstructive airway disease presents in winter with high fever, myalgia, and rapid-onset dyspnea. Chest X-ray shows bilateral infiltrates [40].
Diagnosis: Influenza H1N1 with viral pneumonia [40].
Management: Immediate initiation of Oseltamivir (neuraminidase inhibitor) is critical, as it is most effective when started within 48 hours of onset [40, 41].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Classify viruses based on the Baltimore system.
    Answer: Class 1 (dsDNA), Class 2 (ssDNA), Class 3 (dsRNA), Class 4 (ssRNA + sense), Class 5 (ssRNA - sense), Class 6 (Retroviruses using reverse transcriptase) [22, 23].
  2. Describe the methods used for viral cultivation.
    Answer: Animal inoculation (mice), Embryonated eggs (CAM, allantoic cavity, amniotic sac), and Tissue culture (Primary, Diploid, and Continuous cell lines like HeLa or Vero) [42-45].
  3. Explain the term "Antigenic Shift" and its significance.
    Answer: It is an abrupt, drastic change in the antigenic structure (HA or NA) of Influenza A, often due to genetic reassortment between human and animal strains. It results in a novel virus to which the population has no immunity, leading to pandemics [25, 46].
  4. Define "Interferons" and list their biological effects.
    Answer: Host-coded proteins induced by viruses. Effects include antiviral activity (blocking translation), cellular effects (inhibiting growth), and immunomodulation (activating NK cells) [19, 20, 47].
  5. Compare and contrast Salk (IPV) and Sabin (OPV) vaccines. (Refer to Core Concepts/Table 53.1) [48].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 4-year-old child presents with fever, cough, and white specks on the buccal mucosa opposite the lower molars. What is the most likely diagnosis?
    Correct Answer: Measles.
    Reasoning: Koplik's spots are pathognomonic for the prodromal stage of measles [49, 50].
  2. A patient on high-dose steroids for an autoimmune condition develops severe, painful vesicular eruptions along a single dermatome. What is the mechanism?
    Correct Answer: Reactivation of latent Varicella-Zoster Virus in sensory ganglia.
    Reasoning: Herpes Zoster (shingles) occurs when latent VZV is reactivated due to waning immunity or immunosuppression [51].
  3. Which virus is characterized by a "space vehicle" appearance under electron microscopy?
    Correct Answer: Adenovirus.
    Reasoning: The icosahedral capsid has penton bases with projecting fibers (knobs), giving it a unique shape [52, 53].
  4. An HIV-positive patient with a CD4 count <100 presents with diminishing vision and "fluffy" retinal infiltrates. Which virus is likely involved?
    Correct Answer: Cytomegalovirus (CMV).
    Reasoning: CMV retinitis is a common and severe opportunistic infection in advanced AIDS [54, 55].
  5. A medical student is accidentally stuck with a needle from a patient with chronic Hepatitis B. What component of the virus determines the infectivity of the patient's blood?
    Correct Answer: HBsAg (surface antigen).
    Reasoning: HBsAg is the major surface antigen used for screening and indicates an active infection [56, 57].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias: "Original Antigenic Sin"
Example: An individual exposed to a new strain of Influenza may respond by producing antibodies primarily against the first strain they ever encountered, rather than the current infecting strain. This can lead to a less effective immune response [58].
Structured Approach to Avoid Diagnostic Bias:

  • Molecular Confirmation: Do not rely solely on clinical presentation; use PCR for high sensitivity and specificity [32].
  • Travel History: Always ask about travel (e.g., to areas endemic for Dengue or avian flu) to broaden the differential beyond common local viruses [59].
  • Vaccination Status: Verification of the immunization record is essential to correctly calculate the probability of diseases like Polio or Measles [36].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Definition: Viruses are non-cellular, obligate intracellular parasites containing either DNA or RNA [2, 3].
  • Cultivation: Use living systems (animals, eggs, cell cultures) as viruses cannot grow on inanimate media [42].
  • Latency: Herpesviruses (HSV, VZV, CMV, EBV) are known for establishing lifelong latent infections with periodic reactivation [60-62].
  • Influenza: Pandemic potential is linked to antigenic shift; annual epidemics are linked to antigenic drift [25, 46].
  • Polio: Eradication efforts rely on OPV for herd and mucosal immunity, though IPV is safer for immunocompromised individuals [29, 48, 63].
  • Measles: Highly contagious; identified by Koplik's spots and a centrifugal maculopapular rash [49, 64, 65].
