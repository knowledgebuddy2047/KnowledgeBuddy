Comprehensive Exam Notes: Immunology & Immunohematology

1. Full Chapter Explanation Notes

Autoimmunity: This condition occurs when immunologically competent cells or antibodies act against the body's normal components, leading to structural or functional damage [1]. It represents a failure of immunological tolerance, where the system "injures self" rather than protecting it [1, 2].

  • Mechanisms: Key triggers include antigenic alterations (neoantigens) caused by physical, chemical, or viral influences [3, 4]; sequestered antigens (e.g., lens protein) that never established tolerance during fetal life [4, 5]; and molecular mimicry, where microbial epitopes share identical peptide sequences with self-antigens [6].
  • Classification: Autoimmune diseases are categorized as hemocytolytic (e.g., autoimmune hemolytic anemias), localized/organ-specific (e.g., Hashimoto’s, Graves’, Myasthenia Gravis), or systemic (e.g., SLE, Rheumatoid Arthritis) [7-9].

Transplantation: This involves the restoration of function by grafting tissue (the graft) from a donor to a recipient [10].

  • Graft Types: Autografts (self), Isografts (identical twins), Allografts (same species, different genetics), and Xenografts (different species) [11, 12].
  • Rejection Mechanisms: First set response occurs in 7–10 days due to T-cell-mediated ischemia and necrosis [12]. Second set response is an accelerated rejection occurring if the recipient was previously sensitized to the same donor's antigens [13]. Hyperacute rejection occurs within hours due to pre-existing antibodies [14].

Immunohematology: Focuses on blood group systems, primarily ABO and Rh [15, 16].

  • ABO System: Determined by A and B antigens on RBCs; individuals possess natural isoantibodies (IgM) against the antigens they lack [15, 17].
  • Rh System: The D (Rho) antigen is the most powerful [18]. Unlike ABO, Rh antibodies (IgG) only arise after sensitization through pregnancy or transfusion [19].

2. Core Concepts & High-Yield Explanations

  • SLE (Systemic Lupus Erythematosus): A chronic multisystem disease [20]. ANA is highly sensitive but not specific; Anti-dsDNA and Anti-Sm are relatively specific for SLE [21].
  • Graft-Versus-Host (GVH) Reaction: Occurs when a graft containing immunocompetent T cells is transplanted into a recipient who cannot reject it (e.g., immunodeficient), leading the graft to attack host tissues [22].
  • Hemolytic Disease of the Newborn (HDN): Primarily caused by Rh-negative mothers sensitized to Rh-positive fetuses [23]. Rh-antibodies are IgG and can cross the placenta, while natural ABO antibodies (usually IgM) in A or B group mothers do not [24, 25].
  • HLA (Human Leucocyte Antigen): The major histocompatibility system in humans, crucial for graft survival [26, 27].

3. Critical Thinking & Reasoning

The "Association vs. Causation" Pitfall: In clinical practice, the presence of autoantibodies does not always prove they caused the disease [28]. They may simply be a result of tissue injury, acting to help the immune system eliminate damaged elements [28].

Reflective Questions:

  • Why does the first Rh-positive child of an Rh-negative mother typically escape HDN, while subsequent children are at higher risk? [23, 29]
  • How does the "privileged site" status of the cornea allow for successful transplantation without strict HLA matching? [30, 31]

4. Clinical Case Studies & Discussions

Case 1: The Classic Malar Rash

Scenario: A 27-year-old woman presents with joint pain, swelling in her hands, and a rash across her cheeks [32].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE), Rheumatoid Arthritis, or viral-induced arthritis [21, 33].
  • Step-by-Step Reasoning: Check for ANA pattern. If homogeneous, test for anti-dsDNA [21, 32].
  • Conclusion: A positive anti-dsDNA and presence of LE cells confirm SLE [21, 32].

Case 2: Post-Transplant Deterioration

Scenario: A 38-year-old man receives a kidney transplant. On the 7th post-operative day, his graft becomes tender and creatinine levels rise [34].

  • Differential Diagnosis: Acute allograft rejection, surgical complication, or infection [34, 35].
  • Prioritizing Intervention: Perform a renal biopsy. Findings of lymphocytic infiltration confirm acute rejection [35].
  • Management: Initiate high-dose intravenous corticosteroids immediately [35].

Case 3: Decision Under Pressure (HDN)

Scenario: An Rh-negative pregnant woman with a history of one previous Rh-positive child shows high titers of anti-D antibodies at 32 weeks [23, 36].

  • Reasoning Under Uncertainty: High titers suggest potential fetal damage (erythroblastosis fetalis) [23, 37].
  • Decision: Monitor via indirect Coombs test. If fetal distress is high, consider intrauterine transfusion or premature delivery followed by exchange transfusion [38].

5. University-Level Exam Questions

  1. Define autoimmunity and list four diagnostic features of autoimmune diseases.
    Answer: Autoimmunity is structural or functional damage produced by immunologically competent cells or antibodies against self [1]. Features include: elevated immunoglobulins, demonstrable autoantibodies, lymphoid accumulation at lesion sites, and benefit from immunosuppressive therapy [32, 39].
  2. Describe the "First Set Response" in allograft rejection.
    Answer: Initially, the graft is vascularized. By day 4, inflammation and lymphocytic invasion occur. By day 10, blood vessels are occluded by thrombi, leading to ischemic necrosis and sloughing [12].
  3. Explain the "Bombay Phenotype."
    Answer: A rare condition where A, B, and H antigens are all absent from red cells. These individuals have anti-A, anti-B, and anti-H antibodies, making their blood incompatible with all except other Bombay phenotype donors [40].
  4. Compare and contrast "Warm" vs "Cold" autoantibodies in hemolytic anemia.
    Answer: Cold antibodies (IgM) agglutinate at 4°C and are seen in conditions like atypical pneumonia [7]. Warm antibodies (IgG) do not agglutinate, react at 37°C, and are often drug-induced (e.g., alpha-methyldopa) [41].

6. PLAB & USMLE Pattern Questions

  1. A 30-year-old female presents with muscle fatigue that worsens throughout the day and improves with rest. Antibodies are likely directed against which of the following?
    A) Thyroglobulin
    B) Acetylcholine receptor
    C) Parietal cells
    D) Desmoglein
    Correct: B. Myasthenia Gravis is characterized by antibodies against the acetylcholine receptor at the myoneural junction [42].
  2. Which of the following describes "Molecular Mimicry"?
    A) Hidden antigens leaking from the eye.
    B) Breakdown of suppressor T-cell function.
    C) Structural similarity between Streptococcal M protein and heart muscle.
    D) Polyclonal B cell activation by EBV.
    Correct: C. Molecular mimicry involves identical peptide sequences between microbial and self-antigens [6].
  3. A patient with a history of multiple blood transfusions rejects a kidney transplant within hours. This is an example of:
    A) Accelerated rejection
    B) First set response
    C) Hyperacute rejection
    D) GVH reaction
    Correct: C. Pre-existing antibodies from prior transfusions lead to hyperacute rejection [14].
  4. Which test is the most sensitive for detecting IgG anti-D antibodies in a pregnant woman?
    A) Saline slide test
    B) Indirect Coombs test
    C) Enzyme-treated RBC test
    D) Rose-Waaler test
    Correct: B. The indirect Coombs test is the most sensitive method for identifying Rh incompatibility [36, 43].

7. Bias Recognition & Problem-Solving

Cognitive Bias: Availability Bias. Doctors may prematurely diagnose SLE in any young woman with joint pain because it is a "classic" textbook case [20, 32].
Approach to avoid: Use structured criteria. Ensure positive ANA results are followed by specific tests (anti-dsDNA) and clinical correlation, as ANA can be positive in viral infections or chronic inflammation [21].

8. Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Autoimmunity is "injury to self" due to failed tolerance [1, 2].
  • Molecular Mimicry explains why certain infections (e.g., Strep) lead to autoimmune damage (e.g., Rheumatic heart disease) [6, 44].
  • Allografts are rejected primarily by T-cells (First set) or pre-existing antibodies (Hyperacute) [12, 14, 45].
  • GVHD requires a graft with T-cells and an immunodeficient host [22].
  • Rh-HDN involves maternal IgG crossing the placenta; it is preventable and treatable [23, 24, 38].
  • O Negative is the "universal donor" in emergencies because it lacks A, B, and Rh antigens [46, 47].
