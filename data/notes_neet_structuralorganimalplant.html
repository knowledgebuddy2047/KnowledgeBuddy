NEET Notes – Unit 2: Structural Organisation in Animals and Plants

Covers NCERT Class 11 & 12 – Morphology & Anatomy of Flowering Plants, Structural Organisation in Animals (Earthworm, Cockroach, Frog), and key NEET‑oriented concepts.

1. Morphology of Flowering Plants (Class 11)

Roots

  • Tap root system – main root with lateral branches (e.g., mustard, dicots).
  • Fibrous root system – cluster of similar‑sized roots from stem base (e.g., wheat, grasses).
  • Modifications:
    • Storage: tuberous (turnip), fasciculated (sweet potato), napiform (radish).
    • Aerial: prop roots (banyan), stilt roots (maize), pneumatophores (Rhizophora).

Stems

  • Characteristics: nodes, internodes, buds, leaves, branches.
  • Underground modifications for storage:
    • Rhizome (ginger), corm (Colocasia), tuber (potato), bulb (onion).
  • Aerial modifications:
    • Tendrils (climbing), thorns (protection), phylloclade (Opuntia), cladode (Asparagus).

Leaves

  • Parts: lamina, petiole, stipules.
  • Arrangements: alternate, opposite, whorled.
  • Modifications:
    • Tendrils (pea), spines (cactus, Argemone), pitcher (Nepenthes).

Inflorescence, Flower, Fruit, Seed

  • Inflorescence:
    • Racemose – main axis grows indefinitely; flowers acropetal.
    • Cymose – main axis ends in a flower; flowers basipetal.
  • Flower parts (4 whorls):
    • Calyx (sepals), Corolla (petals), Androecium (stamens), Gynoecium (carpels).
    • Symmetry: actinomorphic (radial), zygomorphic (bilateral).
    • Ovary position: hypogynous, perigynous, epigynous.
  • Fruit types:
    • Simple (mango), aggregate (custard apple), multiple (pineapple).
  • Seed – seed coat, cotyledons, embryo (radicle, plumule, hypocotyl, epicotyl).

Important Plant Families (NEET)

  • Fabaceae – e.g., pea, gram; zygomorphic, papilionaceous flower, fruit pod.
  • Solanaceae – e.g., brinjal, potato, tobacco; pentamerous, bicarpellary, superior ovary.
  • Liliaceae – e.g., lily, onion; trimerous, tricarpellary, superior ovary.

2. Anatomy of Flowering Plants (Class 11)

Tissues

  • Meristematic – apical, intercalary, lateral; actively dividing cells.
  • Permanent:
    • Simple – parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma.
    • Complex – xylem, phloem.

Tissue Systems

  • Dermal – epidermis, stomata, cuticle, root hairs, trichomes.
  • Ground – cortex, pericycle, pith.
  • Vascular – xylem and phloem in vascular bundles.

Root Anatomy

  • Dicot root:
    • Radial, exarch vascular bundles; xylem and phloem alternate.
    • Pericycle gives rise to lateral roots and vascular cambium.

Stem Anatomy

  • Dicot stem:
    • Conjoint, collateral, open vascular bundles in a ring; cambium present → secondary growth.
  • Monocot stem:
    • Scattered conjoint, closed vascular bundles; no cambium → no secondary growth.

Leaf Anatomy

  • Dorsiventral leaf:
    • Upper epidermis → palisade mesophyll → spongy mesophyll → lower epidermis with stomata.
    • Vascular bundles (midrib + veins) in mesophyll.

Secondary Growth (Dicot)

  • Vascular cambium → secondary xylem (wood) and secondary phloem.
  • Cork cambium (phellogen) → cork (outer) and secondary cortex (inner).
  • Annual rings in secondary xylem reflect seasonal growth.

3. Structural Organisation in Animals – Animal Tissues

Four Main Tissue Types

  • Epithelial tissue – lining and covering (e.g., squamous, cuboidal, columnar, ciliated, glandular).
  • Connective tissue – loose, dense, adipose, cartilage, bone, blood.
  • Muscular tissue – smooth, cardiac, skeletal.
  • Nervous tissue – neurons + neuroglia.

Cartilage matrix = chondrin; bone matrix = mineralized (calcium phosphate). Blood is a fluid connective tissue (RBCs, WBCs, platelets, plasma).

4. Earthworm (Pheretima posthuma)

  • Metameric segmentation; clitellum present in segments 14–16.
  • Body wall: cuticle, epidermis, circular & longitudinal muscles, coelomic lining.
  • Coelom – fluid‑filled space with hydrostatic skeleton.
  • Digestive tract: mouth → pharynx → esophagus → gizzard → stomach → intestine with typhlosole (increase surface).
  • Excretion: nephridia (pharyngeal, septal, integumentary, intestinal).
  • Reproduction: monoecious, cross‑fertilization, cocoon formed in clitellum.
  • Nervous system: circum‑pharyngeal nerve ring + ventral nerve cord.

5. Cockroach (Periplaneta americana)

  • Chitinous exoskeleton; body: head, thorax (3 segments), abdomen.
  • Head: fused from 6 segments; antennae, compound eyes, mandibles, maxillae.
  • Thorax: 3 segments; each with one pair of legs; 2nd & 3rd with one pair of wings each.
  • Digestive system:
    • Foregut – crop (storage), gizzard (grinding).
    • Midgut – gastric caecae; hepatopancreas.
    • Hindgut – ileum, colon, rectum.
  • Excretion: **Malpighian tubules**.
  • Circulation: **open type**; dorsal heart; colorless haemolymph.
  • Respiration: tracheae → tracheoles → spiracles.
  • Nervous system: brain, ventral nerve cord with ganglia.
  • Reproduction: unisexual, oviparous; egg case = **ootheca**.

6. Frog (Rana tigrina)

  • Amphibian; body: head + trunk.
  • Moist, glandular skin; cutaneous respiration.
  • Digestive system: mouth → pharynx → oesophagus → stomach → small intestine (duodenum + ileum) → large intestine → cloaca.
  • Respiration: larva (gills), adult (lungs + skin).
  • Circulation: **3‑chambered heart** (2 atria, 1 ventricle); incomplete double circulation.
  • Excretion: mesonephric kidneys + ureters + urinary bladder + cloaca.
  • Nervous system: brain (10 pairs cranial nerves), spinal cord, peripheral nerves.
  • Reproduction: dioecious, external fertilization, eggs in water; development via tadpole.

7. NEET‑Style MCQs – Unit 2 (with Answers & Explanations)

Q1. In a dicot root, the vascular bundles are:

  1. Conjoint and collateral
  2. Radial and exarch
  3. Conjoint and closed
  4. Conjoint and endarch

Answer: b) Radial and exarch

Explanation: In dicot roots, xylem and phloem are arranged alternately (radial bundles) and protoxylem is towards the periphery (exarch).

Q2. Which of the following is a modification of stem for storage?

  1. Rhizome
  2. Tendril
  3. Phyllode
  4. Haustorium

Answer: a) Rhizome

Explanation: Rhizome (e.g., ginger) is an underground horizontally growing stem that stores food.

Q3. The “floral formula” K(5) C5 A5 G(2) indicates a flower that is:

  1. Zygomorphic with superior ovary
  2. Actinomorphic with inferior ovary
  3. Actinomorphic with superior ovary
  4. Zygomorphic with superior ovary

Answer: c) Actinomorphic with superior ovary

Explanation: K(5), C5, A5, G(2) with no special sign for zygomorphy and ovary in G implies actinomorphic and superior ovary.

Q4. The tissue that gives rise to secondary xylem and secondary phloem is:

  1. Cork cambium
  2. Epidermis
  3. Vascular cambium
  4. Pericycle

Answer: c) Vascular cambium

Explanation: Vascular cambium is a lateral meristem that produces secondary xylem (towards centre) and secondary phloem (towards periphery).

Q5. In cockroach, excretion is mainly carried out by:

  1. Green glands
  2. Malpighian tubules
  3. Nephrons
  4. Contractile vacuole

Answer: b) Malpighian tubules

Explanation: Malpighian tubules are thin, branched excretory tubules attached to the junction of midgut and hindgut in insects.

Q6. In frog, the heart is:

  1. Two‑chambered
  2. Three‑chambered
  3. Four‑chambered
  4. Absent

Answer: b) Three‑chambered

Explanation: Adult frog has 2 atria and 1 ventricle, giving a 3‑chambered heart with incomplete double circulation.

Q7. In a dicot stem, vascular bundles are arranged:

  1. Scattered
  2. In a single ring
  3. Vertically
  4. Radially

Answer: b) In a single ring
