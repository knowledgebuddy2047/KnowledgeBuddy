Chapter 2: Is Matter Around Us Pure?

Pure Substance vs Mixture

  • Pure substance: Consists of only one type of particle, same chemical nature throughout.
  • Mixture: Contains two or more pure substances mixed physically, composition can vary.

Types of Mixtures

  • Homogeneous mixture: Uniform composition throughout (e.g., salt in water, air).
  • Heterogeneous mixture: Non-uniform composition, distinct parts visible (e.g., oil and water, soil).

Solutions

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances.

  • Solvent: Component present in larger amount, dissolves the solute.
  • Solute: Component present in smaller amount, gets dissolved.

Properties of Solutions

  • Homogeneous mixture.
  • Particles smaller than 1 nm, not visible to naked eye.
  • Do not scatter light (no Tyndall effect).
  • Stable, cannot be separated by filtration.

Suspensions

A suspension is a heterogeneous mixture where solute particles remain suspended in the medium.

  • Particles visible to naked eye.
  • Scatter light (Tyndall effect).
  • Unstable, particles settle on standing.
  • Can be separated by filtration.

Colloids

A colloid is a heterogeneous mixture with particles too small to be seen but large enough to scatter light.

  • Stable, particles do not settle.
  • Show Tyndall effect.
  • Cannot be separated by filtration, but can be separated by centrifugation.

Physical and Chemical Changes

  • Physical change: No change in chemical composition (e.g., melting of ice, boiling water).
  • Chemical change: New substance formed with different properties (e.g., rusting, burning).

Types of Pure Substances

Elements

  • Cannot be broken down into simpler substances.
  • Classified as metals, non-metals, metalloids.

Compounds

  • Formed by chemical combination of elements in fixed proportion.
  • Properties different from constituent elements.

Difference Between Mixtures and Compounds

Mixtures Compounds

Properties same as constituentsProperties different

Objective Type Questions (MCQs)

  1. Which of the following is a homogeneous mixture?
    a) Oil and water
    b) Air
    c) Soil
    d) Sand and salt

    Answer: b) Air

  2. Which of the following shows Tyndall effect?
    a) Salt solution
    b) Copper sulphate solution
    c) Milk
    d) Sugar solution

    Answer: c) Milk

  3. Which of the following is a pure substance?
    a) Milk
    b) Ice
    c) Wood
    d) Soil

    Answer: b) Ice

  4. Which separation technique is used to separate tea leaves from tea?
    a) Filtration
    b) Distillation
    c) Chromatography
    d) Evaporation

    Answer: a) Filtration

  5. Which of the following is a chemical change?
    a) Melting of butter
    b) Dissolving salt in water
    c) Rusting of iron
    d) Cutting of trees

    Answer: c) Rusting of iron

Subjective Questions

  1. Differentiate between homogeneous and heterogeneous mixtures with suitable examples.
  2. Explain the properties of solutions, suspensions, and colloids. Give one example of each.
  3. What is the Tyndall effect? How can it be used to distinguish between a solution and a colloid?
  4. Describe the difference between mixtures and compounds with examples.
  5. Classify the following as physical or chemical changes: burning of paper, boiling of water, rusting of iron, dissolving sugar in water.
  6. Explain why alloys are considered mixtures even though they look like pure substances.
