Chapter 1: Matter in Our Surroundings

Definition of Matter

Everything that occupies space and has mass is called matter. Examples include air, water, stones, plants, animals, and even a drop of water or a grain of sand.

  1. Which of the following are matter: Chair, air, love, smell, almonds, thought?
  2. Why do we say matter has both mass and volume?

Physical Nature of Matter

  • Matter is made up of particles.
  • Particles are extremely small and cannot be seen with the naked eye.

Characteristics of Particles of Matter

  • Particles have space between them.
  • Particles are in continuous motion.
  • Particles attract each other.

States of Matter

Matter exists in three states: Solid, Liquid, Gas.

Comparison of States of Matter

Property Solid Liquid Gas
Shape Definite Indefinite Indefinite
Volume Definite Definite Indefinite
Compressibility Negligible Low High
Kinetic Energy Low Moderate High

Change of State of Matter

  • Melting Point of Ice: 273 K (0°C).
  • Boiling Point of Water: 373 K (100°C).
  • Latent Heat: Heat required to change state without changing temperature.
  • Sublimation: Direct change from solid to gas.
  • Effect of Pressure: Increasing pressure can liquefy gases.

Evaporation

Evaporation is the process of liquid changing into vapour at a temperature below its boiling point.

Factors Affecting Evaporation

  • Increase in surface area.
  • Increase in temperature.
  • Decrease in humidity.
  • Increase in wind speed.

Evaporation Causes Cooling

  • Particles absorb energy from surroundings during evaporation, making surroundings cooler.
  • Examples: Acetone on palm, perspiration in summer, sprinkling water on hot surfaces.

Key Takeaways

  • Matter is made up of particles.
  • Exists in three states: solid, liquid, gas.
  • States are interconvertible by changing temperature or pressure.
  • Sublimation and deposition are direct changes between solid and gas.
  • Evaporation is a surface phenomenon and causes cooling.
