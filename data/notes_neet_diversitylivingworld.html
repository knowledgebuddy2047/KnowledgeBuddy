NEET Notes – Unit 1: Diversity in the Living World
(NCERT Class 11 Biology)

Based on NCERT Chapters: 1. The Living World, 2. Biological Classification, 3. Plant Kingdom, 4. Animal Kingdom.

Chapter 1 – The Living World

1. What is Living?

Living organisms share certain defining characteristics that distinguish them from non‑living things:

  • Growth – increase in mass and number of cells, not mere accumulation like in non‑living objects.
  • Reproduction – ability to produce new individuals (asexual or sexual).
  • Metabolism – sum of all biochemical reactions (anabolism + catabolism); occurs in all organisms.
  • Cellular organization – made of cells (unicellular or multicellular).
  • Consciousness – ability to sense and respond to environmental stimuli (even in plants, e.g., phototropism).

Viruses do not show independent metabolism and are not cellular but can replicate inside host cells. Hence, they are considered obligate intracellular parasites, not truly “alive”.

2. Diversity in the Living World

Biodiversity refers to the enormous variety of organisms present on Earth in terms of form, structure, habitat, and function.

  • Organisms occur from deep oceans to high mountains and hot deserts.
  • Over 1.5 million species are known; many more remain undiscovered.
  • More species are still undescribed than described (“ignoramus stage”).

3. Need for Classification

Classification is essential for:

  • Easy identification and study of organisms.
  • Understanding evolutionary relationships.
  • Organizing information for agriculture, forestry, medicine, and industry.

4. Taxonomy, Systematics, and Binomial Nomenclature

  • Taxonomy – science of identification, naming, and classification of organisms.
  • Systematics – broader than taxonomy; includes evolutionary relationships and biodiversity studies.
  • Binomial nomenclature (Linnaeus): each species has a two‑part scientific name.

Format: Genus species (e.g., Homo sapiens, Panthera tigris).
Rules: Genus starts with a capital letter, species with a small letter; both are italicized or underlined.

5. Taxonomic Hierarchy

Hierarchical levels from broadest to most specific:

  1. Kingdom
  2. Phylum (Division in plants)
  3. Class
  4. Order
  5. Family
  6. Genus
  7. Species

Each rank contains one or more lower ranks (e.g., many species form a genus; many genera form a family).

6. Concept of Species

  • Species is the basic unit of classification.
  • Members of a species can interbreed and produce fertile offspring.

7. Taxonomical Aids

Tools that help in identification and classification:

  • Herbarium – collection of dried, pressed, and labelled plant specimens.
  • Museum – preserved animal specimens (wet and dry), skeletons, etc.
  • Zoological parks – live wild animals for observation and study.
  • Botanical gardens – living collections of plants for research and education.
  • Keys – dichotomous keys used for identification (paired contrasting statements).

Chapter 2 – Biological Classification

1. Traditional Two‑Kingdom System

  • Proposed by Aristotle and later refined by Linnaeus.
  • Kingdoms: Plantae and Animalia.
  • Limitations: fungi, bacteria, protozoa, and others could not be placed correctly.

2. Five‑Kingdom Classification (Whittaker)

Kingdoms based on cell type, body organization, and mode of nutrition:

  1. Monera – prokaryotic, unicellular; e.g., bacteria, cyanobacteria.
  2. Protista – eukaryotic, mostly unicellular; e.g., amoeba, euglena.
  3. Fungi – eukaryotic, mainly saprophytic or parasitic; e.g., Rhizopus, mushrooms.
  4. Plantae – eukaryotic, multicellular, autotrophic; e.g., algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiosperms.
  5. Animalia – eukaryotic, multicellular, heterotrophic; e.g., sponges, insects, mammals.

3. Kingdom Monera

  • Prokaryotes (no membrane‑bound nucleus or organelles).
  • Includes bacteria, actinomycetes, and cyanobacteria.
  • Shapes: coccus, bacillus, vibrio, spirillum.
  • Bacteria show diverse modes: autotrophic and heterotrophic (saprophytic, parasitic, symbiotic).
  • Binary fission is the main mode of reproduction.

4. Kingdom Protista

  • Mostly unicellular eukaryotes.
  • Subgroups:
    • Chrysophytes – diatoms and golden algae; siliceous cell walls.
    • Dinoflagellates – photosynthetic marine protists; e.g., Gonyaulax.
    • Euglenoids – e.g., Euglena; flexible pellicle.
    • Protozoans – amoeboid, flagellated, ciliated, sporozoan.

5. Kingdom Fungi

  • Cell wall of **chitin**.
  • Body structure: **mycelium** made of hyphae.
  • Mode: saprophytic, parasitic, or symbiotic (lichens, mycorrhiza).
  • Reproduction: vegetative, asexual (spores), sexual (zygospores, ascospores, basidiospores).
  • Major groups:
    • Phycomycetes – e.g., Mucor.
    • Ascomycetes – e.g., Penicillium, Aspergillus.
    • Basidiomycetes – e.g., Agaricus (mushroom).
    • Deuteromycetes – imperfect fungi (only asexual stage known).

6. Viruses, Viroids, Lichens

  • Viruses – acellular, protein coat + nucleic acid (DNA or RNA); obligate parasites.
  • Viroids – small RNA molecules without protein coat; infect plants.
  • Lichens – symbiotic association of fungus + algae or cyanobacteria.

Chapter 3 – Plant Kingdom

1. Algae

  • Mostly aquatic, photosynthetic.
  • Classes:
    • Green algae (Chlorophyceae) – chlorophyll a, b; starch reserve; e.g., Chlorella, Ulothrix.
    • Brown algae (Phaeophyceae) – chlorophyll a, c; fucoxanthin; laminarin; e.g., Laminaria, Fucus.
    • Red algae (Rhodophyceae) – chlorophyll a, d; phycoerythrin; floridean starch; e.g., Polysiphonia.

2. Bryophytes – “Amphibians of the Plant Kingdom”

  • Mostly terrestrial but need water for fertilization.
  • Non‑vascular (no true xylem/phloem); dominant gametophyte generation.
  • Examples: liverworts (Marchantia), mosses (Funaria).

3. Pteridophytes – Vascular Cryptogams

  • Vascular tissue (xylem and phloem) present.
  • True roots, stems, leaves; sporophyte dominant.
  • Examples: ferns (Pteris, Nephrolepis), Selaginella, Lycopodium.

4. Gymnosperms – “Naked Seed” Plants

  • Seeds not enclosed in fruits.
  • Well‑developed xylem and phloem; mostly evergreen.
  • Examples: Pinus, Cycas, Cedrus.
  • Used for timber, resin, paper, etc.

5. Angiosperms – Flowering Plants

  • Seeds enclosed in fruits; highly evolved.
  • Two major groups:
    • Monocots – one cotyledon, parallel venation, fibrous roots.
    • Dicots – two cotyledons, reticulate venation, tap roots.
  • Reproduction: flowers, pollination, double fertilization.

Chapter 4 – Animal Kingdom

1. Basic Terms

  • Cellular organization: cellular, tissue, organ, organ‑system level.
  • Body symmetry: radial, bilateral, asymmetrical.
  • Coelom: body cavity lined by mesoderm – acoelomate, pseudocoelomate, coelomate.
  • Segmentation: body divided into repeated units (metamerism).

2. Phylum Porifera (Sponges)

  • Simplest multicellular animals; pore‑bearing.
  • Body with canal system and osculum.
  • Example: Sycon, Euspongia.

3. Phylum Coelenterata (Cnidaria)

  • Radial symmetry; diploblastic; cnidoblasts for defense.
  • Polyp and medusa forms (e.g., Hydra, Aurelia).

4. Phylum Ctenophora

  • Radial symmetry; bioluminescent; “comb plates” for locomotion.
  • Example: Pleurobrachia.

5. Phylum Platyhelminthes

  • Flatworms; dorsoventrally flattened; triploblastic; acoelomate.
  • Many parasitic (e.g., Taenia, Fasciola).

6. Phylum Aschelminthes (Nematoda)

  • Cylindrical, bilaterally symmetrical; pseudocoelomate.
